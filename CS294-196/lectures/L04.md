# L04 Compound AI Systems & DSPy

The lecture treats compound systems as the real unit of engineering. The move is not "ask the model harder." It is split the task, inspect the parts, and compile the parts into something portable.

## Summary
- Monolithic LLMs are great for demos but hard to control, debug, and improve. Compound AI systems break the work into modular pieces so the trace is visible and the architecture is adjustable. Source: `[0:08]` to `[2:42]` in `/Users/otonashi/thinking/pratchett-os/tmp/research-123/L04.transcript.txt`.
- Retrieval-augmented systems are the base case: retrieve relevant context, feed it to the model, and inspect what was retrieved when the answer is wrong. Source: `[3:01]` to `[4:46]`.
- Multi-hop retrieval and report generation push the same pattern further. The system decomposes the question, gathers evidence step by step, then synthesizes a longer artifact. Source: `[4:46]` to `[7:25]`.
- The lecture's pivot is DSPy: treat compound systems like Python programs with signatures, modules, optimizers, and adapters instead of giant prompt blobs. Source: `[13:42]` to `[19:53]`.
- The hard part is not the idea. It is the coupling: prompts mix signatures, inference strategy, preprocessing, objective, and model-specific hacks in one place. Source: `[9:02]` to `[13:38]`.
- The practical claim is blunt: if you can compile the higher-level design, you get portability across models and faster iteration on the system itself. Source: `[14:00]` to `[26:45]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Compound AI system**: a modular program where the LLM is one component, not the whole system. Source: `[2:38]` to `[3:11]`.
- **RAG**: retrieve from a corpus, pass the retrieved context to the model, and inspect the retrieval trace for transparency. Source: `[3:09]` to `[4:46]`.
- **Multi-hop RAG**: break a question into smaller retrieval steps and compose the answers. Source: `[5:06]` to `[5:36]`.
- **STORM-style report generation**: brainstorm, outline, generate subquestions, retrieve sources, and synthesize a long cited report. Source: `[6:10]` to `[7:05]`.
- **Inference-time scaling**: spend more compute at test time to explore more candidate paths. Source: `[7:29]` to `[8:37]`.
- **Signature**: the declarative description of inputs, outputs, and behavior for a module. Source: `[10:05]` to `[10:32]`.
- **Predictor**: a module that wraps the signature and adds a reasoning strategy. Source: `[26:03]` to `[26:45]`.
- **Adapter**: code that turns a signature into the right prompt format for a specific model. Source: `[26:03]` to `[26:45]`.
- **DSPy program**: normal Python control flow plus language-model modules that can be optimized. Source: `[16:02]` to `[19:53]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture is not arguing for better prompts. It is arguing that prompts are the wrong abstraction boundary because they entangle architecture with implementation details. Source: `[9:02]` to `[13:38]`.
- The compiler analogy matters. A good high-level program should survive a model swap the way a C program survives a CPU swap. Source: `[14:00]` to `[15:25]`.
- Retrieval is not just a knowledge trick. It is a control-flow trick because it makes the trace auditable. Source: `[3:42]` to `[4:16]`.
- The more interesting gain is portability. If the system is expressed as code, the design can move from one model to another without rewriting every prompt. Source: `[14:34]` to `[15:35]`.
- The optimization target is the whole pipeline, not an isolated module. That is why the lecture keeps returning to compiled behavior, not artisanal prompt tuning. Source: `[18:37]` to `[21:06]`.
