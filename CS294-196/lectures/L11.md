# L11 Measuring Agent Capabilities and Anthropic's RSP

Ben Mann's thesis is that safety starts with measurement. The Responsible Scaling Policy turns that into a release discipline, and the computer-use demo makes the threat model concrete the moment an agent can touch a desktop.

## Summary
- The lecture starts from elicitation and capability measurement: model behavior changes with prompting, so safety cannot be based on intuition alone. Source: `[00:01:15]` to `[00:05:04]` in `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN85DQWKFHXYEY6J85J11FWA/6y2AnWol7oo.txt`.
- Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy borrows the logic of biosafety levels and turns capability thresholds into concrete containment and deployment obligations. Source: `[00:11:45]` to `[00:23:43]`.
- The computer-use demo shows why agent safety is now operational, not abstract: a model can drive a desktop, control a browser, and get trapped by hidden instructions or prompt injection. Source: `[00:29:46]` to `[00:38:44]`.
- The layered defense story is explicit: sandboxing, instruction hierarchy, and scalable oversight are all needed, and none is enough on its own. Source: `[00:36:34]` to `[00:38:44]`.
- The close shifts from demos to governance: capability benchmarks, forecast-based release decisions, and the long-term benefit trust are all part of the safety stack. Source: `[00:41:15]` to `[00:44:21]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Measurement matters** because capability and safety evolve together. If you cannot measure capability, you cannot define a release threshold. Source: `[00:01:15]` to `[00:03:15]`.
- **Elicitation** is the prompt-dependent gap between latent capability and observed behavior. Source: `[00:02:29]` to `[00:03:15]`.
- **Responsible Scaling Policy** maps capability growth to required safety measures before deployment. Source: `[00:11:45]` to `[00:23:43]`.
- **ASL-1 through ASL-4** are the policy buckets the lecture uses to talk about escalating model risk. Source: `[00:14:51]` to `[00:18:53]`.
- **Computer use** is the concrete agent setting: the model can move the mouse, type, and inspect screenshots inside a loop. Source: `[00:33:52]` to `[00:40:54]`.
- **Prompt injection** is the attack class that makes computer use dangerous. The malicious text may be invisible to the human overseer but visible to the agent. Source: `[00:35:23]` to `[00:38:44]`.
- **Defense in depth** is the answer: sandbox, instruction hierarchy, and scalable oversight. Source: `[00:36:34]` to `[00:38:44]`.
- **SWE-bench Verified** and **OSWorld** are the capability benchmarks the lecture treats as useful measurement snapshots. Source: `[00:41:15]` to `[00:42:39]`.
- **Long-term benefit trust** is the governance mechanism Anthropic uses to align control with safety and public benefit. Source: `[00:43:40]` to `[00:44:21]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The RSP is not just a statement of intent. It is a precommitment device that links measured capability to release conditions. Source: `[00:12:39]` to `[00:23:43]`.
- The safety problem gets much sharper when the model can act on a real machine with credentials. Text generation alone is not the same risk surface. Source: `[00:35:23]` to `[00:37:11]`.
- Benchmarks are valuable even when they saturate quickly, because they still give a reproducible snapshot of what the world looks like now. Source: `[00:41:15]` to `[00:42:06]`.
- Live-internet evaluation sounds ideal but gets messy fast, so the lecture prefers controlled benchmarks and fake environments as the practical path. Source: `[00:42:39]` to `[00:43:21]`.
- Governance can be encoded in ownership and control structure, not only in model policy text. Source: `[00:43:40]` to `[00:44:21]`.
