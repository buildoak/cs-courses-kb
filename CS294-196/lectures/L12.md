# L12 Towards Building Safe & Trustworthy AI Agents and A Path for Science- and Evidence-based AI Policy

Dawn Song treats agent safety as an adversarial systems problem. Misuse, malfunction, and systemic risk all show up together, so the lecture moves from attacks and defenses to the policy question: what evidence should govern release, monitoring, and intervention?

## Summary
- The lecture opens by naming the risk classes plainly: misuse, malicious use, malfunction, and systemic risk. It also makes the safety/security split explicit. Source: `[00:00:15]` to `[00:03:08]` in `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN85DQWKFHXYEY6J85J11FWA/QAgR4uQ15rc.txt`.
- The privacy section shows how memorization, data extraction, and membership inference turn training data into a real attack surface. Source: `[00:06:11]` to `[00:18:41]`.
- The attack surface widens with adversarial examples, poisoned training data, and backdoors that can survive safety training. Source: `[00:19:18]` to `[00:33:50]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Safety vs security** is the opening distinction. Safety protects the external world from the system; security protects the system from external attackers. Source: `[00:03:00]` to `[00:03:08]`.
- **Memorization and extraction** are framed as privacy risks in the training data, not theoretical curiosities. Source: `[00:06:11]` to `[00:11:27]`.
- **Adversarial robustness** covers both input attacks and model behavior under attack. Source: `[00:18:56]` to `[00:23:57]`.
- **Prompt injection** is the agent-era attack pattern where malicious instructions ride inside the content the agent is reading. Source: `[00:39:09]` to `[00:41:56]`.
- **Poisoned data and backdoors** are the training-time attack path. Safety training does not automatically erase them. Source: `[00:29:15]` to `[00:33:50]`.
- **Reactive vs proactive defense** is the lecture's core defense distinction. Detection alone is too slow; defense by construction matters more. Source: `[00:50:44]` to `[01:01:42]`.
- **Science- and evidence-based policy** means policy should follow measured harms, transparency, monitoring, and demonstrated mitigation. Source: `[01:35:37]` to `[01:42:56]`.
- **Representation engineering** is one of the lecture's examples of recent work on transparency. Source: `[00:56:10]` to `[00:59:17]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Agent safety is not just a model problem. Once the model has tools, the attacker can target the whole loop, including the environment around the model. Source: `[00:37:29]` to `[00:43:00]`.
- Defense costs are asymmetric. Attackers need one exploit; defenders need enough coverage, monitoring, and deployment discipline to stop many. Source: `[01:20:18]` to `[01:29:54]`.
- The lecture treats policy as a mapping problem: observed harms should map to candidate responses. That is a much more operational framing than general fear. Source: `[01:41:51]` to `[01:42:56]`.
- Transparency is not a nice-to-have. It is the evidence base for both evaluation and policy. Source: `[01:40:05]` to `[01:41:12]`.
- The attack story is recursive: privacy leakage, prompt injection, and adversarial examples all collapse into the same control problem once agents are deployed broadly. Source: `[00:06:11]` to `[00:43:00]`.
