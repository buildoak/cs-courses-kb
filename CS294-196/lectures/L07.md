# L07 AI Agents for Enterprise Workflows

This lecture treats enterprise agents as workflow systems, not chatbots. The hard part is the loop around the model: user, action space, benchmark, safety, and deployment context.

## Summary
- Nicolas Chapados frames the lecture around enterprise work: agents should help people get actual work done inside IT, HR, support, and similar workflow-heavy settings. Source: `[0:24]` to `[1:03]` in `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN85D7Z10NHHFK7XC3V3H2BS/L07-transcript-ts.txt`.
- The web-agent demo shows the shape of the problem: a human asks for help, the agent acts through a Chrome-like browser, replans, and asks follow-up questions when needed. Source: `[14:51]` to `[16:44]`.
- The lecture splits the stack into API agents and web agents, then argues that we need a framework for developing, optimizing, and debugging them. TapeAgents is the explicit answer to that need. Source: `[16:57]` to `[27:25]`.
- The benchmark section is the real hinge: toy web tasks are not enough, so the lecture moves to trace-based and live benchmarks, then to WorkArena, BrowserGym, AgentLab, and human evaluation. Source: `[37:57]` to `[54:48]`.
- The last third turns to deployment risk and labor impact: prompt injection, multimodal attacks, O*NET-based task analysis, and the difference between centaur and cyborg work patterns. Source: `[55:35]` to `[1:10:52]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Enterprise workflow** is the unit of work here: tickets, permissions, scheduling, support, onboarding, and other repeatable business tasks. Source: `[1:18]` to `[1:43]`.
- **API agent** emits structured actions and works over backend systems instead of a browser. Source: `[16:57]` to `[18:28]`.
- **Web agent** acts through a browser and needs navigation, visual grounding, and replanning. Source: `[14:51]` to `[16:44]`, `[18:11]` to `[19:13]`.
- **Tape / trace** is the internal record of an agent run. The lecture uses it as the basis for development, debugging, and distillation. Source: `[19:13]` to `[27:25]`.
- **WorkArena** is the concrete benchmark for real workplace tasks on ServiceNow-backed workflows. Source: `[41:47]` to `[49:17]`.
- **BrowserGym** standardizes observation spaces, action spaces, and human evaluation across web-agent benchmarks. Source: `[50:26]` to `[52:07]`.
- **AgentLab** is the toolkit for building, inspecting, and reproducing web-agent experiments. Source: `[52:10]` to `[54:53]`.
- **Prompt injection** is not a corner case. Browser content can actively hijack an agent if the system is not robust. Source: `[58:28]` to `[59:53]`.
- **Centaur vs cyborg** is the workforce framing: one pattern outsources chunks of work, the other interleaves human and machine more tightly. Source: `[1:08:31]` to `[1:09:19]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The benchmark is part of the product. If the evaluation does not look like the workflow, the agent is being trained for the wrong job. Source: `[37:57]` to `[44:04]`.
- Local-server benchmarks are cleaner, but remote-hosted benchmarks are closer to the real web. The lecture treats that tradeoff as unavoidable, not academic. Source: `[40:22]` to `[42:04]`.
- Standardized action and observation spaces matter because web-agent research otherwise fragments into incompatible demos. Source: `[50:26]` to `[51:34]`.
- The safety problem is architectural, not a patch. A browser agent is exposed to hostile instructions from the page, the DOM, and the rendered text. Source: `[58:28]` to `[59:44]`.
- The right impact question is not "can the model do one task?" It is "which occupations and sub-tasks change, and how fast?" Source: `[1:04:01]` to `[1:07:53]`.
