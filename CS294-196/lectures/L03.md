# L03 AutoGen, Multimodal RAG, and Agentic Production

This lecture shifts from agent theory to agent plumbing. The message is blunt: if you want useful agentic AI, you need a framework, a data pipeline, and a production path.

## Summary
- Chi Wang argues that future AI applications are agentic: software that can plan, call tools, recover from errors, and build whole artifacts with minimal supervision. Source: `[0:29]` to `[6:24]` in `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN853HZCNQYE4QJJ70Q992ZE/research-122-L03-transcript.txt`.
- AutoGen's basic abstraction is conversation. Define agents, then make them talk; nested chats let one visible agent hide a deeper conversation tree. Source: `[19:21]` to `[24:09]`.
- The design problem is a pile of tradeoffs: flexibility vs control, static vs dynamic workflows, centralized vs decentralized orchestration, and automation vs intervention. Source: `[17:21]` to `[19:53]`.
- Jerry Liu's section adds the data side: multimodal RAG needs parsing, chunking, indexing, and retrieval before any agent can reason over complex documents. Source: `[44:16]` to `[50:21]`.
- Agentic RAG sits on top of retrieval and adds reasoning layers like ReAct and LLMCompiler, but the lecture keeps favoring constrained flows when reliability matters. Source: `[52:45]` to `[58:23]`.
- The final target is production: microservices, human-in-the-loop, report generation, customer support, and a multimodal knowledge assistant over your data. Source: `[59:43]` to `[1:04:46]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Agentic AI** means AI applications built around multi-step workflows, not single-turn chat completion. Source: `[0:29]` to `[6:24]`.
- **AutoGen** is a programming framework for agentic AI built around multiagent conversation. Source: `[19:56]` to `[23:07]`.
- **Conversable agent** is the generic AutoGen abstraction: the backend can be an LLM, a tool, or a human. Source: `[22:48]` to `[23:07]`.
- **Conversation program** is the workflow that tells agents who talks to whom and how. Source: `[22:48]` to `[23:42]`.
- **Nested chat** hides a sub-workflow inside an agent so the outer caller sees one interface. Source: `[23:11]` to `[25:10]`.
- **Group chat** is the dynamic pattern for multiple agents with a manager. Source: `[26:40]` to `[26:57]`.
- **Reflection** shows up as a writer/critic loop and is one of the simplest useful multi-agent patterns. Source: `[23:54]` to `[24:29]`.
- **Multimodal RAG** parses complex documents, extracts structure, indexes summaries and image-linked nodes, and retrieves both text and images. Source: `[44:16]` to `[50:57]`.
- **Agentic RAG** adds a reasoning layer above retrieval so the agent can decompose the question before calling tools. Source: `[52:45]` to `[56:10]`.
- **Constrained workflows** are often more reliable than unconstrained ones, even if they are less expressive. Source: `[56:10]` to `[58:23]`.
- **Human-in-the-loop as a service** is a first-class production pattern, not an exception path. Source: `[1:03:42]` to `[1:04:35]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Conversation is the universal interface here because it can wrap humans, models, tools, and even other agents without changing the outer contract. Source: `[19:21]` to `[23:42]`.
- The data pipeline is part of the agent. If parsing is broken, the agent inherits the hallucination. Source: `[44:50]` to `[47:12]`.
- More flexibility is not always better. Reliability pushes real systems toward explicit routers, narrower tool sets, and constrained control flow. Source: `[56:10]` to `[58:23]`.
- The lecture treats production as a system property: model, workflow, queue, and human override all matter together. Source: `[1:02:05]` to `[1:04:46]`.
- Multi-agent systems are easier to extend when the behavior is modular, because a human or a tool can replace any single role. Source: `[12:06]` to `[15:00]`.
