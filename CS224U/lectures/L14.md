# L14 The Future of NLU: Metrics, Cost, and Scale

This lecture starts with IR metrics, then turns into the future of language-model work: multi-objective evaluation, cheap baselines, fast retrievers, and the hardware and optimizer tricks needed to train and fine-tune large models. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN83TXKDJPHK87ERMGKN5WFG/transcripts/L50.transcript.txt`, `[0:05]` to `[1:20:34]`; playlist video: <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JVKtPZsiv4k>.

## Summary
- The lecture opens by revisiting IR metrics like success, reciprocal rank, precision, recall, F1@K, and MRR, then immediately warns that each metric hides a different cost model. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN83TXKDJPHK87ERMGKN5WFG/transcripts/L50.transcript.txt`, `[0:42]` to `[13:26]`.
- It then widens the evaluation frame to latency, index size, RAM, and Pareto frontiers, which makes deployment cost part of the score. Source: transcript `[13:52]` to `[16:48]`.
- The lecture argues that evaluation should reflect what users actually pay, not just what a benchmark rewards. Source: transcript `[15:34]` to `[16:48]`.
- From there it pivots into the mechanics of building large language models: stability, learning-rate warmup, mixed precision, tensor cores, and ZeRO redundancy. Source: transcript `[53:43]` to `[1:20:34]`.
- The closing message is blunt: future progress in NLU depends on better metrics, better tasks, and better infrastructure, not just new architectures. Source: transcript `[20:37]` to `[21:45]` and `[1:18:46]` to `[1:20:34]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Success and reciprocal rank.** Good for coarse retrieval judgments, especially when there is only one relevant item. Source: transcript `[0:42]` to `[2:22]`.
- **Precision and recall.** Better when you need to count multiple relevant items. Source: transcript `[2:41]` to `[5:33]`.
- **F1@K and MRR.** Useful, but still only one slice of the retrieval story. Source: transcript `[13:04]` to `[13:38]`.
- **Cost-aware evaluation.** Latency, index size, and RAM are first-class evaluation dimensions. Source: transcript `[13:52]` to `[16:48]`.
- **Pareto frontier.** The lecture uses this to show that there is no single winner when different systems trade accuracy for cost differently. Source: transcript `[16:18]` to `[16:48]`.
- **Topic-stratified evaluation.** A more nuanced way to see where models work and where they fail. Source: transcript `[43:11]` to `[45:06]`.
- **Learning-rate warmup and stability.** Modern large-model training still needs careful scheduling and conservative optimization. Source: transcript `[53:43]` to `[1:00:36]`.
- **Mixed precision.** Training and part of backprop in 16-bit form saves memory and enables tensor-core acceleration. Source: transcript `[1:15:44]` to `[1:17:43]`.
- **Tensor cores.** The hardware reason mixed precision matters. Source: transcript `[1:16:40]` to `[1:17:24]`.
- **ZeRO redundancy.** Partitioning gradients and optimizer state across devices lets much larger models fit. Source: transcript `[1:18:46]` to `[1:20:18]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- A retrieval system can win on score and still be a worse system if it is too slow or too large to ship. Source: transcript `[14:24]` to `[16:48]`.
- The lecture treats evaluation as multi-objective optimization. Accuracy is one axis; user time, memory, and compute budget are the others. Source: transcript `[13:52]` to `[16:48]`.
- The future of NLU is not only about better models. It is also about better measurement, better benchmarks, and better engineering discipline. Source: transcript `[20:37]` to `[21:45]`.
- Large-model training is still fragile enough that data order and "bad batches" can destabilize a run. Source: transcript `[53:43]` to `[1:00:36]`.
- Inference: the lecture's version of responsible AI is infrastructural. A system that is too expensive, too unstable, or too hard to measure is not responsibly deployable, even if its raw metric looks good. Source: transcript `[15:34]` to `[16:48]`, `[53:43]` to `[1:20:34]`.
