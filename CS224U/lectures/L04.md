## Summary
- The lecture moves from sparse feature vectors and count-based methods to learned static vectors, then to contextual representations.
- The linguistic argument is simple: words like `break`, `flat`, `throw`, `crane`, and `any` change sense with context, so a single fixed vector per word is too blunt.
- The historical path to contextual models runs through language-model pretraining: Dai and Le, CoVe, ELMo, GPT, BERT, then GPT-3.
- The architecture story is about reducing hand-built bias: addition, tree structure, and fixed left-to-right processing all give way to learned sequence models and attention.
- Subword modeling and positional encoding show up as the practical scaffolding that makes contextual models workable.

## Key Concepts
- **Contextual meaning:** the same surface form can realize multiple senses depending on the surrounding sentence or discourse.
- **Static vector limits:** one vector per word cannot capture the malleability shown by `break`, `flat`, or `crane`.
- **Distributional pretraining:** unlabeled corpora plus language-model objectives became the bridge from static representations to contextual ones.
- **Fine tuning:** pretrained representations matter because downstream tasks can reuse them instead of relearning everything from scratch.
- **Subword modeling:** character CNNs in ELMo and WordPiece in BERT reduce out-of-vocabulary failures.
- **Positional encoding:** sequence order has to be injected somehow, because transformers do not preserve it on their own.
- **Massive scale:** GPT-3 marks the jump where scale itself changes what the model can do in context.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture treats context sensitivity as a linguistic fact first, not a benchmark trick.
- The important break is not just "bigger models." It is the shift from hand-built feature engineering to representation learning from unlabeled text.
- ELMo matters because open vocabulary is the real bottleneck. A huge word list still fails on real text.
- Positional encoding is already a compromise. It solves order, but it can also hard-code position in a way that hurts generalization.
- The transformer era looks powerful because it removes a lot of structural commitment and lets data fill in the details.

## Connections
- This lecture is the conceptual bridge from classical distributional semantics to transformer-era contextual embeddings.
- It sets up L05, which turns the historical story into the concrete transformer block.
