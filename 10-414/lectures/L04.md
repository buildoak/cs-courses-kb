# L04 Automatic Differentiation

Lecture 4 turns gradient computation into a graph problem: numerical checking, manual rules, and then reverse mode AD as the practical answer.

## Summary
- The lecture starts by putting automatic differentiation back into the ML loop: hypothesis class, loss, and optimization all collapse onto the problem of computing gradients of `loss(theta)`. Source: transcript `[0:50]` to `[4:25]`.
- Numerical differentiation is the easiest way to define a gradient, and the centered-difference form is more accurate than the forward-difference form, but both are too slow and too noisy for real training. Source: transcript `[5:17]` to `[13:48]`.
- Gradient checking is the real use case for numerical differentiation. It is the debugging oracle, not the training algorithm. Source: transcript `[11:13]` to `[13:48]`.
- Manual symbolic differentiation on a toy product exposes the hidden cost: you recompute the same subexpressions over and over if you do it naively. Source: transcript `[14:14]` to `[18:23]`.
- Reverse mode AD turns a function into a computational graph, walks it backward with adjoints, and then extends the graph so gradient-of-gradient falls out naturally. Source: transcript `[18:28]` to `[1:03:29]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Numerical differentiation.** The finite-difference definition of a derivative, used here as a correctness check. Source: transcript `[5:27]` to `[10:05]`.
- **Gradient checking.** Compare autodiff output against numerical estimates along random directions. Source: transcript `[11:13]` to `[13:36]`.
- **Symbolic differentiation.** Hand-derived gradients on a toy function show where duplication appears. Source: transcript `[14:14]` to `[18:23]`.
- **Computational graph.** A DAG of intermediate values and operations, evaluated in topological order. Source: transcript `[18:28]` to `[22:43]`.
- **Forward mode AD.** Propagate derivatives from inputs forward through the graph. Source: transcript `[22:48]` to `[29:30]`.
- **Reverse mode AD.** Propagate adjoints backward from outputs to inputs. Source: transcript `[29:38]` to `[39:40]`.
- **Adjoint / partial adjoint.** The reverse-mode quantity attached to each node and edge. Source: transcript `[38:40]` to `[39:40]`.
- **Tensor generalization.** Scalar AD rules extend to matrix and tensor operations. Source: transcript `[57:40]` to `[1:00:11]`.
- **Differentiable programming.** The same idea can apply to richer data structures, not just tensors. Source: transcript `[1:00:11]` to `[1:02:12]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Numerical differentiation is more valuable as a test harness than as an algorithm. The lecture explicitly pushes it into the gradient-checking bucket. Source: transcript `[11:13]` to `[13:58]`.
- Reverse mode wins because it computes all input gradients in one backward pass. That is the actual asymmetry that matters. Source: transcript `[35:01]` to `[39:40]`.
- Extending the graph for the backward pass is the trick that makes higher-order derivatives cheap enough to be practical. Source: transcript `[50:15]` to `[56:15]`.
- The graph-extending formulation is also easier to optimize because the gradient computation itself remains a graph. Source: transcript `[56:26]` to `[57:20]`.
- Theano shows up as the historical bridge between classic backprop and modern graph-based reverse mode AD; TensorFlow and PyTorch then take that approach over. Source: transcript `[52:44]` to `[53:45]`.
