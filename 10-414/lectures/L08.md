# L08 Normalization, Dropout, + Implementation

Lecture 8 is the "stop trusting raw initialization" lecture. It is about keeping training stable, then being honest about the hacks.

## Summary
- A ReLU network wants variance-preserving initialization. The lecture shows why `2/n` is the canonical choice and why bad variance makes activations shrink or blow up across 50 layers. Source: transcript `[1:07]` to `[12:02]`.
- Layer norm fixes each example independently. Batch norm normalizes across the minibatch, which preserves feature scale but introduces batch dependence and train/test mismatch. Source: transcript `[13:34]` to `[33:31]`.
- Batch norm uses running means and variances at test time, not the current batch statistics. That detail is the source of a lot of implementation bugs. Source: transcript `[29:20]` to `[33:26]`.
- L2 regularization can be written either as a loss term or as weight decay inside the optimizer. The lecture prefers thinking of it as an optimizer update. Source: transcript `[47:01]` to `[55:38]`.
- Dropout is stochastic regularization on activations. It zeros features during training, rescales survivors by `1/(1-p)`, and disappears at test time. Source: transcript `[55:43]` to `[1:03:54]`.
- The closing case study is BatchNorm itself: the lecture walks through competing explanations and a distribution-shift use case, then refuses to pretend the field is settled. Source: transcript `[1:06:47]` to `[1:15:31]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Activation norm.** What matters is whether norms stay stable with depth, not just whether the first layer looks okay.
- **Gradient norm.** Initialization affects gradients too, and large gradients are where training falls apart.
- **Layer norm.** Normalize each example's activations to zero mean and unit variance.
- **Batch norm.** Normalize features across the batch, then use running statistics at inference.
- **Running averages.** Batch norm keeps exponential moving averages of mean and variance for test time.
- **Weight decay.** L2 regularization can be folded into the optimizer update.
- **Dropout.** Mask activations stochastically during training and rescale the survivors.
- **Distribution shift.** BatchNorm gets a second life as a robustness baseline when the test distribution changes.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture's main target is not "initialization theory"; it is the claim that stable training is a systems problem, not just a math problem.
- Layer norm is a clean fix, but for plain MLPs it can make optimization worse because it deletes relative-scale information between examples.
- Batch norm is weird because the output of one example depends on the other examples in the batch.
- The test-time running-statistics hack is not a footnote. It is part of what makes BatchNorm usable in real systems.
- Dropout is best thought of as stochastic approximation over subnetworks, not as magic robustness to missing features.
- The lecture is blunt about uncertainty: several popular explanations for BatchNorm coexist, and none is fully settled.
- In practice, many very different recipes still get similar results. The space of "good enough" deep-learning pipelines is larger than the lore suggests.
