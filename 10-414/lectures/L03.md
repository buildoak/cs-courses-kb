# L03 Manual Neural Networks / Backprop

Lecture 3 keeps the same loss and optimizer, but swaps the hypothesis class for nonlinear networks and then walks the long way through backpropagation.

## Summary
- Linear classifiers fail on non-linear structure, so the lecture introduces feature maps and learned nonlinear features as the first escape hatch. Source: transcript part 1 `[2:37]` to `[10:16]`.
- Two-layer ReLU networks are universal approximators, but the lecture is careful to say that theorem is not the real reason depth matters. Source: transcript part 1 `[27:54]` to `[35:33]`.
- The practical case for depth is stronger efficiency and better empirical performance at fixed parameter count, especially for structured networks. Source: transcript part 1 `[49:03]` to `[52:57]`.
- Backprop is the same old training loop with a different gradient engine: forward pass, backward pass, cached activations, and chain rule. Source: transcript part 2 `[0:11]` to `[43:13]`.
- The modular takeaway is the vector-Jacobian product. A layer only needs to consume an incoming gradient and multiply by its local derivatives. Source: transcript part 2 `[44:24]` to `[46:58]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Feature mapping.** `phi(x)` turns raw inputs into a representation that is easier to classify linearly. Source: transcript part 1 `[5:33]` to `[8:24]`.
- **Learned features.** Neural networks are presented as automatic feature extractors rather than hand-built feature pipelines. Source: transcript part 1 `[9:45]` to `[17:00]`.
- **ReLU.** The proof sketch for universal approximation uses a one-hidden-layer ReLU network and piecewise linear interpolation. Source: transcript part 1 `[35:12]` to `[40:45]`.
- **Deep network.** In engineering terms, deep learning means machine learning with neural network hypothesis classes. Source: transcript part 1 `[20:27]` to `[22:37]`.
- **MLP / L-layer network.** The lecture defines feed-forward layers as repeated `Z_{i+1} = sigma_i(Z_i W_i)` updates. Source: transcript part 1 `[43:47]` to `[48:52]`.
- **Backward pass.** Backprop computes `G_i` terms in reverse order while reusing forward activations. Source: transcript part 2 `[38:07]` to `[43:13]`.
- **Caching.** The forward pass stores `Z_i` because the backward pass needs them. That is the memory cost of the algorithm. Source: transcript part 2 `[41:00]` to `[43:13]`.
- **Vector-Jacobian product.** This is the layer-local abstraction that makes autograd modular. Source: transcript part 2 `[44:55]` to `[46:58]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Universal approximation is not the selling point people think it is. Many other function families have it too, and that does not make them useful. Source: transcript part 1 `[31:08]` to `[32:07]`.
- The real depth argument is efficiency and empirical bias, not a magical claim that shallow nets are incapable in theory. Source: transcript part 1 `[49:03]` to `[52:57]`.
- The lecture deliberately makes the manual gradient derivation ugly so the value of autograd is obvious later. Source: transcript part 2 `[3:00]` to `[3:39]`, `[37:37]` to `[38:07]`.
- Backprop is basically chain rule plus caching. The "algorithm" is mostly an organizational trick. Source: transcript part 2 `[41:23]` to `[43:13]`.
- The memory tradeoff is real: the network becomes twice as expensive as a forward pass because it has to keep intermediate activations around. Source: transcript part 2 `[42:13]` to `[43:13]`.
