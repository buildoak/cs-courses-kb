# L10 Convolutional Networks

Lecture 10 makes the core CNN tradeoff explicit: keep spatial structure, keep weights shared, and you get a model that is both smaller and more faithful to image geometry than a flattened MLP.

## Summary
- Flattening an image into a vector blows up parameter count and discards the locality that images already have. The lecture uses a 256x256 RGB example to show why fully connected layers do not scale. Source: transcript `[1:48]` to `[4:32]`.
- Convolutions are built from two constraints: local interaction and weight sharing across spatial positions. That is the whole structural win. Source: transcript `[5:00]` to `[9:53]`.
- The lecture uses image processing to show the difference between learned and fixed filters, with Gaussian blur and edge detection as the classical baseline. Source: transcript `[14:06]` to `[18:10]`.
- Multi-channel convolutions are easiest to reason about as matrix-vector products at each spatial location, not as scalar sliding windows. Source: transcript `[18:56]` to `[25:29]`.
- Practical CNNs need padding, pooling or strided convolutions, grouped convolutions, and dilations to manage size, resolution, and receptive field. Source: transcript `[26:02]` to `[38:35]`.
- Backprop through convolution is still convolution, just with the flipped filter, and `im2col` is the implementation bridge to dense matmul. Source: transcript `[41:04]` to `[1:07:19]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Local receptive field.** Each output location only depends on a neighborhood of the input. Source: transcript `[5:57]` to `[7:08]`.
- **Weight sharing.** The same filter slides across the whole image instead of learning a separate weight set per position. Source: transcript `[6:49]` to `[9:08]`.
- **Channel tensors.** Inputs and hidden states are treated as 3D tensors: height, width, channel. Source: transcript `[18:56]` to `[20:06]`.
- **Matrix-vector view.** Each spatial location holds a vector in `R^c_in`, and each filter element is a `c_out x c_in` matrix. Source: transcript `[22:06]` to `[25:29]`.
- **Padding.** Zero padding keeps output size aligned with input size when kernel size is odd. Source: transcript `[26:02]` to `[28:40]`.
- **Downsampling.** Max pooling, average pooling, and strided convolutions all reduce spatial resolution. Source: transcript `[28:46]` to `[33:15]`.
- **Grouped and depthwise convolutions.** Restrict channel mixing to reduce parameter count and compute. Source: transcript `[33:35]` to `[35:33]`.
- **Dilation.** Spread the kernel to widen receptive field without changing the kernel size itself. Source: transcript `[35:51]` to `[38:35]`.
- **Adjoint view of backward pass.** The transpose of the convolution matrix is another convolution with a flipped kernel. Source: transcript `[54:46]` to `[59:11]`.
- **`im2col`.** Turning a convolution into a matrix-matrix product can be faster than a direct scalar implementation. Source: transcript `[1:01:03]` to `[1:06:49]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The important abstraction is not "sliding window math". It is "local vectors plus per-location matrix maps". That is the form that maps cleanly to implementation. Source: transcript `[22:06]` to `[25:29]`.
- The lecture is blunt about naming: ML uses "convolution" for what signal processing would call correlation. Source: transcript `[12:37]` to `[13:39]`.
- `im2col` duplicates input values, which looks wasteful, but it is the right tradeoff when the backend can crush dense matmul. Source: transcript `[1:03:50]` to `[1:06:49]`.
- The backward pass is not a special case. Once the forward op is written as a matrix, the transpose gives the gradient structure almost for free. Source: transcript `[44:11]` to `[59:11]`.
- Dilations are the lecture's warning that local filters can become too local. Bigger receptive field means more padding bookkeeping. Source: transcript `[35:51]` to `[38:35]`.

## Connections
- This lecture is the bridge from abstract CNNs to efficient implementation. The next hardware lectures make the same "reuse beats raw compute" argument with matrices. Source: transcript `[1:07:19]` to `[1:08:28]`.
- The matrix-multiplication lowering used here is the same systems move that later shows up in linear algebra libraries and GPU kernels. Source: transcript `[1:03:50]` to `[1:06:49]`.
- The lecture foreshadows later attention-style models by noting that dilations try to widen the receptive field, while later architectures often use different mechanisms entirely. Source: transcript `[36:00]` to `[36:13]`.
- Classical image processing and learned vision reuse the same operator family. The difference is fixed filters versus trained filters. Source: transcript `[14:06]` to `[18:10]`.
