# L22 Customize Pretrained Models

Lecture 22 is the "attach adaptation at the cheapest point" lecture. The course page has no public video link for this slot, so this note is based on the slide deck.

## Summary
- The lecture frames pretraining as a leverage problem: instead of training a domain-specific model from scratch, start with a pretrained backbone and customize it for the target domain or task. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 4-5.
- On the image side, CLIP embeddings, latent diffusion, and ControlNet show three different ways to steer generation without retraining the whole stack. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 6-12.
- On the language side, the customization surface includes weights, token embeddings, prompts/context, and logits. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 13-17.
- LoRA adds low-rank deltas to a frozen pretrained weight matrix, which makes the adaptation small enough to ship and reuse. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 15-16.
- Structured output is treated as a decoding constraint problem, and XGrammar is the systems answer to making grammar masks fast enough for modern GPUs. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 17-20.

## Key Concepts
- **Pretrained model leverage.** The lecture contrasts a per-domain training loop with a pretrained backbone that gets customized downstream. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 4-5.
- **CLIP embeddings.** Text and image embeddings become reusable features that can drive many downstream tasks. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 6-7.
- **Latent diffusion conditioning.** Text-to-image generation works by injecting an extra condition embedding into the diffusion process. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 6-9.
- **ControlNet.** A control branch can be initialized from the original model with zero-weight convolution so the base model stays intact. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 10-12.
- **LoRA.** Low-rank matrices `A` and `B` adapt a fixed pretrained `W`, which keeps the delta small and the base weight stable. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slide 15.
- **Multimodal embedding mapping.** LLaVA and MiniGPT-4 map visual features into LLM token space so image signals can ride the language model interface. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slide 16.
- **Logit postprocessing.** Repetition penalties and grammar masks shape what the model is allowed to emit after raw decoding. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slide 17.
- **Constraint decoding.** The lecture treats structured output as grammar enforcement, then introduces XGrammar as a fast mask engine for that job. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 18-20.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture treats customization as a placement problem: inject control at the lowest-cost layer that still changes behavior. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 4-17.
- The same adaptation problem can be solved at different layers of the stack: input embeddings, latent conditioning, low-rank weight updates, or logit masks. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 6-20.
- LoRA is operationally conservative. The base weights stay fixed, so the adapter is the part you move, test, and version. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slide 15.
- Structured output is not a garnish. If the application needs JSON or grammar-constrained text, the decoding layer becomes part of the product contract. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 17-20.
- XGrammar pushes most token decisions into precomputation and only checks the hard cases at runtime, which is the only way to keep up with faster GPUs. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 18-20.

## Connections
- To L20-L21: this lecture moves from generative backbones to practical ways of adapting them for new domains. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 6-20.
- To L23: customization only matters if the adapted model can be exported and deployed into a real backend. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 13-20 and lecture 23 transcript `[8:11]` to `[11:04]`.
- To multimodal systems: CLIP, LLaVA, and MiniGPT-4 all turn learned representations into product-facing interfaces. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 6, 16.
- To application code: grammar-constrained decoding is the bridge from free-form generation to API-shaped outputs. Source: lecture 22 slide deck, slides 17-20.
