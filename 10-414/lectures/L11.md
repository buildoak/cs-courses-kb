# L11 Hardware Acceleration for Linear Algebra

Lecture 11 makes the systems thesis concrete: the winning optimizations are mostly about memory movement, layout, and reuse, not changing `O(n^3)` into something magical.

## Summary
- The course stack matters: models sit on computational graphs, which sit on tensor libraries, which sit on hardware-specific memory and instruction behavior. Source: transcript `[3:03]` to `[4:21]`.
- Vectorization only works well when memory is aligned and contiguous enough to feed SIMD-style loads and stores. Source: transcript `[4:31]` to `[9:33]`.
- Strides make layout transforms zero-copy, but they also make access patterns harder for vectorization and sometimes require compaction first. Source: transcript `[12:10]` to `[17:34]`.
- The naive matrix multiply is still `O(n^3)`; the speedup comes from reusing data in registers and caches instead of reloading it from DRAM. Source: transcript `[19:01]` to `[30:00]`.
- Cache-line aware tiling layers L1 reuse on top of register tiling, then generalizes the same reuse logic to convolution. Source: transcript `[32:00]` to `[44:44]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Vectorization.** Use vector registers and aligned loads/stores to batch scalar work. Source: transcript `[4:31]` to `[7:13]`.
- **Alignment.** 128-bit alignment is part of the contract if you want wide vector loads. Source: transcript `[7:13]` to `[9:24]`.
- **Row-major and column-major layout.** Both are just special cases of a more general stride description. Source: transcript `[10:02]` to `[13:24]`.
- **Strides.** A stride vector can encode slicing, transpose, and broadcast without copying data. Source: transcript `[13:24]` to `[15:24]`.
- **Compactness.** Not every strided view is good for downstream kernels; some operations need a compact array first. Source: transcript `[16:00]` to `[17:34]`.
- **OpenMP-style parallelization.** Independent loop iterations can be split across CPU cores. Source: transcript `[17:48]` to `[18:34]`.
- **Memory hierarchy.** Registers, L1, L2, and DRAM have radically different latencies, so data motion dominates runtime. Source: transcript `[21:09]` to `[22:21]`.
- **Register tiling.** Keep a submatrix in registers to reuse values across many multiply-accumulate steps. Source: transcript `[24:38]` to `[31:29]`.
- **Cache tiling.** Keep larger tiles in L1, then apply register tiling inside each cached block. Source: transcript `[32:00]` to `[40:34]`.
- **Reuse patterns.** Tile the dimension that does not affect the current operand to get repeated reuse. Source: transcript `[42:38]` to `[44:44]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture is not saying matrix multiplication got faster because of better asymptotics. It got faster because the same numbers are reused from a faster level of memory. Source: transcript `[21:09]` to `[22:21]`, `[29:14]` to `[31:29]`.
- Strides are a double-edged tool. They let you express many transformations without copying, but they can silently wreck access regularity. Source: transcript `[13:24]` to `[17:34]`.
- Register tiling and cache tiling are the same idea at different capacities. The only difference is which memory level is being saturated. Source: transcript `[24:38]` to `[40:34]`.
- The lecture's most reusable trick is looking for the index that does not appear in one operand. That missing dimension is the one you can tile for reuse. Source: transcript `[42:38]` to `[44:44]`.
- Convolution is not an exception to the reuse story. It is the next place to look once matrix multiplication is understood. Source: transcript `[44:00]` to `[44:44]`.

## Connections
- L10 lowers convolution into matrix operations conceptually; this lecture explains why that lowering is not just mathematically clean but performance-relevant. Source: transcript `[42:40]` to `[44:44]`; L10 transcript `[1:03:50]` to `[1:06:49]`.
- L12 extends the same memory-reuse logic onto GPU memory hierarchies and cooperative fetches. Source: transcript `[39:35]` to `[41:31]` in L12.
- The stride discussion is the local prerequisite for later `as-strided`-style implementation tricks in the library. Source: transcript `[13:24]` to `[17:34]`.
- The course is converging on a single systems idea: choose data layout so the hardware sees contiguous reuse, not scattered scalar work. Source: transcript `[21:09]` to `[22:21]`, `[42:38]` to `[44:44]`.
