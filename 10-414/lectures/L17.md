# L17 Transformers and Autoregressive Models

Lecture 17 is the "why recurrence is not the only answer" lecture. It starts with time-series modeling and ends with the mask that makes Transformers causal.

## Summary
- The lecture contrasts two sequence-modeling styles: latent-state recurrence and direct prediction. RNNs compress history into `h_t`; direct prediction keeps the whole prefix available. Source: transcript `[0:00]` to `[10:06]`.
- Temporal CNNs are the first direct-prediction alternative the lecture uses to make the point concrete. Causal convolutions respect time order, but receptive field depth still bounds how far back the model can see. Source: transcript `[10:06]` to `[19:14]`.
- Self-attention is the bridge to Transformers. It mixes values across all timesteps in one shot, with no parameter growth from longer sequences. Source: transcript `[19:14]` to `[44:40]`.
- A bare Transformer block is not causal. The lecture fixes that with an upper-triangular mask and restores order with positional encodings. Source: transcript `[44:47]` to `[1:03:24]`.
- The ending claim is blunt: for a lot of time-series work, Transformers now beat both RNNs and temporal CNNs once you can afford the compute. Source: transcript `[1:04:52]` to `[1:10:13]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Latent-state modeling.** RNNs summarize the prefix into a hidden state that carries the past forward. Source: transcript `[1:06]` to `[3:23]`.
- **Direct prediction.** The model maps `X_1..X_t` directly to `Y_t` instead of hiding the prefix inside one state vector. Source: transcript `[3:23]` to `[9:52]`.
- **Causal convolution.** Temporal CNNs keep future inputs out of the current output by masking the convolution footprint. Source: transcript `[10:46]` to `[13:49]`.
- **Receptive field.** Depth, kernel size, and dilation determine how far back a temporal CNN can look. Source: transcript `[13:49]` to `[19:14]`.
- **Attention as weighted mixing.** Attention is the general idea; self-attention is the form where the sequence weights itself. Source: transcript `[20:49]` to `[27:44]`.
- **K/Q/V mechanics.** The lecture frames self-attention as a matrix operation: `softmax(K Q^T / sqrt(d)) V`. Source: transcript `[28:26]` to `[38:11]`.
- **Causal mask.** Upper-triangular `-inf` entries zero out future dependence after the softmax. Source: transcript `[55:20]` to `[59:24]`.
- **Positional encoding.** Sine/cosine features inject order so attention is not blind to permutation. Source: transcript `[59:29]` to `[1:03:24]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The real break from RNNs is not "better memory." It is the abandonment of a single latent bottleneck in favor of direct prefix mixing. Source: transcript `[3:31]` to `[9:52]`, `[19:14]` to `[25:55]`.
- Self-attention is attractive because it gives a full receptive field in one layer. A temporal CNN needs depth or bigger kernels to get the same reach. Source: transcript `[53:37]` to `[54:19]`.
- The lecture treats the attention matrix as a costed object: masking does not magically remove the `T x T` work, it just makes the result causal. Source: transcript `[57:04]` to `[59:24]`.
- Positional encoding is not decorative. Without it, attention is permutation equivariant and loses sequence order. Source: transcript `[59:29]` to `[1:03:24]`.
- The model family wins because it mixes long-range context without increasing parameter count as sequence length grows. Source: transcript `[54:02]` to `[54:19]`.
