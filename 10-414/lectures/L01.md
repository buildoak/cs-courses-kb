# L01 Introduction / Logistics

Lecture 1 is the course pitch: why deep learning systems matter, why frameworks changed the field, and how the homework stack turns theory into a build-from-scratch exercise.

## Summary
- The course is about internals: automatic differentiation, neural network architectures, optimization, and efficient execution on GPUs. The homework spine is Needle, a PyTorch-like library built from scratch. Source: transcript `[1:46]` to `[3:00]`.
- The lecture uses AlexNet, AlphaGo, StyleGAN, GPT-3, AlphaFold 2, and Stable Diffusion to show what deep learning systems can already do. Source: transcript `[3:52]` to `[10:39]`.
- Small teams still matter. DeOldify and timm are used as examples of one- and two-person efforts that changed practice. Source: transcript `[11:11]` to `[13:30]`.
- The adoption story is mostly a tooling story: Keras, TensorFlow, PyTorch, and easy Python autograd mattered more than papers alone. The contrast is stark: 44,000 lines of code in 2012 versus about 100 lines today. Source: transcript `[16:37]` to `[20:21]`.
- The course structure is explicit: lectures, coding homeworks, a group project, forum interaction, and a completion certificate for people who finish the work. Source: transcript `[37:15]` to `[46:32]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Needle.** The course implements a deep learning library from scratch to make the stack concrete. Source: transcript `[2:28]` to `[3:00]`.
- **Deep learning systems.** The stack is not just models. It includes autograd, optimization, and efficient hardware execution. Source: transcript `[1:46]` to `[2:23]`.
- **Automatic differentiation.** The lecture treats autograd as the core enabling technology behind the modern framework ecosystem. Source: transcript `[16:37]` to `[18:04]`.
- **Open-source frameworks.** PyTorch, TensorFlow, JAX, and Torch are presented as the infrastructure that made experimentation fast. Source: transcript `[17:41]` to `[18:04]`, `[20:26]` to `[20:32]`, `[21:15]` to `[22:17]`.
- **Systems background.** The course expects C++, Python, linear algebra, calculus, and prior ML exposure. Source: transcript `[39:02]` to `[44:50]`.
- **Course mechanics.** Lectures, homeworks, project, and forum are all part of the learning loop. Source: transcript `[44:54]` to `[55:57]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Framework ergonomics, not only model novelty, drove deep learning adoption. The lecture explicitly ties the popularity spike to easy Python-based autograd stacks. Source: transcript `[16:44]` to `[18:04]`.
- The `AI` label is partly a rebrand effect. The chart can look flatter because many things are now counted under AI rather than deep learning. Source: transcript `[18:09]` to `[18:36]`.
- A small group can still move the field if the toolchain is good enough. The lecture uses DeOldify, timm, and Stable Diffusion as proof. Source: transcript `[11:11]` to `[13:30]`.
- The course is designed as a systems apprenticeship. The point is not to memorize concepts; it is to rebuild the machinery that makes the concepts usable. Source: transcript `[2:28]` to `[3:00]`, `[37:15]` to `[46:32]`.
- The raw productivity delta is the headline: 44,000 lines and six months versus roughly 100 lines and a few hours. Source: transcript `[19:37]` to `[20:21]`.

## Connections
