# L20 Generative Models

Lecture 20 turns from supervised classification to generative modeling. The core move is simple: replace the label-prediction objective with a learned game between a generator that makes fake samples and a discriminator that tries to tell real from fake.

## Summary
- Generative modeling tries to learn a distribution, not a label map: sample latent noise, transform it, and make the output look like the dataset. Source: transcript `[1:47]` to `[4:28]`.
- GANs solve the missing objective by pairing a generator with a discriminator in a minimax game. The discriminator learns real-vs-fake; the generator learns to fool it. Source: transcript `[7:15]` to `[14:47]`.
- The training loop alternates discriminator and generator updates, which is powerful but unstable and sensitive to tuning. Source: transcript `[14:47]` to `[20:32]`.
- Once you treat GAN as a module or loss, you can compose it with other architectures, including DCGAN and CycleGAN. Source: transcript `[21:08]` to `[37:54]`.
- The lecture treats modularity as the real superpower: deep learning components become Lego bricks, and GANs are just one more brick. Source: transcript `[21:08]` to `[37:54]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Unsupervised / generative modeling.** The model is trained from data samples only; no class labels are required. Source: transcript `[3:31]` to `[4:28]`.
- **Distribution matching.** The goal is not one-sample accuracy; it is making the generated sample distribution close to the target distribution. Source: transcript `[2:29]` to `[3:28]`.
- **Generator `G`.** Maps latent noise `z` into fake samples. Source: transcript `[8:13]` to `[10:26]`.
- **Discriminator `D`.** A classifier that distinguishes real data from generated data. Source: transcript `[11:01]` to `[12:20]`.
- **Minimax objective.** The discriminator maximizes correct classification; the generator pushes the discriminator toward failure. Source: transcript `[13:22]` to `[16:30]`.
- **Alternating updates.** GAN training repeatedly updates `D`, then `G`, rather than optimizing a single static loss. Source: transcript `[14:47]` to `[16:30]`.
- **DCGAN.** Uses convolutional generator and discriminator networks, with `Conv2dTranspose` as the upsampling primitive. Source: transcript `[24:58]` to `[27:19]`.
- **CycleGAN.** Solves unpaired image-to-image translation using two GAN losses plus cycle-consistency loss. Source: transcript `[27:51]` to `[37:54]`.
- **Cycle consistency.** Mapping `x -> y -> x` should reconstruct the original input even without paired data. Source: transcript `[33:45]` to `[35:22]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- GANs are not mainly about classification. The discriminator is really a learned metric that pressures the generator toward realism. Source: transcript `[7:15]` to `[10:26]` and `[17:01]` to `[20:01]`.
- The discriminator does not end the story. It exists to make the generator better by surfacing subtle artifacts humans might miss. Source: transcript `[17:01]` to `[19:59]`.
- Unpaired translation becomes possible once you combine adversarial distribution matching with cycle consistency. Source: transcript `[30:50]` to `[35:22]`.
- The lecture treats modularization as a design principle, not a convenience. GANs become useful because they compose with other networks cleanly. Source: transcript `[21:08]` to `[27:19]` and `[36:02]` to `[36:55]`.
- The brittleness is structural: the same adversarial pressure that improves realism also makes convergence hard. Source: transcript `[20:07]` to `[20:32]`.

## Connections
- To supervised learning: GANs swap the direct label loss for a two-player game, but the training mechanics still rely on backpropagation. Source: transcript `[0:39]` to `[5:04]` and `[10:02]` to `[14:47]`.
- To the rest of the course: this lecture is the theory layer for the implementation lecture that follows. Source: transcript `[20:53]` to `[27:19]` and lecture 21 transcript `[0:00]` to `[35:22]`.
- To modular deep learning: the lecture explicitly compares GAN composition to swapping loss functions in residual and detection pipelines. Source: transcript `[21:10]` to `[22:44]`.
- To image translation: CycleGAN is the example that makes the modularity argument concrete. Source: transcript `[27:51]` to `[37:54]`.
- To later generative practice: the lecture notes that diffusion models are a more recent successor, which puts GANs in the historical middle of the generative-model stack. Source: transcript `[19:13]` to `[19:25]`.
