# L19 Policy Gradients in Practice

This lecture is the handoff from policy-gradient math to code. The move is ugly but useful: write a fake weighted maximum-likelihood loss, let autodiff take the derivative, and keep the real RL objective out of the graph.

## Summary
- Policy gradients can be implemented as a pseudo-loss: weighted log-likelihood with reward-to-go weights, then mean-reduce and backprop. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[2:21]` to `[5:28]`.
- The trick works because autodiff only differentiates the graph you hand it; the `q_hat` weights are treated as constants during backprop, so the pseudo-loss returns the desired policy gradient. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[2:59]` to `[3:15]` and `[5:32]` to `[5:54]`.
- In code, this is just supervised learning with sample weights: compute logits, cross-entropy or log-probs, multiply by reward-to-go, and call backward. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[3:54]` to `[5:28]`.
- Policy gradients are noisy enough that the engineering defaults change: larger batches, more learning-rate care, and Adam are practical starting points; vanilla SGD with momentum is brittle. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[5:59]` to `[6:57]`.
- The lecture closes by recapping the on-policy / off-policy picture and pointing to natural gradient as the next stability fix. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[7:00]` to `[7:29]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Pseudo-loss.** A quantity that is not the RL objective, but whose gradient is the policy gradient. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[2:45]` to `[3:15]`.
- **Weighted maximum likelihood.** The policy loss is the same shape as supervised learning, except the sample log-likelihoods are weighted by reward-to-go. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[3:54]` to `[5:20]`.
- **Autodiff graph control.** The framework differentiates only the graph it sees, so the reward weights stay outside the gradient path. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[3:00]` to `[3:15]`.
- **Reward-to-go.** `q_values` are precomputed outside the autodiff package and fed in as weights. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[5:02]` to `[5:54]`.
- **Large-batch optimization.** High-variance gradients force larger batches and more careful optimizer tuning. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[6:04]` to `[6:57]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The forward pass is intentionally fake. Only the gradient matters, so the right question is not "is this loss meaningful?" but "does its derivative equal the policy gradient?" Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[2:45]` to `[3:15]`.
- Keeping `q_values` outside the graph is a memory and tooling choice, not a mathematical one. It keeps the implementation close to supervised learning while avoiding a much larger graph. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[5:02]` to `[5:54]`.
- The lecture treats variance as the real systems problem. The algorithm looks like cross-entropy, but the optimization regime is not remotely the same. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[6:04]` to `[6:57]`.
- Hyperparameter pain is structural here. The model is not "harder to tune" by accident; the gradient estimator is noisy by construction. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L19.txt` `[6:18]` to `[6:57]`.
