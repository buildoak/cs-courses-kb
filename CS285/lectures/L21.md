# L21 Actor-Critic Algorithms and Policy Evaluation

This is where the course stops pretending Monte Carlo returns are free. The critic trades exactness for lower variance, and in practice that is the whole game.

## Summary
- Actor-critic splits the job: the actor updates the policy, the critic estimates value functions. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[0:01]` to `[1:28]`.
- Replacing the Monte Carlo return with a learned `V(s)` baseline makes the policy-gradient estimate much lower variance, because state-only prediction is easier than `Q(s,a)` or `A(s,a)`. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[4:05]` to `[7:52]` and `[14:12]` to `[15:14]`.
- The advantage is `Q - V`, and the lecture derives an approximate version using bootstrapping: `r + V(s') - V(s)`. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[12:55]` to `[14:10]`.
- Monte Carlo policy evaluation is unbiased but noisy; bootstrapping lowers variance at the cost of bias, which is usually the right trade. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[16:07]` to `[23:22]`.
- The board-game examples make the point concrete: in backgammon or Go, `V(s)` is basically "how likely am I to win from this board?" Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[23:38]` to `[25:11]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Policy evaluation.** Estimating `V^pi(s)` for the current policy. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[15:15]` to `[16:07]`.
- **Monte Carlo target.** Sum the rewards that actually happened after the visited state. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[16:13]` to `[17:34]`.
- **Value function.** A state-only estimator of expected future return, easier to fit than Q or A. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[14:14]` to `[15:14]`.
- **Advantage function.** `Q(s,a) - V(s)`, the quantity the policy gradient wants. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[6:52]` to `[7:52]` and `[12:55]` to `[14:10]`.
- **Bootstrap estimate.** Use `V-hat(s')` to build the next target instead of relying entirely on the sampled rollout. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[21:51]` to `[23:22]`.
- **Actor-critic.** The actor uses the critic's value estimates to lower gradient variance. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[0:01]` to `[1:28]` and `[14:45]` to `[15:14]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The critic is not a side quest. It is the variance-reduction mechanism that makes policy gradients usable at all. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[4:05]` to `[10:10]`.
- Fitting `V(s)` is easier than fitting `Q(s,a)` because the target depends on fewer variables, so the approximation problem is simpler. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[14:14]` to `[14:45]`.
- Bootstrapping is a deliberate bias-for-variance swap, not a hack. It imports the critic's earlier estimate into the next target. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[21:51]` to `[23:22]`.
- The board-game examples are useful because they collapse value estimation into a probability-of-winning story, which is much easier to reason about than abstract returns. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[23:38]` to `[25:11]`.
- The lecture's subtle point is that policy improvement and policy evaluation become a coupled loop. That is the real actor-critic move. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN852J8508KMKHW3C24QRNB0/transcripts/L21.txt` `[15:15]` to `[23:22]`.
