# L14 RL Algorithms in the Wild

This lecture is a gallery of actual systems. The point is to stop treating RL families as abstract labels and look at what they do on Atari, robots, and continuous control.

## Summary
- The lecture starts with concrete examples of value-based, policy-gradient, actor-critic, and model-based RL systems. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[0:00]` to `[1:04]`.
- Atari DQN is the value-based example: a CNN estimates `Q(s,a)` and action selection becomes an `argmax` over a small discrete action set. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[1:09]` to `[1:49]`.
- Guided policy search is the model-based robotics example: it mixes dynamics models with image-based convolutional networks to learn control skills. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[1:53]` to `[2:10]`.
- TRPO with GAE is the continuous-control policy-gradient example, and the lecture treats it as an actor-critic-style method. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[2:13]` to `[2:38]`.
- The closing example shows that Q-learning can be modified to handle continuous-action grasping, so the family boundaries are not rigid. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[2:41]` to `[2:58]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Value-based RL.** Learn `Q(s,a)` and pick actions by `argmax`. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[1:28]` to `[1:49]`.
- **DQN.** The Atari demo combines Q-learning with convolutional networks over pixels. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[1:17]` to `[1:49]`.
- **Model-based robotics.** Guided policy search uses learned dynamics plus perception to control physical systems. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[1:53]` to `[2:10]`.
- **Policy gradient with a critic.** TRPO plus value-function approximation lands in the actor-critic bucket. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[2:13]` to `[2:30]`.
- **Continuous control.** The humanoid-walking demo shows why policy-gradient methods matter when actions are not discrete. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[2:31]` to `[2:38]`.
- **Continuous-action Q-learning.** The grasping robot example shows that value-based methods can be adapted beyond Atari. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[2:41]` to `[2:58]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture uses examples to erase category worship. "Model-based" and "policy gradient" are not clean silos once you see the actual systems. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[0:24]` to `[1:04]`.
- TRPO with a value function is effectively actor-critic, even when the headline sounds like a pure policy-gradient method. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[2:18]` to `[2:30]`.
- Q-learning is not just for discrete games. The grasping demo shows a modified form that works with continuous actions. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[2:45]` to `[2:58]`.
- Robotics is the place where model-based RL looks especially natural, because the learned dynamics and the perceptual front-end are both doing useful work. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[1:53]` to `[2:10]`.
- The lecture is less about choosing a winner than about showing where each family already works well. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RDFTW32XFS91AMF6VYQC/research-118/L14.transcript.txt` `[0:00]` to `[2:58]`.
