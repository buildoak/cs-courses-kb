# L18 Off-Policy Policy Gradients and Importance Sampling

This lecture asks the annoying question: can we reuse data from an old policy? The answer is yes in theory, but the exact correction explodes in variance, so the useful version is already an approximation.

## Summary
- Policy gradients are on-policy because each update needs fresh samples from the current policy. Reusing old data requires importance sampling. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[0:11]` to `[3:24]`.
- Importance sampling makes off-policy estimation exact in expectation; for trajectories, the environment terms cancel and only policy-probability ratios remain. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[3:24]` to `[6:13]`.
- The full trajectory importance weight is dangerous: it multiplies ratios over time, so variance grows exponentially with horizon. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[8:33]` to `[11:47]`.
- A practical workaround drops the state-marginal ratio and keeps only action-probability ratios, giving a bounded-error approximation when policy updates are small. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[11:54]` to `[15:32]`.

## Key Concepts
- **On-policy.** The policy gradient needs samples from the policy it is currently optimizing. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[0:11]` to `[1:32]`.
- **Off-policy.** Reusing data from a different policy or from demonstrations requires a distribution correction. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[2:43]` to `[3:24]`.
- **Importance sampling.** The exact identity that converts an expectation under one distribution into samples from another distribution. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[3:24]` to `[4:40]`.
- **Trajectory distribution.** For MDPs, the ratio between two trajectory distributions cancels the initial state and transition terms, leaving policy terms only. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[5:26]` to `[6:13]`.
- **State-action marginal.** The lecture rewrites the gradient using state-action marginals to set up a more practical approximation. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[12:28]` to `[13:16]`.
- **State marginal ratio.** The approximate algorithm drops the state probability ratio because it is the term that drives exponential variance. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[13:28]` to `[15:32]`.
- **Policy iteration.** If the reward-side importance weights are ignored, the lecture says the result becomes a policy-iteration-style improvement procedure. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[10:07]` to `[10:28]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: the environment dynamics disappear from the importance ratio, so the only thing that matters is how the two policies differ. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[5:26]` to `[6:13]`.
- Inference: the real failure mode is multiplicative; ratios slightly below one collapse toward zero, which is why variance blows up with horizon. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[11:04]` to `[11:47]`.
- Inference: the practical algorithm is deliberately not exact; it trades exactness for bounded error and only makes sense when the new policy stays close to the old one. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[14:37]` to `[15:32]`.
- Inference: this lecture is the first point where the course starts treating "close policy updates" as a design constraint, not just an optimization detail. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L18.txt` `[14:37]` to `[15:32]`.
