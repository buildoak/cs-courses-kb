# L16 Policy Gradient Intuition and the Variance Problem

This lecture turns the policy gradient equation back into intuition. The move is simple: treat reward as a weight on the log-probability gradient, then accept that the estimator is noisy enough that practical RL needs variance reduction.

## Summary
- Policy gradients behave like weighted maximum likelihood: increase log-probability on good trajectories, decrease it on bad ones. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L16.txt` `[4:48]` to `[6:07]`.
- The same derivation works for discrete and continuous action spaces; for continuous actions, the lecture uses a Gaussian policy whose mean comes from a neural network. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L16.txt` `[0:31]` to `[4:19]`.
- The derivation does not use the Markov property, so the same policy-gradient equation applies in partially observed systems by swapping states for observations. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L16.txt` `[6:15]` to `[7:30]`.
- The practical problem is variance: finite-sample gradients swing wildly, especially when rewards are shifted or all positive, so vanilla policy gradients need later fixes. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L16.txt` `[8:53]` to `[12:09]`.

## Key Concepts
- **`grad log pi`.** The gradient of the log policy probability; this is the core term in the policy-gradient update. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L16.txt` `[0:31]` to `[1:57]`.
- **Weighted maximum likelihood.** Policy gradient raises the probability of trajectories with positive reward and lowers it for negative reward, instead of blindly increasing every observed action probability. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L16.txt` `[1:23]` to `[6:07]`.
- **Gaussian policy.** For continuous actions, the policy can output a mean and variance for a multivariate normal distribution. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L16.txt` `[3:09]` to `[4:19]`.
- **Trial-and-error formalization.** The lecture explicitly frames policy gradient as the gradient-ascent version of trial-and-error learning. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L16.txt` `[5:54]` to `[6:07]`.
- **Partial observability.** The same gradient derivation works when policies condition on observations instead of states. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L16.txt` `[6:15]` to `[7:30]`.
- **High variance.** Reward offsets can change finite-sample gradients a lot even though they do not change the optimum. Source: `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN84RXDMMSH1Q2P6XKTC9538/transcripts/L16.txt` `[9:52]` to `[12:09]`.
