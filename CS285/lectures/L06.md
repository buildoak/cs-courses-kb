## Summary
- Q-learning only becomes usable once you stabilize the moving target. Replay buffers decorrelate samples; target networks slow the target drift.
- The UTD ratio is the hidden efficiency knob. More inner-loop optimization per collected transition can improve data efficiency, but too much of it turns into overfitting.
- Overestimation bias is the next failure mode. Double Q-learning fixes it by separating action selection from action evaluation.
- Continuous actions make the `argmax` the hard part. The practical options are random search, CEM/CMA-ES, a quadratic Q class, or a learned maximizer network.
- The lecture's theory punchline matches lecture 7: fitted value methods with neural nets are often unstable, so most of the real performance comes from engineering patches.

## Key Concepts
- **Replay buffer.** Stored transitions reused across many updates.
- **Target network.** A slower copy of the Q parameters used to build the bootstrap target.
- **Moving target.** The bootstrap target changes as the network changes, which makes naive regression unstable.
- **DQN.** The classic replay-buffer plus target-network recipe for deep Q-learning.
- **UTD ratio.** Update-to-data ratio, the amount of regression work done per collected transition.
- **Bellman error.** The squared regression error between current Q and the target.
- **Double Q-learning.** Use one network to choose the action and another to evaluate it.
- **n-step return.** A longer backup that often helps early training.
- **Huber loss / gradient clipping.** Stabilizers for large Bellman-error gradients.
- **CEM / CMA-ES.** Derivative-free optimizers for continuous-action maximization.
- **NAF.** Quadratic-in-action Q functions that make the max closed-form.
- **DDPG.** A learned maximizer network for continuous-action Q-learning.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Replay buffers are not just memory. They turn a correlated online process into something much closer to regression.
- Target networks are the simplest way to break the feedback loop between the predictor and its own target.
- Q-learning can look like supervised learning without actually being gradient descent on a fixed objective. The target moves, so the optimization problem keeps changing.
- In continuous control, the max is often more expensive than the Bellman backup itself. That is why the lecture spends so much time on argmax approximators.
- DDPG is conceptually approximate Q-learning, not a separate kingdom. The "actor" is just the maximizer network.
- Value-based and actor-critic methods are closer than the taxonomy suggests. The lecture keeps moving between them because the computational bottleneck is the same argmax.

## Referenced Papers
- Watkins, *Learning from Delayed Rewards* - the original Q-learning paper.
- Riedmiller, *Neural Fitted Q-Iteration* - batch-mode fitted Q-learning with function approximation.
- Mnih et al. (2013), *Playing Atari with Deep Reinforcement Learning* - the classic DQN recipe with replay and target networks.
- van Hasselt et al. (2016), *Deep Reinforcement Learning with Double Q-learning* - the standard fix for max overestimation.
- Lillicrap et al. (2015), *Continuous control with deep reinforcement learning* - DDPG, the learned-maximizer route.
- Gu et al. (2016), *Continuous Deep Q-Learning with Model-Based Acceleration* - the NAF-style quadratic Q-function trick.
- Munos et al. (2016), *Safe and Efficient Off-Policy Reinforcement Learning* - the off-policy/importance-sampling background for longer backups.
- Wang et al. (2016), *Dueling Network Architectures for Deep Reinforcement Learning* - the discrete-action Q decomposition that sits next to the NAF idea.
