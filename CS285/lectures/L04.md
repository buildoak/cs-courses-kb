## Summary
- Actor-critic is policy gradient plus a learned critic. The critic estimates value or advantage, so the policy update uses a lower-variance signal than raw reward-to-go.
- A state-only baseline keeps the policy gradient unbiased. A value function is the natural baseline because it approximates expected return without depending on the action.
- The practical design space is mostly about tradeoffs: separate actor/critic networks are simple and stable, shared trunks are more efficient, and batching can be synchronous, asynchronous, or off-policy.
- n-step returns and generalized advantage estimation sit in the middle of the bias/variance tradeoff. They are not new objectives, just better estimators.
- The lecture ends by treating actor-critic as an engineering family, not a single algorithm: A3C, GAE, Q-Prop, and SAC all land in the same neighborhood.

## Key Concepts
- **Actor.** The policy network that chooses actions.
- **Critic.** The value-function network that estimates how good a state or state-action pair is.
- **Reward-to-go.** The sum of future rewards used in REINFORCE-style updates.
- **Baseline.** Any subtraction term that reduces variance without changing the expected gradient, if it depends only on state.
- **Advantage.** `Q(s, a) - V(s)`, or an estimate of how much better an action is than average.
- **State-dependent baseline.** The value function used as a safe baseline.
- **Control variate.** An action-dependent baseline with an extra correction term.
- **n-step return.** A hybrid between one-step TD and full Monte Carlo return.
- **GAE.** A weighted mixture of n-step returns that smooths the bias/variance tradeoff.
- **Batch actor-critic.** Collect a batch, fit the critic, then update the policy.
- **Synchronous / asynchronous actor-critic.** Parallel workers either update in lockstep or with stale but useful parameters.
- **Off-policy actor-critic.** Reuse older transitions from a replay buffer instead of insisting on fresh on-policy data.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The critic is mostly a variance machine. It does not make the objective better by itself; it makes the estimator less noisy.
- The baseline proof is the real trick. If the baseline ignores the action, the policy-gradient expectation still cancels cleanly.
- Asynchronous actor-critic is a batching hack with small staleness. It works because the lag is usually small enough to be useful.
- Replay-buffered actor-critic pushes further toward off-policy learning, but then the clean on-policy derivation stops applying without extra care.
- GAE is just a weighted average of n-step estimators. The win is the weighting, not the existence of a new formula.
- Discounting shows up twice in this lecture: once as a practical horizon control, once as a variance-reduction knob.

## Referenced Papers
- Sutton, McAllester, Singh, Mansour (1999), *Policy Gradient Methods for Reinforcement Learning with Function Approximation: Actor-Critic Algorithms with Value Function Approximation* - the classic actor-critic foundations paper.
- Thomas (2014), *Bias in Natural Actor-Critic Algorithms* - the lecture's explicit further-reading note on bias in actor-critic updates.
- Mnih et al. (2016), *Asynchronous Methods for Deep Reinforcement Learning* - the A3C-style parallel online actor-critic template.
- Schulman, Moritz, Levine, Jordan, Abbeel (2016), *High-Dimensional Continuous Control Using Generalized Advantage Estimation* - the batch-mode GAE paper.
- Gu et al. (2017), *Q-Prop: Sample-Efficient Policy Gradient with an Off-Policy Critic* - action-dependent control variates with a critic.
- Haarnoja, Zhou, Abbeel, Levine (2018), *Soft Actor-Critic: Off-Policy Maximum Entropy Deep Reinforcement Learning with a Stochastic Actor* - the practical off-policy actor-critic example cited on the slide deck.
