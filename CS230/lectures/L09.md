# CS230 L09: What's Going On Inside My Model?

This lecture is the course's debugging toolkit: check the loss, inspect failures, visualize internal representations, watch scaling laws, and keep evaluation honest. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[1:49:45]`.

## Summary
- The lecture starts from a simple model-trainer question: if a checkpoint gets worse, inspect error cases, loss curves, data batches, and sanity checks before touching the model. Source: transcript `[2:37]` to `[10:42]`.
- CNN interpretability gets a practical tour through saliency maps, integrated gradients, class activation maps, class model visualization, and deconvolution / transpose-convolution reverse tracing. Source: transcript `[21:02]` to `[1:05:30]`.
- Transformer interpretability is much harder; attention and embeddings help, but the lecture points to Anthropic's transformer-circuit work as the best current handle. Source: transcript `[1:14:17]` to `[1:18:14]`.
- Scaling laws, capability benchmarks, and safety evals are the macro diagnostics, but benchmark contamination is a real failure mode. Source: transcript `[1:20:42]` to `[1:31:08]`.
- Data diagnostics close the loop: gradient norms, learning-rate behavior, checkpoint-to-checkpoint comparisons, and token distribution shifts are the operational signals. Source: transcript `[1:32:35]` to `[1:38:31]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Sanity checks.** When a run misbehaves, the first move is to inspect loss curves and examples, not to guess. Source: transcript `[2:49]` to `[4:14]`.
- **Training and validation loss.** Smooth trajectories matter; spikes are a sign that something broke. Source: transcript `[5:09]` to `[5:44]`.
- **Data batches.** Bad batches or poisoned data can explain sudden regressions. Source: transcript `[5:53]` to `[6:15]`.
- **Exploding and vanishing gradients.** Gradient pathologies are a standard thing to look for when training goes sideways. Source: transcript `[8:30]` to `[8:33]`.
- **Saliency maps.** Gradient-based pixel attribution gives a quick check on what the model is using. Source: transcript `[22:15]` to `[23:10]`.
- **Integrated gradients.** A stronger variant of saliency that integrates gradient path information. Source: transcript `[23:36]` to `[24:46]`.
- **Class activation maps.** Weighting feature maps by class contribution gives a coarse localization view. Source: transcript `[34:14]` to `[35:21]`.
- **Class model visualization.** Gradient ascent can synthesize an input that maximizes a class or neuron. Source: transcript `[39:20]` to `[41:18]`.
- **Deconvolution / transpose convolutions.** Reverse tracing helps map a strong activation back into input space. Source: transcript `[49:27]` to `[1:05:04]`.
- **Embeddings and attention.** Embedding geometry and attention patterns provide sanity checks on learned representations. Source: transcript `[1:16:34]` to `[1:17:14]`.
- **Transformer circuits.** Anthropic's circuit analysis is presented as the best current way to reason about internal transformer behavior. Source: transcript `[1:17:37]` to `[1:18:14]`.
- **Scaling laws.** Training curves should respect the expected power-law behavior; deviations are a warning sign. Source: transcript `[1:20:42]` to `[1:24:40]`.
- **Benchmark contamination.** A benchmark can look good while practice is bad if the benchmark is leaked, narrow, or gamed. Source: transcript `[1:26:48]` to `[1:28:02]`.
- **Safety evals.** Safety is a first-class diagnostic, not a side note. Source: transcript `[1:30:12]` to `[1:31:08]`.
- **Data diagnostics.** Gradient norms, checkpoint comparisons, and token distributions are part of the operational dashboard. Source: transcript `[1:37:40]` to `[1:38:31]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Interpretability is useful, but it is still partial. The lecture is explicit that modern transformers are still beyond full human-level explanation. Source: transcript `[1:17:19]` to `[1:18:14]`.
- Saliency maps are not a final answer. They are a quick sanity check before deeper interpretation. Source: transcript `[22:30]` to `[23:10]`.
- A benchmark can mislead you twice: once through contamination and again through mismatch between benchmark behavior and real deployment. Source: transcript `[1:26:48]` to `[1:28:02]`.
- Scaling-law plots are not vanity metrics. They are a regression test for whether the training run is behaving like a real model should. Source: transcript `[1:22:13]` to `[1:24:40]`.
- The lecture keeps separating model quality from infrastructure quality. Many failures are debugging problems, not architecture problems. Source: transcript `[2:49]` to `[10:42]`, `[1:37:40]` to `[1:38:31]`.
- Transformer interpretability is still a small-model game. The lecture says the cutting edge is mostly limited to two-layer transformers. Source: transcript `[1:17:19]` to `[1:17:30]`.
- Data diagnostics are not optional ops clutter. They are the evidence layer for deciding whether to retrain, patch data, or change evaluation. Source: transcript `[1:32:35]` to `[1:38:31]`.
