# L02 Supervised, Self-Supervised, and Weakly Supervised Learning

Lecture 2 is about label design. The model matters, but the shape of the labels, the task, and the embedding space matter more than people expect.

## Summary
- The lecture starts with a recap of supervised learning and then walks through three case studies: day/night classification, trigger-word detection, and face verification. Source: `[3:30]` to `[3:54]`, `[18:52]` to `[19:18]`, `[34:27]` to `[35:26]`, `[55:27]` to `[58:26]`.
- The recurring theme is that output design is part of model design. One-hot, multi-hot, framewise labels, and triplet examples all change what the system can learn. Source: `[11:18]` to `[12:46]`, `[46:55]` to `[47:41]`, `[67:07]` to `[67:52]`.
- Face verification is built around encodings and embeddings, with triplet loss forcing anchor-positive pairs together and anchor-negative pairs apart. Source: `[1:06:58]` to `[1:12:53]`.
- The lecture then moves to self-supervised learning, where the training objective is manufactured from the data itself. Source: `[1:20:24]` to `[1:29:10]`.
- Weak supervision closes the loop by exploiting naturally occurring pairings, especially text-image captions and other shared modalities. Source: `[1:34:31]` to `[1:39:17]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Supervised learning.** Inputs map to explicit labels, usually with a straightforward output layer. Source: `[3:30]` to `[4:04]`.
- **Architecture vs parameters.** The lecture separates the model blueprint from the learned weights. Source: `[3:57]` to `[4:04]`.
- **Regression vs classification.** The same input can become a scalar prediction problem instead of a binary label problem. Source: `[6:41]` to `[6:54]`.
- **One-hot and multi-hot labels.** Multi-class and multi-label problems need label vectors, not scalar 0/1 targets. Source: `[11:18]` to `[12:46]`.
- **Encoding vs embedding.** An embedding is an encoding whose distances carry meaning. Source: `[16:32]` to `[18:35]`.
- **Trigger-word detection.** The useful label is the one that lets the model predict at the right temporal resolution, not the easiest label for a human to draw. Source: `[43:56]` to `[47:41]`.
- **Triplet loss.** Anchor, positive, and negative examples teach the network a distance geometry. Source: `[1:07:07]` to `[1:12:53]`.
- **Self-supervised learning.** Build labels from augmentation, masking, or prediction tasks derived from raw data. Source: `[1:24:01]` to `[1:29:10]`.
- **Contrastive learning.** Make transformed views of the same item agree in embedding space. Source: `[1:25:48]` to `[1:26:52]`.
- **Weak supervision.** Use naturally paired data, such as image-caption pairs, instead of hand-labeling everything. Source: `[1:34:31]` to `[1:35:04]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The biggest mistake in the lecture's case studies is not a bad model. It is a bad labeling scheme. Source: `[11:24]` to `[12:46]`, `[45:04]` to `[47:41]`.
- A label scheme that is harder for humans to produce can be much easier for the model to learn from. Source: `[45:04]` to `[46:50]`.
- For trigger-word detection, a weaker label that marks only the activate word creates imbalance; a more balanced labeling scheme trains better. Source: `[47:00]` to `[48:24]`.
- Triplets are a training trick, not a deployment requirement. At test time, the face encoder runs without the negative examples. Source: `[1:14:08]` to `[1:14:28]`.
- Self-supervision is mostly structured label fabrication. The data already contains the signal if you can define the right pretext task. Source: `[1:24:01]` to `[1:26:36]`.
- Text often becomes the pivot space for multimodal systems because many modalities connect naturally to text. Source: `[1:37:50]` to `[1:39:17]`.
