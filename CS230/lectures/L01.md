# L01 Introduction to Deep Learning

Lecture 1 is not a math lecture. It is the course thesis: deep learning won because large neural nets scale predictably, and the real work is learning how to steer data, compute, and iteration.

## Summary
- The class is flipped: watch the videos online, use in-person time for discussion, and treat the lecture track as the default content stream. Source: `[0:35]` to `[1:58]`.
- Deep learning won because larger neural networks keep improving as data and compute increase, unlike older ML systems that plateaued. Source: `[3:00]` to `[4:54]`.
- The lecture traces that rise to Stanford CUDA work, a Baidu scaling paper, and later OpenAI scaling-laws work that made performance more forecastable. Source: `[4:56]` to `[6:50]`.
- The course is organized around five modules: basics of neural networks and deep learning, tuning hyperparameters, machine-learning project strategy, convolutional networks, and sequence models/transformers. Source: `[23:32]` to `[30:07]`.
- The operational message is data-centric: build fast, inspect failures, and adjust the data instead of assuming the model is the only lever. Source: `[27:02]` to `[30:35]`, `[55:13]` to `[1:06:52]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Flipped classroom.** The lecture format is intentionally asynchronous so live time can be spent on harder questions. Source: `[0:35]` to `[1:58]`.
- **Scaling behavior.** Bigger networks plus more data produced better performance in a way older algorithms often did not. Source: `[3:00]` to `[4:54]`.
- **Compute as enabler.** Early CUDA/GPU work is framed as a practical foundation for deep learning's rise. Source: `[4:56]` to `[5:44]`.
- **Predictable gains.** Scaling laws turn model-building into something closer to forecasting than intuition alone. Source: `[6:18]` to `[6:50]`.
- **Hyperparameters.** Learning rate, network size, and similar knobs are treated as the practical control surface for training. Source: `[24:10]` to `[24:30]`.
- **Data-centric AI.** The course pushes the idea that changing the data is often the most effective way to change the system. Source: `[27:02]` to `[27:47]`.
- **Error analysis.** Look at where the model fails, then collect or fix data for that specific slice. Source: `[27:26]` to `[28:30]`.
- **Data drift / concept drift.** Deployment changes the input distribution, so monitoring is part of the job. Source: `[55:13]` to `[1:06:52]`.
- **Dashboards and telemetry.** You do not know in advance which metrics matter, so you instrument broadly and inspect the surprising parts. Source: `[1:01:47]` to `[1:06:52]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture's core claim is not "deep nets are smart." It is that the field found a recipe that lets large models absorb lots of data without collapsing into a dead end. Source: `[4:18]` to `[4:54]`.
- Speed matters more than purity when collecting data. Fast, imperfect data helps you discover what the model actually needs. Source: `[17:13]` to `[17:37]`, `[20:22]` to `[20:36]`.
- More data is not a single lever. Targeted data for the right failure mode beats generic data hoarding. Source: `[27:19]` to `[30:35]`.
- Models can look fine in development and then fail in the world because the world changes under them. Source: `[55:13]` to `[1:00:52]`.
- Simple systems can be more robust to drift than large neural nets, even if they are less glamorous. Source: `[1:00:30]` to `[1:01:12]`.
- The most useful question is usually not "how do I train a better model?" but "what data and failure mode should I inspect next?" Source: `[27:26]` to `[28:37]`.
