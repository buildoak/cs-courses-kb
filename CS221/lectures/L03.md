# L03 AI Today: Power, Risk, and Amplification

The lecture's core claim is simple: AI is no longer a lab curiosity. It is infrastructure, and infrastructure amplifies whatever it touches.

## Summary
- The lecture opens with the AI Index and the claim that AI has moved from an academic niche into national strategy and real deployment. Source: `[0:05]` to `[1:26]`.
- AI is framed as an amplifier: it makes what is good better and what is bad worse. Source: `[1:15]` to `[1:23]`, `[12:24]` to `[13:32]`.
- The benefit side is concrete: speech recognition, assistants, translation, accessibility tools, autonomous driving, healthcare, and poverty mapping. Source: `[1:36]` to `[5:00]`.
- The risk side is equally concrete: energy use, privacy loss, security attacks, bias, fairness failures, deepfakes, and feedback loops. Source: `[5:19]` to `[12:17]`.
- The closing line is the operational warning: just because you can build it does not mean you should. Source: `[12:24]` to `[13:32]`.

## Key Concepts
- **AI Index.** The lecture uses annual tracking data to show scale: compute, conference growth, and labor-market impact. Source: `[0:21]` to `[0:48]`.
- **Amplifier.** The lecture's main metaphor: AI scales both benefits and harms. Source: `[1:15]` to `[1:23]`.
- **Speech recognition.** One of the clearest wins in the last decade. Source: `[1:36]` to `[1:48]`.
- **Machine translation.** Useful enough to remove language barriers, but still imperfect and socially loaded. Source: `[2:20]` to `[2:49]`.
- **Accessibility tooling.** Seeing AI and auto-captioning make AI legible to people who otherwise lose access. Source: `[3:17]` to `[3:38]`.
- **Healthcare.** Diagnosis and therapeutic development are big deployment zones, especially where clinical expertise is scarce. Source: `[3:41]` to `[4:25]`.
- **Poverty mapping.** Satellite imagery and ML become policy tools when field surveys are too expensive. Source: `[4:30]` to `[5:00]`.
- **Energy consumption.** The lecture treats training cost as an environmental and policy problem, not just an engineering one. Source: `[5:19]` to `[6:49]`.
- **Privacy-preserving ML.** On-device learning and decentralized statistics are the mitigation path. Source: `[6:49]` to `[7:21]`.
- **Security.** Stop-sign stickers and face-ID glasses show that accuracy alone is not robustness. Source: `[7:21]` to `[8:10]`.
- **Bias.** Machine translation can reveal and amplify gender stereotypes from data. Source: `[8:21]` to `[9:15]`.
- **Fairness.** COMPAS and the ProPublica dispute show that different fairness criteria can conflict. Source: `[9:24]` to `[10:30]`.
- **Deepfakes.** Generative models now make fake content cheap, convincing, and socially dangerous. Source: `[10:52]` to `[11:32]`.
- **Feedback loops.** Predictions change user behavior, user behavior becomes training data, and the system can polarize itself. Source: `[11:32]` to `[12:17]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture is not making one generic "AI is risky" argument. It is listing separate failure classes: cost, privacy, security, bias, and dynamical instability. Source: `[5:19]` to `[12:17]`.
- Bias is described as a property of data flowing through statistical learning, not as an accidental edge case. Source: `[8:53]` to `[9:15]`.
- The feedback-loop section matters because deployed ML is not closed-world inference. It changes the world it later learns from. Source: `[11:35]` to `[12:17]`.
- The metaphor of amplification is precise. The same system can expand accessibility and expand disinformation. Source: `[1:15]` to `[1:23]`, `[10:52]` to `[12:17]`.
- The real danger is mundane scale: prediction, recommendations, and ads constantly retrain on human behavior. Source: `[11:35]` to `[12:17]`.
