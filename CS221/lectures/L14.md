# L14: Bayesian Networks

Bayesian networks keep the factor-graph skeleton but give the factors directed meaning: local conditional distributions, multiplied together, become a joint model of the world.

## Summary
- A Bayesian network is a DAG over random variables plus local conditional distributions; multiplying them yields the joint distribution. Source: transcript `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN8361PJE29V637Y0XFT9DQB/xvC6-bayes-nets-2.txt`, `[24:54]` to `[28:16]`.
- The overview lecture treats BNs as a generative language: topic modeling, inverse graphics, error-correcting codes, and DNA matching all fit. Source: transcript `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN8361PJE29V637Y0XFT9DQB/fA7z-bayes-nets-1.txt`, `[1:52]` to `[5:46]`.
- The value is not just prediction: BNs handle heterogeneously missing information, inject prior knowledge, and keep intermediate variables interpretable. Source: transcript `[6:10]` to `[8:56]`.
- Probabilistic inference reduces a BN to a Markov network after conditioning on evidence and deleting irrelevant leaves or disconnected pieces. Source: transcript `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN8361PJE29V637Y0XFT9DQB/dGOW-bayes-nets-4.txt`, `[14:09]` to `[15:09]`.
- The module also previews the rest of the BN unit: probabilistic programming, HMMs, particle filtering, and learning. Source: transcript `[9:02]` to `[10:35]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Random variables.** The BN state space: `X1` through `Xn`. Source: transcript `[24:59]` to `[25:08]`.
- **Directed acyclic graph.** Qualitative dependency structure over the variables. Source: transcript `[25:08]` to `[25:15]`.
- **Local conditional distribution.** `p(xi | parents(xi))`, the quantitative piece attached to each node. Source: transcript `[25:21]` to `[25:37]`.
- **Joint distribution.** The product of all local conditionals. Source: transcript `[25:33]` to `[25:42]`.
- **Probabilistic database.** The BN as a store of uncertain facts you can query. Source: transcript `[26:01]` to `[26:16]`.
- **Evidence vs query variables.** Observed variables are conditioned on; query variables are what you want to know. Source: transcript `[1:36]` to `[2:07]`.
- **Conditioning on evidence.** Substitute observed values into the factors, then run inference on the remaining graph. Source: transcript `[5:00]` to `[6:47]`.
- **Probabilistic programming.** A language-level way to define BNs by writing the generative process directly. Source: transcript `[9:04]` to `[9:16]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- BNs are less about raw predictive fit and more about structure, missingness, and explicit prior knowledge. That is why they matter even in a deep-learning era. Source: transcript `[6:10]` to `[8:56]`.
- The same BN can be reinterpreted as a Markov network; before conditioning, the normalization constant is `1`, and after conditioning it becomes the probability of the evidence. Source: transcript `[3:37]` to `[4:13]`, `[6:03]` to `[6:36]`.
- The burglary/alarm/earthquake example is a warning label: evidence changes posterior belief, but that is not the same thing as a causal intervention. Source: transcript `[13:10]` to `[17:52]`.
- The practical trick is to turn inference in a BN into inference in a factor graph, then reuse whatever algorithm is already good for that graph. Source: transcript `[14:09]` to `[15:09]`.

## Connections
- To Markov networks: same factor-graph backbone, different semantics for the factors. Source: transcript `[0:57]` to `[1:44]`, `[3:37]` to `[4:13]`.
- To HMMs: chain-structured BNs are the substrate for forward-backward and particle filtering. Source: transcript `[9:44]` to `[10:05]`.
- To modern generative modeling: LDA, inverse graphics, and structured code all use the BN idea of latent causes generating observations. Source: transcript `[2:00]` to `[4:13]`.
- To causal models: BNs are an on-ramp, not the finish line. Source: transcript `[7:40]` to `[8:17]`.
