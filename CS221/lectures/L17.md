# L17: Search 1 - Dynamic Programming, Uniform Cost Search

## Summary
- The lecture formalizes search problems with a start state, actions, successor function, cost function, and goal test. Source: `[5:29]` to `[9:59]`.
- Brute-force backtracking is the baseline, but DFS and BFS only work under restrictive cost assumptions. Source: `[24:28]` to `[37:03]`.
- Dynamic programming works when the state space is a minimal sufficient summary of the past and the graph is acyclic. Source: `[1:02:16]` to `[1:12:47]`.
- Uniform cost search handles cycles by keeping frontier and explored sets and expanding states in increasing past cost. Source: `[1:13:00]` to `[1:20:16]`.
- The transport example shows the payoff of state design: the right state compression can shrink the problem from quadratic to linear-ish size. Source: `[1:02:16]` to `[1:11:37]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Search problem.** Initial state, actions, successor function, cost, and end-state predicate. Source: `[5:29]` to `[9:59]`.
- **Search tree.** The brute-force "what if" expansion of all possible action sequences. Source: `[12:04]` to `[12:23]`.
- **Backtracking search.** Recursive enumeration of the tree while tracking history and accumulated cost. Source: `[24:28]` to `[33:24]`.
- **DFS.** Safe only when every edge cost is zero, because the first solution is automatically optimal. Source: `[36:08]` to `[37:03]`.
- **BFS.** Safe only when all edge costs are equal. Source: `[37:03]` to `[37:28]`.
- **Dynamic programming.** Acyclic search plus memoization over a carefully chosen state space. Source: `[57:01]` to `[1:02:02]`.
- **State.** A summary of past actions sufficient to choose future actions optimally. Source: `[1:02:16]` to `[1:02:24]`.
- **Uniform cost search.** Dijkstra-style enumeration by increasing past cost, requiring non-negative edge costs. Source: `[1:13:00]` to `[1:15:27]`.
- **Frontier / explored.** Frontier means seen but unsettled; explored means the cheapest path is already known. Source: `[1:14:32]` to `[1:15:24]`.
- **Priority queue.** The data structure that makes UCS efficient in practice. Source: `[11:18]` to `[12:00]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- State design is the real algorithmic lever. The lecture spends as much time shrinking the state space as it does running search. Source: `[1:02:16]` to `[1:11:37]`.
- Dynamic programming is not magic; it is search with a good compression scheme and an order that prevents recomputation. Source: `[1:12:27]` to `[1:12:47]`.
- UCS can revisit states because it is trying to prove optimality under non-negative costs, not just find any path. Source: `[1:14:32]` to `[1:19:42]`.
- The frontier/explored split is a proof obligation, not just bookkeeping. Once a state moves to explored, the best path to it is locked in. Source: `[1:14:32]` to `[1:18:02]`.
- Adding a small amount of memory to the state can change the asymptotics completely. The odd-city examples show the difference between `n^2`, `2n`, and `3n`. Source: `[1:04:56]` to `[1:11:37]`.
