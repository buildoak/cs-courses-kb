# L15: Hidden Markov Models

Hidden Markov models are Bayesian networks with a time axis: hidden state evolves, noisy observations fall out, and inference becomes a dynamic-programming problem instead of a brute-force one.

## Summary
- HMMs are Bayesian networks for latent state over time: hidden positions generate noisy observations in the object-tracking example. Source: transcript `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN8361PJE29V637Y0XFT9DQB/N-ZPbp-forward-backward.txt`, `[0:06]` to `[1:38]`.
- Filtering asks for the current state given past evidence; smoothing also conditions on future evidence. Source: transcript `[1:41]` to `[4:23]`.
- Forward-backward solves smoothing exactly by representing all paths in a lattice and propagating forward and backward messages. Source: transcript `[4:23]` to `[16:26]`.
- The lattice compresses exponentially many assignments into a polynomial-size dynamic program, and shared subcomputations mean all smoothing queries come "for free" once the messages are built. Source: transcript `[5:01]` to `[5:35]` and `[13:23]` to `[13:45]`.
- When the state space is huge, particle filtering approximates filtering by proposing particles, reweighting them against evidence, and resampling. Source: transcript `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN8361PJE29V637Y0XFT9DQB/8sOt-bayes-nets-6.txt`, `[0:06]` to `[7:03]`, `[22:01]` to `[23:55]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Hidden state `H_i`.** The latent quantity you care about but do not observe directly. Source: transcript `[0:22]` to `[1:03]`.
- **Emission `E_i`.** The noisy observation generated from the hidden state. Source: transcript `[1:03]` to `[2:07]`.
- **Transition distribution.** How the hidden state moves from one time step to the next. Source: transcript `[1:12]` to `[1:37]`.
- **Filtering.** `P(H_i | past evidence)`. Source: transcript `[2:47]` to `[3:20]`.
- **Smoothing.** `P(H_i | all evidence)`. Source: transcript `[3:20]` to `[4:23]`.
- **Forward message `F_i`.** Sum of weights of paths from the start to a node. Source: transcript `[8:39]` to `[9:47]`.
- **Backward message `B_i`.** Sum of weights of paths from a node to the end. Source: transcript `[10:05]` to `[10:47]`.
- **Lattice.** A DAG representation of all assignments to the hidden states. Source: transcript `[4:23]` to `[5:35]`.
- **Particle.** A weighted sample used by particle filtering to approximate a filtering distribution. Source: transcript `[3:18]` to `[6:23]` in the particle-filter lecture.
- **Resampling.** The step that reallocates mass toward higher-weight particles. Source: transcript `[22:49]` to `[23:05]` in the particle-filter lecture.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The hard part is not defining the HMM; it is doing inference cheaply enough to matter. Forward-backward is exact for chain structure, particle filtering is approximate for scale. Source: transcript `[2:37]` to `[3:02]` and `[22:01]` to `[23:19]`.
- The lattice is the key compression trick: many paths, one DAG. That is what makes dynamic programming possible here. Source: transcript `[4:23]` to `[5:35]`.
- Forward-backward gives all smoothing answers at once because the same forward and backward computations are reused across queries. Source: transcript `[13:23]` to `[13:45]`.
- Particle filtering is basically beam search with a probabilistic interpretation and resampling to keep diversity. Source: transcript `[3:18]` to `[6:23]`, `[7:35]` to `[7:58]`, `[22:49]` to `[23:05]`.

## Connections
- To Bayesian networks: HMMs are a special case with chain structure. Source: transcript `[0:07]` to `[0:13]`.
- To Markov networks: once you build the lattice, the inference logic looks like factor-graph message passing and variable elimination. Source: transcript `[14:28]` to `[15:17]` and `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN8361PJE29V637Y0XFT9DQB/dGOW-bayes-nets-4.txt`, `[14:09]` to `[15:09]`.
- To robotics and tracking: the object-tracking example is the running mental model for the entire unit. Source: transcript `[0:17]` to `[0:41]`.
- To approximation methods: particle filtering generalizes beyond HMMs to larger factor graphs. Source: transcript `[23:36]` to `[23:55]`.
