# L06: Game Playing, Minimax, and Alpha-Beta Pruning

## Summary
- Games are turn-taking, fully observable, zero-sum problems. Utility is only assigned at terminal states.
- The core modeling move is to add a `player` function to the usual search skeleton, so the state knows whose turn it is.
- Expectimax assumes a stochastic opponent; minimax assumes an adversarial opponent who actively tries to lower your utility.
- Evaluation functions approximate minimax value when full tree search is too expensive.
- Alpha-beta pruning cuts away subtrees that cannot change the answer, and move ordering determines how much pruning you get.

## Key Concepts
- **Game tree.** The state-space tree of all legal move sequences.
- **Player function.** Tells you whose turn it is at a state.
- **Utility.** Terminal payoff for the agent. In zero-sum games the opponent gets the negation.
- **Policy.** A mapping from states to actions. The lecture distinguishes deterministic and stochastic policies.
- **Expectimax.** Choose the action with the highest expected utility when the opponent is random.
- **Minimax.** Choose the action that maximizes your utility assuming the opponent minimizes it.
- **Expectiminimax.** The extension with chance nodes, where randomness is explicit in the tree.
- **Evaluation function.** A cheap approximation to minimax value for non-terminal states.
- **Alpha-beta pruning.** Track lower and upper bounds on value and prune when the intervals no longer overlap.
- **Move ordering.** Better ordering means more pruning and a deeper effective search.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Zero-sum is the reason minimax works cleanly. Opponent utility is just the negative of agent utility.
- Expectimax and minimax are the same tree with different aggregation rules. The structure does not change; the semantics do.
- Evaluation functions are game heuristics. They play the same role that heuristics play in search.
- Alpha-beta pruning is interval reasoning. It does not change the objective, only the amount of tree you need to inspect.
- The lecture's pruning examples show that order matters a lot. Good ordering can cut the tree dramatically; bad ordering can leave you with full exponential search.
- If the opponent is actually random, minimax is the wrong model. Expectimax is the better abstraction.
