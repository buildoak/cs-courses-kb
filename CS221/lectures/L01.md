# L01 CS221 Overview and the Modeling Stack

CS221 opens by telling you what kind of course this is: less "cover a syllabus" and more "turn messy problems into models you can actually reason about."

## Summary
- The lecture starts with course mechanics: pre-recorded modules, faculty chats, and a flipped format that buys live time back for discussion. Source: `[2:01]` to `[5:26]`.
- The course's core loop is modeling, inference, and learning. Modeling is explicitly lossy; learning fills in unknown parameters from data. Source: `[28:18]` to `[31:34]`.
- CS221 is organized as a ladder of model classes: reflex-based, state-based, variable-based, and logic. Source: `[33:49]` to `[46:53]`.
- The state-based block covers search problems, Markov decision processes, and adversarial games; the variable-based block covers constraint satisfaction problems and Bayesian networks. Source: `[35:33]` to `[41:25]`.
- The history segment frames AI as three traditions that keep reappearing in different forms: symbolic, neural, and statistical. Source: `[49:19]` to `[1:07:44]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Modules.** The lecture format is broken into 10-20 minute pre-recorded chunks, released weekly. Source: `[3:25]` to `[4:19]`.
- **Modeling.** Real problems get compressed into formal structures, usually graph-like or variable-like, because the world is too messy to keep intact. Source: `[28:56]` to `[29:47]`.
- **Inference.** Once the model exists, inference asks questions over it: shortest path, scenic path, prediction, or policy choice. Source: `[29:53]` to `[30:26]`.
- **Learning.** Learning completes an incomplete model by estimating unknown parameters from data. Source: `[30:28]` to `[31:34]`.
- **Reflex-based models.** These are feedforward models with almost no reasoning: linear classifiers and deep neural networks live here. Source: `[33:49]` to `[35:28]`.
- **State-based models.** These capture sequential structure: chess, routing, robotics, natural language generation, and machine translation. Source: `[35:33]` to `[37:09]`.
- **Search problems.** State plus action plus transition is the first state-based abstraction; the course treats this as controllable reasoning. Source: `[37:11]` to `[37:23]`.
- **Markov decision processes.** Search with uncertainty; nature supplies probabilities and the agent plans through them. Source: `[37:21]` to `[37:44]`.
- **Adversarial games.** Another state-based model, but now the opponent is strategic rather than random. Source: `[37:46]` to `[38:01]`.
- **Variable-based models.** These remove ordering and let constraints or probabilistic relations do the work. Source: `[39:03]` to `[41:17]`.
- **Constraint satisfaction problems.** Sudoku and scheduling show the hard-constraint version of variable-based modeling. Source: `[40:10]` to `[40:32]`.
- **Bayesian networks.** These soften hard constraints into probabilistic dependencies for noisy worlds like tracking cars or airplanes. Source: `[40:34]` to `[41:25]`.
- **Logic.** The virtual-assistant demo shows facts, rules, contradiction, and query answering over heterogeneous symbolic input. Source: `[41:32]` to `[46:53]`.
- **Turing test.** The history segment treats Turing's question as objective specification: define the what before the how. Source: `[49:19]` to `[51:25]`.
- **Symbolic, neural, statistical AI.** The lecture uses these as the three historical lenses that shape modern AI. Source: `[52:12]` to `[1:07:22]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The course is organized around representation, not just algorithms. Search, CSPs, MDPs, and logic are different answers to the same question: what structure survives the model cut? Source: `[28:56]` to `[31:34]`, `[35:33]` to `[41:25]`.
- Learning is framed as repair. You start with a skeleton model and use data to fill in the unknowns. Source: `[30:28]` to `[31:34]`.
- The Turing test matters here less as a benchmark and more as a clean spec. The point is to separate objective from implementation. Source: `[50:28]` to `[51:22]`.
- The history section is not a victory lap for one paradigm. It is a repeated pattern of ambition, overreach, winter, and partial recovery across symbolic, neural, and statistical AI. Source: `[52:12]` to `[1:07:22]`.
- The logic demo is doing more than showing off. It marks a category boundary: symbolic systems can ingest facts and rules in a way that is very different from image-based training. Source: `[43:10]` to `[46:17]`.
