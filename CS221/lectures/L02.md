# L02 A Brief History of AI

This module compresses AI into three overlapping stories: symbolic AI, neural AI, and statistical AI. The point is not to crown one. The point is to show how they keep reappearing inside each other.

## Summary
- The lecture starts with Turing's 1950 paper and treats the imitation game as a way to define intelligence without locking in the implementation. Source: `[0:25]` to `[2:33]`.
- Symbolic AI emerges through the Dartmouth workshop, early theorem proving, and General Problem Solver, then runs into the first AI winter. Source: `[2:48]` to `[6:10]`.
- The second symbolic wave, expert systems, buys real applications but collapses under brittle rules and real-world complexity. Source: `[6:19]` to `[8:32]`.
- Neural AI runs from McCulloch-Pitts through perceptrons, backpropagation, LeCun's digit recognizer, and AlexNet. Source: `[8:44]` to `[12:32]`.
- Statistical AI is the bridge that makes modern AI feel mathematically clean: least squares, Dijkstra, Bellman, Bayesian networks, and SVMs. Source: `[13:41]` to `[17:22]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Turing test.** The course uses it as an objective specification: define the what, not the how. Source: `[0:33]` to `[2:00]`.
- **Symbolic AI.** The top-down approach: logic, rules, theorem proving, and general problem solving. Source: `[2:12]` to `[6:10]`.
- **First AI winter.** AI overpromised on logic-heavy problems, but search exploded and hardware was too weak. Source: `[5:34]` to `[6:10]`.
- **Knowledge-based systems.** The 1970s and 1980s pushed rules and expert systems as a practical fix. Source: `[6:43]` to `[8:32]`.
- **Neural AI.** The bottom-up path starts with simple neurons and eventually becomes deep learning. Source: `[8:44]` to `[12:32]`.
- **McCulloch-Pitts neuron.** The first neural network paper is framed as a logical model as much as a biological one. Source: `[8:55]` to `[9:11]`.
- **Perceptron.** Rosenblatt's algorithm is the first learning rule that actually works well for a single layer. Source: `[9:21]` to `[9:36]`.
- **Backpropagation.** Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams make multilayer training practical again. Source: `[10:38]` to `[11:01]`.
- **AlexNet.** ImageNet-era deep learning lands the decisive blow by making convolutional networks useful at scale. Source: `[11:45]` to `[12:32]`.
- **Statistical AI.** The lecture pulls in statistics, algorithms, and control theory to give AI its mathematical vocabulary. Source: `[13:41]` to `[17:22]`.
- **Bayesian networks.** Pearl's framework makes uncertainty explicit instead of pretending it away. Source: `[15:31]` to `[15:44]`.
- **SVMs.** The pre-deep-learning machine learning workhorse: easier to tune than neural nets and good enough to dominate for a while. Source: `[15:44]` to `[16:00]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The history is not a straight line. It is a cycle of ambitious formalism, practical collapse, narrow recovery, and then a new synthesis. Source: `[2:48]` to `[17:22]`.
- Symbolic AI did fail, but not because the questions were bad. It failed because search spaces were too large and knowledge was too hard to encode. Source: `[5:34]` to `[6:10]`.
- Neural AI's early criticisms do not kill deep learning. They mostly kill the shallow version. Source: `[9:54]` to `[10:28]`.
- Statistical AI matters because it gives AI its current operating language: objectives, optimization, uncertainty, and complexity. Source: `[13:41]` to `[17:22]`.
- The lecture keeps implying a synthesis: modern AI is not "neural wins." It is a hybrid stack that borrows from all three histories. Source: `[13:01]` to `[17:22]`.
