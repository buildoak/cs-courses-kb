# L12: Backpropagation

## Summary
- Backpropagation is introduced as a general way to compute gradients automatically on computation graphs, not just for neural networks. Source: `[0:05]` to `[3:14]`.
- The lecture builds a small derivative cheat sheet for sum, product, square, max, and sigmoid, then shows that chain rule is just the product of local edge gradients. Source: `[3:27]` to `[8:25]`.
- The hinge-loss gradient for linear classification drops out of the graph cleanly: active margin indicator, negative sign, label, and feature vector. Source: `[10:04]` to `[13:04]`.
- The same machinery computes gradients for two-layer neural nets, including the gradient with respect to `V`, which reuses forward activations and the sigmoid derivative `h(1-h)`. Source: `[13:46]` to `[19:08]`.
- The second half of the lecture is about optimization reality: forward pass, backward pass, non-convexity, initialization, overparameterization, adaptive step sizes, and vanishing or exploding gradients. Source: `[19:15]` to `[30:40]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Computation graph.** A DAG of subexpressions whose root is the final loss. Source: `[2:10]` to `[3:14]`.
- **Forward pass.** Evaluate the graph bottom-up and store the value at every node. Source: `[20:19]` to `[21:55]`.
- **Backward pass.** Compute gradients top-down, one backward value per node. Source: `[21:59]` to `[24:08]`.
- **Chain rule.** Gradients along a path multiply. Source: `[8:41]` to `[9:49]`.
- **Hinge loss.** `max(1 - margin, 0)` as the linear-classification loss. Source: `[10:04]` to `[13:04]`.
- **Backpropagation.** The general algorithm that applies local derivative rules to a computation graph. Source: `[19:39]` to `[20:01]`.
- **Non-convex optimization.** Neural-network training has no global-minimum guarantee the way convex linear problems do. Source: `[24:18]` to `[25:49]`.
- **Initialization.** Random noise around zero is used to break symmetry; zero initialization is bad. Source: `[26:24]` to `[27:05]`.
- **Overparameterization.** More hidden units can make training easier by giving the model more chances to fit useful structure. Source: `[27:10]` to `[27:47]`.
- **Adaptive step sizes.** AdaGrad and Adam assign per-parameter step sizes instead of one global `eta`. Source: `[27:49]` to `[28:17]`.
- **Vanishing / exploding gradients.** Too-small gradients stall training; too-large gradients blow it up. Source: `[28:22]` to `[29:11]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Backprop is local. Once the forward values are cached, the backward pass only needs the derivative on each edge. Source: `[22:45]` to `[23:56]`.
- The "cheat sheet" is the core abstraction. Sum, product, square, max, and sigmoid are enough to build much larger gradient computations. Source: `[6:04]` to `[8:25]`.
- The graph view makes shared subexpressions visible. That matters because the same forward values get reused when you differentiate a deeper model. Source: `[17:09]` to `[17:28]`.
- The hard part of training is not just calculus. Initialization, step size choice, and architecture choices often matter as much as the gradient formula. Source: `[24:18]` to `[29:11]`.
- The lecture treats differentiation as infrastructure. Once the graph exists, new models and new losses become much cheaper to iterate on. Source: `[29:22]` to `[30:36]`.
