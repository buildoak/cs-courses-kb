# L11: Neural Networks

## Summary
- The lecture uses the two-car collision toy problem to show why linear predictors fail on XOR-shaped data. Source: `[1:08]` to `[3:04]`.
- The fix is problem decomposition: define `h1` and `h2` as two simpler tests, then predict safe if either one is true. Source: `[3:11]` to `[4:51]`.
- Those subproblems get stacked into a hidden vector `h(x)`, and the final prediction is still linear on top of that learned representation. Source: `[5:16]` to `[7:07]`.
- Threshold units have useless gradients, so the lecture switches to smoother activation functions, especially sigmoid historically and ReLU in practice. Source: `[7:38]` to `[10:10]`.
- The same pattern generalizes to deeper networks: repeated matrix multiply plus nonlinearity gives multiple levels of abstraction and multiple steps of computation. Source: `[10:21]` to `[18:28]`.

## Key Concepts
- **XOR problem.** A non-linearly separable pattern that a single line cannot solve. Source: `[2:51]` to `[3:04]`.
- **Problem decomposition.** Split a hard prediction task into simpler intermediate tests. Source: `[3:11]` to `[4:08]`.
- **Hidden units `h1` and `h2`.** Intermediate detectors for the two half-spaces in the collision example. Source: `[3:21]` to `[4:51]`.
- **Matrix form.** The two hidden units are stacked into a weight matrix `V`, then combined with a final vector `w`. Source: `[5:16]` to `[7:07]`.
- **Activation function.** A nonlinearity applied after each matrix multiply. Source: `[7:38]` to `[10:10]`.
- **ReLU.** The activation that won because it keeps gradients usable in most of the range. Source: `[9:14]` to `[10:10]`.
- **Feature learning.** Neural networks learn a better intermediate representation instead of relying on a fixed `phi(x)`. Source: `[11:34]` to `[12:04]`.
- **Depth.** More layers mean more repeated compute and more levels of abstraction. Source: `[13:39]` to `[18:11]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The network is still linear at the top. The main change is that the representation feeding that top layer is learned instead of hand-built. Source: `[11:39]` to `[12:04]`.
- The lecture is not just about "more nonlinear models." It is about learning a representation that makes the final linear decision easy. Source: `[10:47]` to `[12:04]`.
- The abstraction story is useful, but the lecture is careful: edge/part/object layers are a story, not a universal law. Source: `[14:45]` to `[15:29]`.
- Depth is framed two ways at once: as hierarchical abstraction and as extra steps of computation. Source: `[15:42]` to `[16:43]`.
- Theoretical justification is still incomplete. The lecture explicitly says the jury is still out on why depth works as well as it does. Source: `[16:54]` to `[17:17]`.
