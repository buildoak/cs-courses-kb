# L13: Probabilistic Graphical Models

Markov networks are the cleanest bridge from factor graphs to uncertainty: keep the graph, normalize the weights, and the model becomes a distribution instead of a max-weight puzzle.

## Summary
- Markov networks turn factor graphs into probability by normalizing assignment weights with the partition function `Z`. Source: transcript `[2:23]` to `[4:19]`.
- Marginals answer a different question than max-weight search; the lecture's `x2` example shows the most likely marginal value can differ from the best joint assignment. Source: transcript `[4:48]` to `[7:55]`.
- The Ising model shows the same machinery in statistical physics: neighboring sites prefer matching spins, and `beta` controls how strongly they stick together. Source: transcript `[8:09]` to `[10:42]`.
- Image denoising is the computer-vision version: observed pixels become hard constraints, neighboring pixels prefer agreement. Source: transcript `[10:51]` to `[12:35]`.
- When exact marginals are hard, Gibbs sampling approximates them by resampling one variable at a time from the local conditional and averaging the samples. Source: transcript `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN8361PJE29V637Y0XFT9DQB/k6a-markov-networks-2.txt`, `[1:56]` to `[6:13]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Factor graph.** Variables plus factors; each factor takes a subset of variables and returns a non-negative weight. Source: transcript `[0:36]` to `[0:52]`.
- **Markov network / Markov random field.** A factor graph interpreted as a joint distribution over random variables. Source: transcript `[2:38]` to `[2:47]`.
- **Partition function `Z`.** The sum of all assignment weights; the normalization constant that turns weights into probabilities. Source: transcript `[3:03]` to `[3:31]`.
- **Marginal probability.** The probability that one variable takes a value after summing over all full assignments consistent with it. Source: transcript `[5:11]` to `[5:42]`.
- **Maximum weight assignment.** The CSP-style answer: pick the single best assignment instead of reasoning over the whole distribution. Source: transcript `[1:42]` to `[2:02]`, `[13:35]` to `[13:58]`.
- **Ising model.** A canonical pairwise Markov network where neighboring spins want to agree. Source: transcript `[8:09]` to `[10:03]`.
- **Gibbs sampling.** Approximate inference by repeatedly resampling one variable from its conditional given the rest. Source: transcript `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN8361PJE29V637Y0XFT9DQB/k6a-markov-networks-2.txt`, `[2:12]` to `[6:13]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The normalization step is the whole move. Once weights become probabilities, the question changes from "which assignment wins?" to "what does the whole distribution say?" Source: transcript `[3:03]` to `[4:19]`.
- The `x2` example is the sharpest point in the lecture: the best joint assignment and the most likely marginal value are not the same thing. Source: transcript `[6:46]` to `[7:55]`.
- Gibbs sampling is not optimization in disguise. It is a Monte Carlo estimator for marginals, and the samples themselves are just a means to the estimate. Source: transcript `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN8361PJE29V637Y0XFT9DQB/k6a-markov-networks-2.txt`, `[5:32]` to `[6:13]`, `[16:57]` to `[17:28]`.
- Markov networks are the probabilistic version of CSPs, so the modeling change is smaller than it looks. The graph stays; the question changes. Source: transcript `[13:13]` to `[13:58]`.

## Connections
- To CSPs: same factor graph scaffold, but CSPs seek maximum weight assignments while Markov networks ask distributional questions. Source: transcript `[13:13]` to `[13:58]`.
- To Bayesian networks: the next unit reuses factor-graph machinery, but the factors become directed local conditionals. Source: transcript `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN8361PJE29V637Y0XFT9DQB/fA7z-bayes-nets-1.txt`, `[0:31]` to `[1:44]`.
- To HMMs: object tracking is the bridge from static factor graphs to time-series inference. Source: transcript `[0:58]` to `[1:42]` and `/tmp/agent-mux-501/01KN8361PJE29V637Y0XFT9DQB/N-ZPbp-forward-backward.txt`, `[0:17]` to `[1:38]`.
