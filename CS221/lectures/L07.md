# L07: Stochastic Gradient Descent

## Summary
- SGD replaces one expensive full-data gradient with many cheap per-example updates. Source: `[0:05]` to `[2:41]`.
- The step size `eta` is a real control knob: small values are stable, large values move faster but can bounce around. Source: `[2:44]` to `[4:07]`.
- The code demo builds a million-example synthetic regression problem so the lecture can compare gradient descent and SGD on the same ground truth. Source: `[5:17]` to `[8:26]`.
- Switching from batch gradients to per-example losses makes the optimizer interface more granular, not just the training loop. Source: `[8:26]` to `[12:25]`.
- The punchline is blunt: SGD wins here because it makes progress with more updates, not because each update is cleaner. Source: `[13:34]` to `[14:55]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Training loss.** Average over the per-example losses on the training set. Source: `[0:23]` to `[0:30]`.
- **Gradient descent.** One update per full pass through the whole training set, using the gradient of the average training loss. Source: `[0:33]` to `[1:28]`.
- **Stochastic gradient descent.** Update after each example instead of waiting for the full gradient. Source: `[1:35]` to `[2:41]`.
- **Epoch.** One pass over the training examples. Source: `[1:47]` to `[2:17]`.
- **Step size / eta.** Hyperparameter that controls update magnitude; the lecture contrasts constant `eta = 0.1` with decreasing schedules like `1 / number_of_updates`. Source: `[2:44]` to `[4:07]`.
- **Synthetic verification.** Generate data from a hidden `trueW`, then see whether optimization recovers it. Source: `[5:17]` to `[6:24]`.
- **Per-example objective API.** SGD needs `loss(w, i)` and `grad_loss(w, i)`, not just a global objective and its gradient. Source: `[8:26]` to `[9:43]`.
- **Learning-rate decay.** Starting aggressive and slowing down near the optimum is presented as the practical schedule intuition. Source: `[10:29]` to `[12:32]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The bottleneck in batch gradient descent is not the math. It is the number of passes over the data required for one update. Source: `[1:05]` to `[1:28]`.
- SGD is not a lower-quality version of gradient descent. It is a different tradeoff: noisier updates, far more of them. Source: `[2:26]` to `[2:41]`.
- The lecture uses synthetic data as a unit test for the optimizer. That is the clean way to verify recovery when the real target is hidden. Source: `[5:17]` to `[6:24]`.
- The step size schedule is part of the algorithm's behavior, not a cosmetic tuning detail. Source: `[3:41]` to `[4:07]`, `[10:29]` to `[12:32]`.
- The lecture's summary is the useful one: optimize the objective, not the gradient computation itself. Source: `[14:26]` to `[14:55]`.

## Connections
- This is the optimization engine underneath the earlier regression and classification objectives.
- `L08` uses the same gradient-descent machinery, but the objective is a maximum over groups, so the update rule changes.
- The same per-example update pattern is what later becomes minibatch training in larger neural systems.
- The lecture's synthetic-data trick is the same move you use when you want to test an implementation against a known answer.
