# CS330 L07: Contrastive Learning for Unsupervised Pre-Training

## Summary
- Contrastive learning learns a representation by pulling together semantically related examples and pushing apart unrelated ones.
- The lecture moves from triplet loss to SimCLR's multi-negative softmax loss, which is the cleaner practical version.
- Augmentations are not decoration; they define the task. If the positive-pair rule is wrong, the representation is wrong.
- Batch size matters because the negative sum sits inside a log. Small batches change the objective, not just the gradient noise.
- The lecture ends by showing that contrastive learning and metric meta-learning are much closer than their names suggest.

## Key Concepts
- **Anchor / positive / negative.** The anchor is the reference example, the positive should stay close, and the negative should move away.
- **Triplet loss.** The simplest contrastive objective: pull anchor-positive together and push anchor-negative apart with a margin.
- **Hinge margin.** The loss stops rewarding larger separation once the negative is far enough away.
- **NT-Xent / SimCLR loss.** A softmax over many negatives that turns the contrastive objective into a probability.
- **Collapse failure mode.** If you only pull positives together, the model can map everything to one constant vector.
- **Batch-size dependence.** Jensen's inequality explains why subsampling negatives gives a looser objective.
- **MoCo / BYOL.** Two ways to reduce or remove the need for huge in-batch negative sets.
- **Task definition by pairing.** Nearby patches, augmentations, adjacent video frames, and paired text/image examples all count.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Contrastive learning is mostly a rule for inventing supervision. The hard part is not the loss; it is deciding what should count as "same."
- The batch-size issue is structural. Small batches do not just make optimization noisier. They change the loss approximation.
- SimCLR and prototypical networks are close cousins. One compares everything in the batch; the other compares query examples to support prototypes.
- Augmentations are domain knowledge in disguise. Bad augmentations can make the model learn the wrong invariances.
- The method is broader than vision. Temporal adjacency, learned augmentations, and image-text alignment are all the same pattern with different pair constructors.
