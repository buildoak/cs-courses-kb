# CS330 L20: Frontiers: Memorization, Unsupervised Meta-Learning, Open Problems

## Summary
- The lecture opens with the memorization problem: meta-learning can get the training tasks right by storing task-specific facts in `Theta` and ignoring the task data. The fix is to control information flow with meta-regularization. Source: `[11:38]` to `[17:53]`.
- When task boundaries are missing, the lecture moves to streaming data. `MOCA` uses Bayesian online changepoint detection to segment a time series and meta-learn across the inferred tasks. Source: `[21:08]` to `[26:31]`.
- For unlabeled images, `CACTUs` clusters unsupervised embeddings to invent tasks. Meta-learning on top of those synthetic tasks beats raw unsupervised features and plain downstream classifiers. Source: `[27:24]` to `[35:21]`.
- The RL analogue is unsupervised meta-RL. `DIAYN` creates diverse skills, then meta-learns across them; `Watch, Try, Learn` adds demonstrations plus trials so sparse-reward manipulation is not pure guesswork. Source: `[35:32]` to `[52:53]`.
- At scale, the bottleneck is often optimization, not data or exploration. `Meta-World` exposes gradient interference, and `PCGrad` helps by projecting conflicting gradients away from each other. Source: `[53:10]` to `[1:04:01]`.
- The lecture ends where the real work is: distribution shift between meta-train and meta-test, multimodal priors, better benchmarks, and sequential task settings that current algorithms barely touch. Source: `[1:07:06]` to `[1:13:06]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The memorization failure is really an information-routing failure. If the model can stash the answer in `Theta`, the task data becomes optional. Source: `[11:38]` to `[15:30]`.
- Naive entropy tricks are not enough. The lecture warns that a model can hide information and still look "task agnostic" unless you constrain the right pathway. Source: `[18:15]` to `[20:44]`.
- Segmentation is part of the learning problem, not a preprocessing assumption. `MOCA` learns change points and adaptation together. Source: `[22:47]` to `[26:31]`.
- `CACTUs` shows that semantic labels are not required to make useful meta-training tasks. Clustered structure is enough if the embedding is good enough. Source: `[27:39]` to `[35:21]`.
- In unsupervised meta-RL, random tasks already help, but explicit skill diversity helps more. The lecture treats that as evidence that task proposal matters. Source: `[36:29]` to `[43:55]`.
- `Watch, Try, Learn` works because the demo says what task exists and the trial supplies the missing correction signal. Either half alone is weaker. Source: `[46:48]` to `[52:53]`.
- The Meta-World result is an optimization story, not a data story. Independent single-task training can beat joint training when gradients fight. Source: `[55:22]` to `[58:05]`.

## Connections
- `L19`: both lectures infer hidden task identity, but `L19` learns it from reward traces and `L20` learns it from structure in the data itself.
- `Bayesian meta-learning`: `MOCA` and the POMDP-style view both treat task inference as posterior updating.
- `Lifelong learning`: the no-task-boundaries setting is the streaming version of the same problem.
- `Domain generalization`: the closing open problems are the same distribution-shift question, just moved from domains to tasks.
- `Benchmark design`: `Meta-World` plays the same role for meta-RL that `Meta-Dataset` and `Mini-ImageNet` play for few-shot vision.
