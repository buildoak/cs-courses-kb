# CS330 L19: Meta-RL, Learning to Explore

## Summary
- The lecture frames meta-RL as two coupled jobs: adapt to a new MDP and decide what experience to collect while adapting. In RL, the agent has to choose its own evidence; that is the extra problem beyond supervised few-shot learning. Source: `[2:44]` to `[4:55]`.
- Two on-policy meta-RL baselines set the tradeoff. `RL^2` uses an RNN policy so the hidden state becomes the learner; `MAML` keeps adaptation as gradient descent, which is cleaner but less expressive. Source: `[5:02]` to `[9:57]`.
- Sparse reward is the fault line. Zero reward can still carry task information, but plain policy gradients do not use that information well, so exploration has to become part of the learning problem. Source: `[9:13]` to `[11:54]`.
- `MAESN` makes exploration temporally coherent by sampling a latent `z` once per episode and holding it fixed. That is the lecture's first concrete answer to "how do we explore on purpose?" Source: `[17:54]` to `[23:24]`.
- The second half rewrites meta-RL as a POMDP over hidden task identity. Posterior sampling plus variational inference gives `PEARL`, which conditions on context rewards and transitions, separates on-policy adaptation data from off-policy actor-critic training, and buys a big sample-efficiency win. Source: `[27:35]` to `[47:08]`.
- The lecture does not pretend the problem is closed. Off-policy meta-RL is still unstable, and learning truly optimal exploration trajectories remains open. Source: `[52:15]` to `[1:07:14]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Sparse reward is not just "hard"; it is information that vanilla policy gradients often throw away. A zero-reward trajectory can still tell you a lot about the task. Source: `[9:13]` to `[10:00]`, `[30:39]` to `[31:00]`.
- `RL^2` and `MAML` are opposite tradeoffs. The recurrent version is expressive but inconsistent; the gradient-based version is more principled but can miss exploration structure. Source: `[7:11]` to `[9:57]`.
- `MAESN` works because it makes exploration coherent over time, not because it adds more randomness. Holding `z` fixed turns noise into a strategy. Source: `[18:04]` to `[23:24]`.
- The POMDP framing is the real pivot. Once task identity becomes a latent state, exploration turns into belief update and action selection under uncertainty. Source: `[27:35]` to `[32:35]`.
- `PEARL` wins by separating concerns: infer the task from context, then let the actor-critic train off-policy on the rest. That separation is where the sample-efficiency comes from. Source: `[44:42]` to `[47:08]`.
- The same separation limits exploration quality. `PEARL` is good at inferring tasks, but it is not built to discover optimal exploration trajectories from scratch. Source: `[53:20]` to `[54:59]`.

## Connections
- `Bayesian meta-learning`: both are posterior-updating stories, but here the posterior is used to pick actions, not just predictions.
- `Lifelong learning`: this is the same hidden-state problem in sequential form, except the latent variable is a task and the actions change the evidence.
- `Model-based RL`: the POMDP view is the bridge between task inference and control.
- `Open problems`: the lecture ends by saying the obvious thing out loud, which is that off-policy meta-RL is still not solved.
