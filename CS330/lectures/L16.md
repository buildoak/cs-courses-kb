# CS330 L16: Towards Understanding Transfer Learning and Its Limits

## Summary
- The lecture argues that transfer learning needs a real science, not just recipes for fine-tuning.
- Feature reuse matters, but it is not the whole story. Low-level statistics and the loss landscape also shape transfer.
- Fine-tuned models often live in the same basin, while randomly initialized models can land in different basins even when they solve the same downstream task.
- Module criticality is the quiet twist: lower layers are more general, higher layers are more fragile, and transfer often breaks in the top of the network.
- Scaling pretraining helps, but downstream gains saturate. After that, data diversity, data curation, and head tuning start to matter more.

## Key Concepts
- **Transfer learning.** Reusing a pretrained model on a new task or domain.
- **Feature reuse.** Downstream gains often come from reusing pretrained representations.
- **Low-level statistics.** Shared input statistics can still help transfer even when the target domain looks very different.
- **Performance barrier.** Linear interpolation between two solutions can reveal whether they sit in the same basin.
- **Model soups.** Averaging fine-tuned models can improve accuracy without extra inference cost.
- **Module criticality.** Some layers matter much more than others when you perturb them.
- **Downstream saturation.** Bigger upstream models do not buy unlimited transfer gains.
- **Data diversity.** More varied pretraining data can matter more than more scale.
- **Head hyperparameters.** The classifier head can dominate transfer performance on some benchmarks.
- **Benchmark design.** Good benchmarks have to expose the actual failure modes, not just reward scale.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture does not treat transfer as a binary yes/no question. It treats it as a geometry problem in weight space.
- Fine-tuned models can be interpolated smoothly because they often share a basin, while random-init models usually do not.
- Scrambling downstream image blocks can hurt feature reuse, but it does not erase all transfer value. Something lower-level still survives.
- The network is not uniformly transferable. Lower layers tend to encode broader structure, while upper layers become more specialized and more sensitive.
- Scaling can improve both optimization speed and final accuracy, but those are not the same effect. The curves saturate differently.
- Model soups are interesting because they are cheap at inference and still recover some of the benefit of ensembling.
- The practical bottleneck shifts over time: first architecture, then scale, then data curation, then task-specific tuning.
