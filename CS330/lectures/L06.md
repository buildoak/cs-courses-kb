# L06: Non-Parametric Few-Shot Learning

This lecture removes the inner gradient loop entirely and asks a simpler question: can we learn a metric so that nearest-neighbor style prediction works well at test time? Source: `[3:05]` to `[4:18]`.

## Summary
- Non-parametric few-shot learning uses learned embeddings plus nearest-neighbor or prototype comparisons instead of explicit task-specific parameters. Source: `[3:05]` to `[4:18]`, `[22:16]` to `[22:43]`.
- The lecture pushes for train/test alignment: soft comparisons during training should match the way the model will predict at test time. Source: `[13:41]` to `[15:32]`, `[21:39]` to `[22:17]`.
- Siamese networks, Matching Networks, Prototypical Networks, and Relation Networks are presented as one family with increasing flexibility. Source: `[8:53]` to `[10:13]`, `[17:47]` to `[18:24]`, `[25:24]` to `[35:45]`.
- The practical upside is speed and simplicity for classification; the downside is limited scope, scaling pain with large support sets, and weaker behavior when K varies. Source: `[1:01:52]` to `[1:03:42]`.
- The case study shows the method in production: Code in Place feedback became a prototypical-network problem once the rubric items were treated as tasks. Source: `[36:33]` to `[47:02]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Non-parametric.** At meta test time, the model does not infer task-specific parameters; it compares examples directly in an embedding space. Source: `[22:16]` to `[22:43]`.
- **Metric learning.** The real learning problem is how to compare inputs, not just how to classify them once the metric exists. Source: `[4:58]` to `[8:18]`.
- **Siamese network.** A pairwise classifier that predicts whether two examples belong to the same class. Source: `[8:53]` to `[10:13]`.
- **Matching Networks.** A soft nearest-neighbor classifier that compares a query example to all support examples with differentiable attention. Source: `[13:41]` to `[18:24]`.
- **Prototypical Networks.** Build one prototype per class by averaging embeddings, then classify by distance to prototypes. Source: `[25:24]` to `[29:18]`.
- **Relation Networks.** Learn the similarity function itself rather than using a fixed distance metric. Source: `[31:03]` to `[35:45]`.
- **Consistency.** The lecture treats monotonic improvement with more data as a stronger property than generic generalization. Source: `[58:21]` to `[59:56]`.
- **Task augmentation.** Self-supervised auxiliary tasks and side information make the method work in harder real-world settings. Source: `[43:04]` to `[45:01]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Matching train-time and test-time computation is the hidden theme. If inference is nearest-neighbor classification, training should look like a softened version of that, not something unrelated. Source: `[13:41]` to `[15:32]`, `[21:39]` to `[22:17]`.
- Prototypes are a compression scheme. They turn many support examples into one class representative, which is why the method is fast enough to be practical. Source: `[25:24]` to `[29:18]`.
- The lecture's "non-parametric" label does not mean "no learning." The metric, encoder, and auxiliary tasks are all learned; only the final per-task adaptation step is absent. Source: `[8:07]` to `[8:18]`, `[22:16]` to `[22:43]`.
- These methods are attractive because they avoid bilevel optimization, but that advantage only holds cleanly for classification with moderate support sizes. Source: `[1:02:28]` to `[1:03:42]`.
- The Code in Place system works because it layers three things on top of ProtoNets: self-supervised tasks, side information, and code pretraining. Source: `[43:04]` to `[45:01]`.
- Consistency is stronger than generalization. If the embedding collapses distinctions, more support data can stop helping. Source: `[58:21]` to `[1:01:02]`.

## Connections
- **To L04.** Black-box meta-learning learns a support-set encoder; non-parametric methods skip the task-state encoder and keep the support set visible as data. Source: `[54:47]` to `[57:04]`.
- **To L05.** MAML and other optimization-based methods keep explicit task adaptation, while ProtoNets trade that for simpler inference. Source: `[1:00:01]` to `[1:03:42]`.
- **To A2.** ProtoNets are the direct conceptual basis for the Prototypical Networks homework. Source: `[25:24]` to `[29:18]`, `[42:12]` to `[45:01]`.
- **To real deployments.** The education case study shows that a metric-learning model can grade open-ended code when the task is reframed carefully. Source: `[36:33]` to `[47:02]`.
- **To the broader course.** The same support/query vocabulary survives across black-box, optimization-based, and non-parametric methods. Source: `[1:14]` to `[2:40]`.
