# L03 Transfer Learning, Meta-Learning

Lecture 3 moves from multitask sharing to sequential adaptation: use source tasks to build a better prior, then learn the next task faster.

## Summary
- Transfer learning uses a source task to initialize a target task. Meta-learning generalizes that by optimizing transferability across a set of source tasks instead of one. Source: `[44:24]` to `[45:39]`, `[1:17:15]` to `[1:17:38]`.
- Common fine-tuning wisdom is not stable law. The lecture shows two recent results: unsupervised pretraining on the target corpus can nearly match a much larger corpus, and the best layer to fine-tune can depend on the shift. Source: `[31:34]` to `[37:05]`.
- The lecture's main meta-learning intuition is explicit optimization for fast adaptation, not just hoping pretraining helps. Source: `[44:24]` to `[45:39]`.
- The probabilistic view says meta-learning is about recovering shared latent structure `theta` that generates task-specific parameters `phi`. Source: `[47:15]` to `[1:09:14]`.
- The mechanistic view says meta-learning is a model that maps a small dataset to parameters for a new task, trained on meta-train tasks and tested on meta-test tasks. Source: `[1:12:32]` to `[1:16:09]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Transfer learning.** Learn one task, then adapt to another with prior knowledge from the source task. Source: `[24:46]` to `[24:59]`.
- **Fine-tuning.** Initialize from pretrained weights and continue optimizing on the target task. Source: `[24:28]` to `[25:08]`.
- **Unsupervised pretraining.** Pretrain on the target data with an unsupervised objective before fine-tuning. Source: `[32:32]` to `[34:06]`.
- **Layer-wise adaptation.** Different shifts may benefit from tuning the first layer, middle layers, or the whole network. Source: `[35:18]` to `[37:05]`.
- **Meta-learning.** Learn how to learn quickly from a family of tasks. Source: `[44:24]` to `[45:39]`.
- **`theta` and `phi`.** `theta` is the shared structure; `phi` is the task-specific parameterization recovered from it. Source: `[52:41]` to `[1:05:05]`.
- **Bayesian graphical model.** Dependencies between tasks are the formal way to think about shared structure. Source: `[47:15]` to `[1:07:58]`.
- **Inductive bias.** Meta-learning can be read as learning the right prior or bias over tasks. Source: `[1:09:22]` to `[1:10:14]`.
- **Meta-train / meta-test.** The training split is over tasks, not examples. Source: `[1:12:32]` to `[1:16:09]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- More pretraining data is not always the whole story. For the unsupervised case, pretraining on the target corpus itself can get very close to a much larger pretraining set. Source: `[32:32]` to `[34:06]`.
- Fine-tuning the last layer is a heuristic, not a theorem. The lecture shows cases where the first layer or a middle layer works better. Source: `[35:18]` to `[37:05]`.
- Meta-learning is not just about speed. The lecture says the objective can also target stability or better overall performance. Source: `[1:15:04]` to `[1:15:23]`.
- The task-level split is the real assumption. Meta-learning only works if train tasks and test tasks come from the same broader task distribution. Source: `[1:15:32]` to `[1:16:09]`.
- Shared structure is not a scalar similarity score. The lecture treats it as latent dependency structure, which is harder to estimate but more faithful to the problem. Source: `[1:06:04]` to `[1:09:14]`.

## Connections
- To L02: multitask learning shares a model across tasks at once; meta-learning shares structure across tasks over episodes.
