# L02 Multi-Task Learning Basics

Lecture 2 turns the intuition from L01 into a design space: define the task, tell the model what task it is on, and decide how much to share.

## Summary
- A task is defined as a triple: a distribution over `x`, a distribution over `y | x`, and a loss function. That definition is the lecture's base object for everything else. Source: `[5:52]` to `[6:00]`.
- Tasks can differ in all three pieces and still share structure. The lecture's paper-review example shows how one task descriptor can drive very different outputs. Source: `[11:03]` to `[14:43]`.
- Conditioning on the task descriptor `z` is the main architectural knob. The lecture walks through one-hot IDs, natural language prompts, concatenation, additive conditioning, multi-head models, and multiplicative conditioning. Source: `[12:10]` to `[34:42]`.
- Sharing is a spectrum, not a binary. The lecture ties that directly to negative transfer, overfitting, and soft parameter sharing. Source: `[55:35]` to `[63:05]`.
- The YouTube recommendation case study shows the payoff: a multi-task ranking system predicts engagement and satisfaction, then a mixture-of-experts variant beats the shared-bottom baseline. Source: `[1:06:33]` to `[1:16:25]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Task descriptor `z`.** Metadata that tells the network which task it is solving. Source: `[12:10]` to `[14:43]`.
- **Prompt-like conditioning.** The lecture explicitly says the task descriptor behaves like a prompt, even before prompt language became the default framing. Source: `[15:27]` to `[15:40]`.
- **Shared bottom.** One set of early layers is shared across tasks, with task-specific heads on top. Source: `[33:45]` to `[34:04]`.
- **Additive conditioning.** Combine task and input signals with a learned linear addition. Source: `[30:41]` to `[31:19]`.
- **Multiplicative conditioning.** Use element-wise gating to increase expressiveness and approximate multi-head behavior. Source: `[34:07]` to `[34:49]`.
- **Task weighting.** The total objective is a sum of task losses, but the weights change what the model prioritizes. Source: `[15:03]` to `[15:25]`.
- **Soft parameter sharing.** Separate task-specific parameters can still be tied together with an explicit similarity penalty. Source: `[1:00:26]` to `[1:01:39]`.
- **Negative transfer.** The tasks interfere instead of helping each other. Source: `[55:35]` to `[57:23]`.
- **Overfitting from under-sharing.** If the multitask model still overfits, the fix may be to share more. Source: `[1:01:41]` to `[1:02:17]`.
- **Mixture of experts.** The YouTube model uses task- and input-dependent expert routing to mix specialization with sharing. Source: `[1:14:52]` to `[1:16:25]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The task descriptor is basically a pre-LLM prompt interface. That is a cleaner way to think about many multitask systems than "one model, many labels." Source: `[15:27]` to `[15:40]`.
- Choosing how to condition on `z` is equivalent to choosing how and where to share parameters. The architecture is the policy. Source: `[27:21]` to `[27:44]`.
- There is no closed-form answer to "will multitask help?" The outcome depends on the task pair, the optimizer, the architecture, and even the stage of optimization. Source: `[1:02:19]` to `[1:03:05]`.
- Task relatedness can change over training. Two tasks may be complementary early and less helpful later. Source: `[1:03:05]` to `[1:03:24]`.
- "More sharing" and "less sharing" are both valid fixes, depending on whether the failure mode is negative transfer or overfitting. Source: `[55:35]` to `[1:02:17]`.

## Connections
- To L01: the lecture's "shared structure" idea becomes a concrete choice of descriptors, heads, and parameter tying.
