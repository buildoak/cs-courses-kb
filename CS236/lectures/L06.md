# L06 Variational Autoencoders

Lecture 6 is the implementation lecture for the ELBO: compute gradients through the decoder, fix the sampling problem with reparameterization, then amortize the per-datapoint posterior with an encoder network. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[1:21:54]`.

## Summary
- The VAE training problem has two gradients: one for the decoder parameters `theta`, which is easy, and one for the variational parameters `phi`, which is hard because the samples depend on `phi`. Source: transcript `[54:15]` to `[55:03]`, `[58:18]` to `[59:48]`.
- The reparameterization trick rewrites sampling from a Gaussian as a deterministic transform of `epsilon ~ N(0, I)`, which lets autodiff see how `phi` changes the sample path. Source: transcript `[1:00:04]` to `[1:06:10]`.
- The trick only works for continuous latent variables with a suitable sampling transform; discrete latents need REINFORCE or another relaxation. Source: transcript `[1:07:21]` to `[1:08:24]`.
- Per-datapoint variational parameters are too expensive, so the practical VAE uses amortized inference: an encoder network predicts the approximate posterior parameters from `x`. Source: transcript `[1:10:06]` to `[1:13:38]`.
- The encoder/decoder split makes the VAE an autoencoder with a probabilistic latent space and a regularized ELBO objective. Source: transcript `[1:17:37]` to `[1:21:54]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Monte Carlo ELBO gradients.** The expectations in the ELBO are approximated with sample averages, usually with one latent sample per datapoint in practice. Source: transcript `[55:14]` to `[57:25]`.
- **Gradient asymmetry.** `theta` is easy because the sampling distribution does not depend on it; `phi` is hard because changing `phi` changes the samples themselves. Source: transcript `[57:20]` to `[59:48]`.
- **Reparameterization as coordinate change.** `z` is written as a deterministic function of a base random variable `epsilon` and the variational parameters, so the expectation no longer sits directly inside a stochastic node. Source: transcript `[1:03:26]` to `[1:05:34]`.
- **Continuous-latent requirement.** The method works for Gaussians and related continuous families, not for arbitrary discrete latent variables. Source: transcript `[1:07:21]` to `[1:08:24]`.
- **Amortized inference.** Instead of a separate `phi_i` per datapoint, the encoder learns a mapping `x -> q_phi(z | x)` that predicts posterior parameters in one feedforward pass. Source: transcript `[1:10:22]` to `[1:13:35]`, `[1:17:37]` to `[1:18:23]`.
- **Regularization by inference sharing.** Sharing one encoder across all datapoints constrains the generative model and can improve generalization, even though it gives up some posterior fidelity. Source: transcript `[1:13:41]` to `[1:14:47]`.
- **Autoencoder interpretation.** The encoder outputs mean and standard deviation for the approximate posterior; the decoder maps `z` back to `x`. That is why the ELBO becomes a regularized autoencoding objective. Source: transcript `[1:20:49]` to `[1:21:54]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: reparameterization is not a model change. It is a gradient-path change, which is why the same ELBO becomes differentiable.
- Inference: amortization is a computational compromise that also changes the inductive bias. The encoder makes inference cheap, but it also regularizes the generator to be inferable by a shared network.
- Inference: the practical VAE is a three-way bargain between expressivity, gradient tractability, and inference speed.
- Inference: the lecture's REINFORCE mention is not decorative. It marks the boundary where reparameterization stops working and other estimators have to take over.
- Inference: the autoencoder resemblance is structural, not cosmetic. The encoder is literally approximating the posterior, and the decoder is literally generating from the latent sample.
