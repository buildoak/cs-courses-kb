# L04 Maximum Likelihood Learning

Lecture 4 turns the chain-rule story from L03 into an optimization problem: pick the model distribution that is closest to the data distribution under forward KL, then train it with maximum likelihood and stochastic gradient descent. Source: transcript `[31:03]` to `[1:18:56]`.

## Summary
- Autoregressive models stay tractable because chain rule makes likelihood exact, so training reduces to maximizing average log probability. Source: transcript `[30:35]` to `[31:23]`, `[1:11:32]` to `[1:12:41]`.
- The learning objective is forward KL, `KL(p_data || p_theta)`. Dropping the data-entropy term leaves the same objective as maximum likelihood. Source: transcript `[40:13]` to `[41:58]`, `[55:29]` to `[57:24]`.
- The asymmetry matters: reverse KL has different behavior, is more mode-seeking, and is flagged here as the quantity that later reappears in GAN-style training. Source: transcript `[49:05]` to `[50:28]`, `[53:08]` to `[53:58]`.
- Because the true data distribution is hidden, the expected log-likelihood is approximated by the empirical average over the training set. Source: transcript `[1:00:02]` to `[1:02:36]`.
- Neural autoregressive models are trained with backpropagation and mini-batch SGD; the practical problem is overfitting, not closed-form optimization. Source: transcript `[1:12:51]` to `[1:18:56]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Forward KL as the training target.** The course defines similarity between `p_data` and `p_theta` with `KL(p_data || p_theta)`, because the forward direction turns into maximum likelihood after the constant entropy term is removed. Source: transcript `[40:13]` to `[41:58]`, `[55:29]` to `[57:24]`.
- **Compression view.** The lecture frames KL as excess code length: if your model assigns probability poorly, your compression scheme is worse. Source: transcript `[42:02]` to `[46:25]`.
- **Asymmetry is not cosmetic.** Forward KL spreads mass across all observed modes; reverse KL prefers concentrating on modes and ignoring low-probability regions. Source: transcript `[49:05]` to `[53:58]`.
- **Entropy is the hidden constant.** When you expand the KL, the data entropy does not depend on `theta`, so it drops out of optimization even though it still matters for absolute calibration. Source: transcript `[55:29]` to `[58:38]`.
- **Empirical approximation.** The expected log-likelihood under the true data distribution is replaced by the average log-likelihood over the finite training set. Source: transcript `[1:00:02]` to `[1:01:32]`.
- **Coin-flip sanity check.** The Bernoulli example shows the clean closed-form case: three heads and two tails gives `theta = 0.6`, which becomes the neural-network case once the model family gets richer. Source: transcript `[1:08:45]` to `[1:11:00]`.
- **Mini-batch Monte Carlo.** The full gradient is a sum over all data points, so the scalable version is to estimate it on a subset of samples. Source: transcript `[1:15:16]` to `[1:17:55]`.
- **Overfitting is still the old enemy.** Maximum likelihood can memorize the training set, so the lecture calls out regularization and hypothesis-space control as the remaining practical constraints. Source: transcript `[1:18:02]` to `[1:18:56]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: maximum likelihood here is not a separate heuristic. It is the forward-KL projection of `p_data` onto the model family.
- Inference: the asymmetry of KL is the real fork in the road. Forward KL spreads probability mass; reverse KL prefers sharp modes and ignores some support.
- Inference: the Bernoulli example is not a toy in the dismissive sense. It is the simplest place where the switch from closed form to gradient-based learning becomes obvious.
- Inference: the model is easy to score once the factorization is chosen. The hard part is choosing a family that does not overfit while still matching the data distribution.

## Connections
- To L03: chain rule makes the likelihood exact, which is why maximum likelihood is even available for autoregressive models.
