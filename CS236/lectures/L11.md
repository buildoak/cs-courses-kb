# L11 Energy-Based Models
Lecture 11 switches from generators-as-samplers to explicit energies: define an unnormalized score, pay the partition-function cost, then recover training and sampling through contrastive divergence and MCMC. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[1:24:41]`.

## Summary
- Energy-based models define `p(x)` by exponentiating an energy and dividing by a partition function. That buys flexibility, but only if you can deal with the normalization constant. Source: transcript `[7:10]` to `[18:23]`.
- The lecture keeps hammering the same bottleneck: `f_theta(x)` is easy to evaluate, `Z_theta` is not. Likelihood-based training becomes expensive because the denominator depends on all possible states. Source: transcript `[18:29]` to `[19:59]`, `[1:03:32]` to `[1:06:29]`.
- Sampling is done with MCMC. Langevin dynamics is the gradient-guided version that makes proposals much better than blind local search. Source: transcript `[8:24]` to `[21:55]`.
- Contrastive divergence turns model samples into an unbiased Monte Carlo estimate of the log-likelihood gradient, which is why RBMs and DBMs were trainable at all. Source: transcript `[1:10:30]` to `[1:18:41]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Unnormalized energy plus partition function.** Any non-negative `g_theta(x)` can be normalized by dividing by `Z_theta`, which is the whole point of EBMs. Source: transcript `[15:48]` to `[18:23]`.
- **The partition function is the hard part.** Computing `Z_theta` means summing or integrating over every possible configuration, which is exponential in the discrete case. Source: transcript `[1:03:32]` to `[1:05:17]`.
- **Product of experts.** Multiplying simple densities gives a more selective model, but the product still needs renormalization, so it becomes an EBM. Source: transcript `[56:58]` to `[58:54]`.
- **RBM / DBM structure.** Restricted Boltzmann Machines connect visible and hidden binary units; stacking them gives Deep Boltzmann Machines and a hierarchy of features. Source: transcript `[59:01]` to `[1:03:27]`.
- **Contrastive divergence.** The gradient of the log-likelihood is a data term minus a model expectation; approximating that model expectation with a sample gives the practical training rule. Source: transcript `[1:08:10]` to `[1:18:41]`.
- **MCMC and detailed balance.** The sampler accepts uphill moves always and downhill moves sometimes, which is what makes the chain converge to the target distribution. Source: transcript `[1:19:13]` to `[1:24:41]`.
- **Langevin dynamics.** For continuous state spaces, you can use noisy gradient ascent instead of a purely local random walk. Source: transcript `[16:24]` to `[21:55]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: EBMs move the difficulty from "can I write the density?" to "can I sample and normalize this thing?" The flexibility is real, but the compute bill shifts to the sampler.
- Inference: contrastive divergence is not a separate magical objective. It is the log-likelihood gradient with the intractable model expectation replaced by a sample.
- Inference: Langevin is the important sampler because it uses the score direction. That is why it beats blind noise nudges.
- Inference: the RBM/DBM story is historical proof that EBMs were once the practical way to get deep unsupervised features when standard deep nets were still hard to train.
- Inference: the lecture keeps repeating the same asymmetry on purpose. The numerator is easy. The partition function is the tax.

## Connections
- To L10: GANs and EBMs both work without explicit likelihoods, but GANs learn a generator adversarially while EBMs keep a proper density and a sampler.
- To L12: score matching is the escape hatch that removes repeated sampling from the training loop.
- To diffusion models: Langevin dynamics is the ancestral move -- gradient plus noise toward higher-density regions.
- To representation learning: RBM hidden units are the old-school version of latent features.
