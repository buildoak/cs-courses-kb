# L16 Evaluation of Generative Models and Open Research Problems
Stanford's 2023 playlist labels this talk as Lecture 15; I kept the ticket's `L16.md` filename because the requested topic matches the evaluation lecture. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[1:21:25]`.

## Summary
- Likelihood is the clean baseline for tractable generative models: on a held-out test set, average log-likelihood doubles as compression. The lecture also flags the obvious problem - not all models expose tractable likelihoods, and equal compression does not mean equal utility. Source: transcript `[8:59]` to `[13:13]`, `[20:36]` to `[22:03]`; slides pp. 6, 10.
- Human evaluation is still the gold standard for sample quality. HYPE turns that into a benchmark with `HYPEtime` and `HYPEinfty`, but the lecture is blunt about the cost, bias, and reproducibility problems. Source: transcript `[35:22]` to `[39:17]`; slides pp. 11-12.
- `Inception Score`, `FID`, and `KID` are the lecture's main automatic image metrics. They each capture a different slice of quality, and each hides a failure mode. Source: transcript `[40:31]` to `[56:09]`; slides pp. 13-17.
- For text-to-image and language models, evaluation becomes multi-objective: alignment, bias, toxicity, aesthetics, robustness, and task breadth all matter. `HEIM`, `HELM`, and `BIG-bench` are the course's examples of widening the benchmark surface. Source: transcript `[57:30]` to `[1:15:01]`; slides pp. 18, 25-26.
- Latent representations are judged by clustering, compression, and disentanglement, but the lecture ends on the same answer it started with: the right metric depends on the task distribution, and that distribution is still an open design problem. Source: transcript `[59:12]` to `[1:21:25]`; slides pp. 19-24.

## Key Concepts
- **Likelihood as compression.** If a model assigns high likelihood to held-out data, it compresses well. That is a real signal for density estimation, but it is not a universal proxy for sample usefulness. Source: transcript `[9:16]` to `[13:13]`; slides pp. 6, 10.
- **Annealed importance sampling.** For latent-variable models, the lecture uses AIS as a way to estimate likelihoods when direct evaluation is hard. It gives unbiased likelihood estimates but biased log-likelihood estimates. Source: transcript `[33:32]` to `[34:26]`; slides p. 10.
- **HYPE.** Human evaluators compare real and fake samples under time pressure. `HYPEtime` measures how long it takes to tell real from fake; `HYPEinfty` measures the fraction of samples that fool people with unlimited time. Source: transcript `[35:40]` to `[38:16]`; slides pp. 11-12.
- **Inception Score.** `IS = D x S`, where `S` rewards confident class predictions and `D` rewards class coverage. It works only when a good classifier is available, and it can miss within-class mode collapse. Source: transcript `[40:31]` to `[45:26]`; slides pp. 14-15.
- **Frechet Inception Distance.** FID compares feature distributions of real and generated samples, usually by fitting Gaussians in the feature space of a pretrained classifier and taking a Wasserstein-2 distance. Lower is better. Source: transcript `[47:31]` to `[52:58]`; slides p. 16.
- **Kernel Inception Distance.** KID replaces the Gaussian approximation with MMD in feature space. It is more principled and unbiased, but the quadratic pairwise cost is the tradeoff. Source: transcript `[53:00]` to `[56:09]`; slides p. 17.
- **HEIM.** The lecture uses Stanford's text-to-image benchmark to show that quality alone is not enough. Alignment, caption robustness, bias, aesthetics, and originality all need separate attention. Source: transcript `[57:30]` to `[58:53]`; slides p. 18.
- **Unsupervised representation evaluation.** The lecture breaks this into clustering, compression, and disentanglement. For labeled datasets you can score clusters; for unlabeled disentanglement, the lecture says the problem is theoretically impossible without extra assumptions. Source: transcript `[59:12]` to `[1:08:24]`; slides pp. 19-24.
- **Prompting as evaluation pressure.** For language models, the model itself becomes the interface. A prompt can turn next-word prediction into sentiment classification, QA, or other tasks without extracting an intermediate representation. Source: transcript `[1:09:01]` to `[1:15:01]`; slides pp. 25-26.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Likelihood is a proxy for compressibility, not for human usefulness. The lecture spends real time on this distinction instead of pretending it is minor.
- Human evaluation can still miss memorization. A model that copies the training set can look good to people and still be useless as a generative model.
- `Inception Score` can be gamed by class coverage alone. It rewards sharpness and diversity at the label level, not diversity within a class.
- `FID` and `KID` inherit the blind spots of the feature extractor. The metric is only as good as the pretrained network it leans on.
- The unsupervised disentanglement result is the quiet killer in the room: if you do not know the true factors, there is no fully objective way to recover them.
- Evaluation is a task-design problem wearing a metrics costume. The real question is not "what is the best model?" but "what is the distribution of tasks we care about?"

## Connections
- To L11 and L12: the evaluation story mirrors the training story. Likelihood, compression, and score-based objectives all show up again, just from the other side of the table.
- To HELM and `BIG-bench`: the lecture's language-model segment is the same move those benchmarks make, just at a wider scale and with more task diversity.
- To modern text-to-image systems: `HEIM` is the course's example of the obvious next step after FID, because image quality alone stopped being enough.
- To prompting and instruction tuning: the model-as-interface idea is why evaluation now includes downstream task success, not just sample realism.

## Referenced Papers
- **HYPE: A Benchmark for Human eYe Perceptual Evaluation of Generative Models** (Zhou et al., 2019). Human perceptual evaluation benchmark behind `HYPEtime` and `HYPEinfty`. Source: transcript `[35:40]` to `[38:16]`; slides pp. 11-12. https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.01121
- **Improved Techniques for Training GANs** (Salimans et al., 2016). Canonical source for `Inception Score`. Source: transcript `[40:31]` to `[45:26]`; slides pp. 14-15. https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.03498
- **GANs Trained by a Two Time-Scale Update Rule Converge to a Local Nash Equilibrium** (Heusel et al., 2017). Introduces `Frechet Inception Distance`. Source: transcript `[47:31]` to `[52:58]`; slides p. 16. https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.08500
- **Demystifying MMD GANs** (Binkowski et al., 2018). Source for `Kernel Inception Distance` via MMD in feature space. Source: transcript `[53:00]` to `[56:09]`; slides p. 17. https://arxiv.org/abs/1801.01401
- **beta-VAE: Learning Basic Visual Concepts with a Constrained Variational Framework** (Higgins et al., 2017). Canonical disentanglement paper behind the lecture's metric discussion. Source: transcript `[1:06:04]` to `[1:08:24]`; slides p. 24. https://dblp.org/rec/conf/iclr/HigginsMPBGBML17
