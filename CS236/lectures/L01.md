# L01 Introduction

Lecture 1 frames generative modeling as a test of understanding: if you can create the thing, you know something real about it. Source: transcript `[3:16]` to `[5:12]`.

## Summary
- The course is about learning the foundations behind modern generative systems, not just using the black boxes. Source: transcript `[0:42]` to `[1:21]`.
- The core target is the unknown data distribution `p_data`. The whole class is about approximating it well enough that samples look like the data. Source: transcript `[44:59]` to `[46:08]`.
- The same idea shows up across images, speech, text, medical imaging, and video. The lecture uses image editing, CT reconstruction, WaveNet, and text completion as examples. Source: transcript `[24:15]` to `[30:04]`, slides pp. 18-27.
- The syllabus is really a map of tractability trade-offs: autoregressive and flow-based models, latent-variable models, implicit models, GANs, energy-based models, and diffusion models. Source: transcript `[47:56]` to `[50:09]`, slides p. 45.
- The class is intentionally mathematical. The point is to understand the machinery, then use it in applications. Source: transcript `[50:13]` to `[50:26]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Generative modeling as understanding.** The lecture leans on the Feynman-style claim that creation is evidence of understanding. Source: transcript `[3:16]` to `[5:12]`.
- **Discriminative vs generative.** Discriminative models draw decision boundaries. Generative models try to learn the joint distribution and can do both generation and classification. Source: slide PDF pp. 14-16, transcript `[44:37]` to `[45:47]`.
- **Data distribution vs model family.** The model is not a single distribution. It is a family of distributions, and training is the problem of projecting `p_data` onto that family. Source: transcript `[44:59]` to `[46:08]`.
- **Inverse problems.** Image editing, CT denoising, speech synthesis, and text completion are all the same shape: condition on partial information, then fill in the missing pieces. Source: transcript `[24:15]` to `[30:04]`.
- **Likelihood-accessible models.** The roadmap separates model families by whether they expose a tractable likelihood, and by how they compare data and model distributions. Source: transcript `[47:56]` to `[50:09]`.
- **Latent-variable intuition.** The intro already points forward to latent variables as a way to make distributions more expressive. Source: transcript `[48:36]` to `[50:06]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: the lecture is not selling "generation" as a product feature. It is treating generation as the operational definition of understanding.
- Inference: the real design variable is not just the architecture. It is the divergence or loss used to compare data and model distributions.
- Inference: the examples are intentionally cross-modal because the same conditioning logic reappears in vision, audio, text, and inverse problems.
- Inference: the syllabus is organized by bottleneck. Each later model family exists because some earlier bottleneck stayed too expensive.

## Connections
- To L02: the high-level `p_data` story becomes Bayes nets, discriminative vs generative factorization, and latent variables.
- To L03: autoregressive models are the first exact likelihood family in the roadmap.
- To later lectures: latent-variable, implicit, GAN, energy-based, and diffusion models all live on the same data-vs-model comparison axis.
- To applications: the lecture's medical, speech, and text demos are not side quests. They are the same conditional-generation problem in different clothing.
