# L09 Generative Adversarial Networks

Lecture 9 drops likelihood training entirely: instead of scoring data points, it learns a discriminator that tells real samples from fake ones, then uses that game to move the generator. Source: transcript `[0:06]` to `[1:18:07]`.

## Summary
- GANs are the first model family in the course that does not train by maximum likelihood. The whole point is to get good samples without needing a tractable density. Source: transcript `[0:06]` to `[7:24]`.
- The lecture opens with a warning: high likelihood and good sample quality are not the same thing, so likelihood-free training can be a rational choice. Source: transcript `[6:06]` to `[11:06]`.
- The discriminator is a learned two-sample statistic. Under the optimal discriminator, the game reduces to a Jensen-Shannon divergence between data and model. Source: transcript `[34:36]` to `[1:02:09]`.
- The generator does not need to be invertible or same-dimensional. That flexibility is the main gain over flows, VAEs, and autoregressive models. Source: transcript `[53:03]` to `[55:12]`.
- The lecture closes on the real cost: unstable optimization, mode collapse, and hard-to-read training dynamics. Source: transcript `[1:10:56]` to `[1:18:07]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Likelihood-free learning.** GANs replace explicit density evaluation with a two-sample test objective. Source: transcript `[7:59]` to `[9:18]`, `[44:36]` to `[45:38]`.
- **Generator and discriminator.** The generator maps noise to samples, and the discriminator learns to classify real vs fake. Source: transcript `[11:11]` to `[15:15]`, `[52:46]` to `[56:21]`.
- **Minimax game.** The discriminator maximizes the objective, the generator minimizes it. Source: transcript `[46:42]` to `[57:14]`.
- **Optimal discriminator.** Bayes rule gives the best discriminator as a density-ratio classifier. Source: transcript `[47:29]` to `[49:24]`, `[57:36]` to `[58:20]`.
- **Jensen-Shannon divergence.** Plugging the optimal discriminator into the game gives a symmetrized divergence between data and model. Source: transcript `[59:00]` to `[1:02:09]`.
- **Arbitrary generator architecture.** The generator can be any neural sampler; it does not have to be invertible or likelihood-friendly. Source: transcript `[53:03]` to `[55:12]`.
- **Training instability.** Oscillation, mode collapse, and weak stopping criteria are not edge cases. They are the central practical problems. Source: transcript `[1:10:56]` to `[1:15:50]`.
- **Extensions.** The lecture and notes point to `f-GAN`, `BiGAN`, and `CycleGAN` as follow-on directions. Source: transcript `[1:15:50]` to `[1:18:07]`, official notes.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: GANs are not "worse likelihood models." They are a deliberate refusal to optimize likelihood at all.
- Inference: the discriminator is not just a classifier. It is a learned statistic that turns sample comparison into a gradient signal.
- Inference: the global optimum is still the same equality condition as in likelihood-based models. The difference is the path, not the endpoint.
- Inference: the flexibility gain is real because the generator can be an arbitrary sampler. The price is that the optimization becomes the hard part.
- Inference: mode collapse is not a side effect. It is the natural failure mode of a game with asymmetric incentives.
