# L13 Score Based Models

Lecture 13 starts by treating the score as the object to learn, then spends the rest of the hour paying down the computational cost of that idea. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[1:21:46]`.

## Summary
- Score matching estimates `\nabla_x log p(x)` directly, so it avoids the partition function, but the vanilla objective is expensive because of Hessian and trace-of-Jacobian terms. Source: transcript `[9:51]` to `[11:17]`, `[31:09]` to `[31:36]`.
- Denoising score matching replaces `p_data` with a noise-perturbed density `q_sigma`, which makes the score-estimation problem tractable in high dimensions. Source: transcript `[31:48]` to `[38:28]`.
- Tweedie's formula makes the denoising view literal: the optimal denoiser follows the perturbed score toward high-probability regions. Source: transcript `[55:02]` to `[58:22]`.
- Sliced score matching replaces full-vector matching with random-direction projections, keeping the Fisher-divergence framing while lowering the derivative cost. Source: transcript `[59:17]` to `[1:01:54]`.
- Langevin dynamics turns a trained score field into a sampler, and the lecture ends by showing why naive Langevin mixes too slowly and why diffusion models are the fix. Source: transcript `[1:09:24]` to `[1:21:46]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Score function.** The network estimates the gradient of the log density, not the density itself. That is the central object throughout the lecture. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[0:35]`.
- **Fisher divergence.** Score matching fits the model score to the true score by minimizing a squared gradient difference. Source: transcript `[33:48]` to `[35:42]`.
- **Denoising score matching.** Add Gaussian noise, estimate the score of the smoothed density `q_sigma`, and use the fact that the smoothed problem is cheaper. Source: transcript `[32:06]` to `[34:47]`.
- **Tweedie's formula.** The best L2 denoiser is the conditional expectation of the clean sample given the noisy one, which is why denoising and score matching collapse into the same objective. Source: transcript `[55:16]` to `[58:22]`.
- **Sliced Fisher divergence.** Random projections turn vector matching into directional matching, which makes the high-dimensional Jacobian manageable. Source: transcript `[1:00:45]` to `[1:01:54]`.
- **Langevin MCMC.** Once the score is learned, sampling becomes "follow the arrow, add noise." The lecture uses this to explain both the promise and the failure mode of naive sampling. Source: transcript `[1:09:51]` to `[1:14:14]`.
- **Annealing as a bridge.** The lecture closes by showing that the diffusion idea is really about stacking many small denoising / Langevin steps. Source: transcript `[1:18:37]` to `[1:21:46]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: denoising score matching is not a detour away from the real objective. It is the same score field after smoothing, which is why it stays useful even though it changes the target density.
- Inference: the hard part is not "learning a neural net." It is paying for full-dimensional gradient geometry. That is why the trace/Jacobian terms dominate the story.
- Inference: sliced score matching is a geometry hack, not a different model family. It keeps the Fisher-divergence framing while swapping vector equality for directional equality.
- Inference: Langevin is only as good as the score field and the mixing rate. The lecture's failure example is not a bug in the score idea; it is a sampling bottleneck.

## Connections

- To L14: the "diffusion" part of the course is the next step after this lecture's annealed Langevin teaser.

## Referenced Papers
- **Estimation of Non-Normalized Statistical Models by Score Matching.** Hyvärinen, 2005. Core score-matching paper for unnormalized densities.
- **A Connection Between Score Matching and Denoising Autoencoders.** Pascal Vincent, 2011. The denoising identity behind Tweedie-style arguments. `https://www.iro.umontreal.ca/~vincentp/publications.html`
- **Sliced Score Matching: A Scalable Approach to Density and Score Estimation.** Song, Garg, Shi, Ermon, 2019. `https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.07088`
- **Generative Modeling by Estimating Gradients of the Data Distribution.** Song & Ermon, 2019. The noise-conditional score-network paper behind the lecture's diffusion teaser. `https://arxiv.org/abs/1907.05600`
