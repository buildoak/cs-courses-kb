# L05 Variational Autoencoders

Lecture 5 is the point where latent-variable modeling stops being a nice idea and becomes a computational problem: `p(x)` requires integrating over `z`, so the class switches to the ELBO as a tractable lower bound. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[1:20:52]`.

## Summary
- A VAE is a latent-variable model with a simple prior `p(z)` and a simple decoder `p(x | z)`, but the marginal over `x` becomes an infinite mixture and is therefore flexible. Source: transcript `[0:58]` to `[3:05]`.
- The marginal likelihood is intractable because you have to integrate over all latent values. That is the central training problem of the lecture. Source: transcript `[3:30]` to `[4:17]`, `[45:07]` to `[47:19]`.
- The ELBO comes from importance sampling plus Jensen's inequality, and the gap to the true log-likelihood is exactly a KL term. Source: transcript `[1:03:42]` to `[1:10:06]`, `[1:18:19]` to `[1:19:31]`.
- The best `q(z | x)` is the true posterior `p(z | x)`, but computing that posterior is the chicken-and-egg problem that makes the whole setup interesting. Source: transcript `[12:20]` to `[14:33]`, `[20:29]` to `[21:24]`.
- The practical version of the method is joint optimization over decoder parameters `theta` and variational parameters `phi`. Source: transcript `[16:09]` to `[17:22]`, `[27:26]` to `[29:59]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Latent variables as hidden structure.** The lecture motivates `z` as a way to capture factors of variation that are not annotated in the data. Source: transcript `[3:00]` to `[4:43]`.
- **Infinite mixture intuition.** A Gaussian prior over `z` plus a Gaussian decoder over `x | z` behaves like a mixture of infinitely many Gaussians. Source: transcript `[1:54]` to `[2:28]`, `[29:24]` to `[30:04]`.
- **Mixture-of-Gaussians warmup.** The finite categorical case is the toy version of the same idea: simple components, rich marginal. Source: transcript `[17:50]` to `[18:41]`, `[28:24]` to `[29:19]`.
- **The posterior is the target.** The best variational distribution is `p(z | x)` because it makes the ELBO tight. Source: transcript `[12:20]` to `[14:33]`.
- **ELBO decomposition.** The bound splits into a reconstruction-like expected log joint term and an entropy term for `q`. Source: transcript `[8:59]` to `[10:25]`.
- **EM analogy.** The lecture is explicit that this has the flavor of EM: infer missing values, then optimize the generative model. Source: transcript `[11:15]` to `[12:04]`, `[1:19:33]` to `[1:19:53]`.
- **Per-data-point inference.** The variational parameters should depend on the evidence `x`, because each datapoint needs a different posterior approximation. Source: transcript `[39:24]` to `[46:07]`.
- **Importance sampling as the bridge.** The derivation rewrites the marginal likelihood using a proposal `q(z)` and importance weights before applying Jensen. Source: transcript `[1:03:42]` to `[1:10:06]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: the VAE is not "an autoencoder plus a decoder." It is an approximate posterior family wrapped around a probabilistic generator.
- Inference: the ELBO is only a lower bound, but the slack is measured exactly. That makes the approximation principled rather than hand-wavy.
- Inference: the most honest way to think about the method is "find a tractable q that is as close as possible to the impossible posterior."
- Inference: the finite-mixture example is doing real work. It shows why a smooth latent space can be much more expressive than a lookup-table mixture with the same simple components.
- Inference: the course keeps the VAE story tied to likelihood, not just reconstruction quality. The point is still density estimation.
