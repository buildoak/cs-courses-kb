# L03 Autoregressive Models

Lecture 3 turns exact likelihood into architecture: chain rule gives you a correct factorization, but the entire problem is making that factorization cheap enough to train and useful enough to sample from. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[5:29]`.

## Summary
- Autoregressive modeling starts from a simple fact: any joint distribution can be written as a product of conditionals. Source: transcript `[5:08]` to `[5:36]`.
- The first workable model is FVSBN, where each conditional is a logistic regression over previous variables. Source: transcript `[27:47]` to `[29:25]`.
- NADE improves the story by tying weights across conditionals. That makes the model smaller, faster, and more reusable. Source: transcript `[32:58]` to `[37:58]`.
- The same factorization works for categorical and continuous data. The lecture generalizes from Bernoulli outputs to softmaxes and mixture-of-Gaussians outputs. Source: transcript `[44:21]` to `[49:16]`.
- Autoencoder-style graphs become autoregressive once you mask future connections. RNNs are the sequence version of the same idea. Source: transcript `[50:22]` to `[56:01]`, `[1:02:49]` to `[1:18:41]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Chain rule factorization.** Exact joint likelihood is always available if you are willing to choose an ordering and pay the sequential cost. Source: transcript `[5:08]` to `[6:19]`.
- **Ordering matters.** The factorization is exact for any order, but some orderings are much easier to model than others. Source: transcript `[5:45]` to `[6:19]`, `[1:06:44]` to `[1:07:41]`.
- **FVSBN.** The simplest autoregressive model uses a logistic regression conditioned on past variables only. Source: transcript `[27:47]` to `[31:45]`.
- **NADE.** A hidden layer plus tied weights turns the separate logistic regressions into one shared architecture with far fewer parameters. Source: transcript `[28:17]` to `[37:58]`.
- **Softmax and mixtures.** Discrete outputs become categorical distributions; continuous outputs become Gaussian or mixture density outputs. Source: transcript `[44:21]` to `[49:16]`.
- **Masking.** If you do not hide the future during training, the model cheats. Masking enforces the chain-rule constraint directly in the network. Source: transcript `[1:02:49]` to `[1:06:27]`.
- **RNNs.** Recursive state update is another way to carry the history forward without storing the whole prefix explicitly. Source: transcript `[1:11:07]` to `[1:18:41]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: NADE's main trick is not "deeper is better." The real win is weight sharing across conditionals, which turns exact likelihood into something practical.
- Inference: autoregressive models do not escape sequentiality. They make the sequential work exact, not free.
- Inference: masking is not a hack layered on top of the model. It is the model's causal rule, encoded in the graph.
- Inference: RNNs solve the "history keeps growing" problem by compressing the past into state. That is extreme sharing, not a Markov assumption.
