# L10 GANs
Lecture 10 turns the vanilla GAN objective into a map of the whole discriminator-based design space: Jensen-Shannon divergence, f-divergences, Wasserstein distance, and latent inference through BiGAN. Source: transcript `[0:04]` to `[1:27:23]`.

## Summary
- The core GAN move is a minimax game between generator and discriminator. With an optimal discriminator, that game becomes Jensen-Shannon divergence minimization instead of likelihood maximization. Source: transcript `[0:04]` to `[4:06]`.
- The lecture then generalizes the GAN trick to the whole f-divergence family by using convex conjugates to turn inaccessible density ratios into a tractable discriminator objective. Source: transcript `[6:04]` to `[32:50]`.
- Wasserstein GAN changes the geometry again: the critic is constrained to be 1-Lipschitz, which gives much better learning signal when the model and data are far apart. Source: transcript `[48:57]` to `[1:17:09]`.
- The last section adds an encoder to the GAN so the model can infer latent codes from data, not just sample data from latent codes. Source: transcript `[1:17:18]` to `[1:27:23]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Likelihood-free training.** GANs avoid explicit likelihoods by comparing real and fake samples through a discriminator instead of computing `p(x)`. Source: transcript `[0:04]` to `[0:58]`.
- **Optimal discriminator as density ratio.** Under the math, the discriminator is not just a classifier; it estimates the density ratio between data and model. Source: transcript `[3:22]` to `[4:06]`.
- **f-divergence via convex conjugates.** The lecture uses Fenchel duality to rewrite the divergence into a GAN-like objective that still only needs sample access. Source: transcript `[22:42]` to `[32:50]`.
- **Wasserstein critic.** The WGAN critic is not an arbitrary classifier; it is a 1-Lipschitz witness function whose shape gives usable gradients. Source: transcript `[1:04:17]` to `[1:17:09]`.
- **Training stability.** The whole reason WGAN matters is that the critic stays informative when regular GAN gradients flatten out and mode collapse becomes a problem. Source: transcript `[1:11:10]` to `[1:17:09]`.
- **BiGAN latent inference.** The adversarial game can be lifted from `x` alone to `(x, z)` pairs, which lets the model learn an encoder alongside the generator. Source: transcript `[1:17:18]` to `[1:27:23]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: the discriminator is doing more than classification. In the f-divergence view, it is a variational estimator of a density-ratio witness.
- Inference: WGAN changes the learning signal, not just the loss label. The critic becomes smoother, so the generator gets a direction even when supports are far apart.
- Inference: BiGAN fixes the "GANs have no encoder" complaint by moving the adversarial test to joint `(x, z)` space instead of trying to invert the generator directly.
- Inference: the lecture treats GAN variants as different ways to score a mismatch, not as separate species. JS, f-divergence, and Wasserstein are all witness-function games with different constraints.
- Inference: the "better gradients" story is the practical one. The math is fancier, but the payoff is simply that the generator stops staring into a flat loss surface.

## Connections
- To L11: the lecture's critique of unstable minimax training is the setup for energy-based models, where the model keeps an explicit density and a sampler instead of a learned generator.
- To L06: BiGAN mirrors the VAE idea of an encoder, but replaces KL matching with adversarial matching.
- To score matching later in the course: all of these methods are trying to define a tractable discrepancy when likelihood is unavailable or unusable.
- To representation learning: the BiGAN section is the bridge from pure generation to latent features that can be reused downstream.
