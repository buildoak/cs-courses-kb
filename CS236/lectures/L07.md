# L07 Normalizing Flow Models

Lecture 7 finishes the VAE thread, then makes the key move: replace an intractable encoder-decoder posterior with a deterministic invertible map. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[1:22:47]`.

## Summary
- The first half of the lecture is still VAE cleanup: posterior collapse, mutual information between `x` and `z`, and why the prior stays fixed and simple. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[25:20]`.
- Flow models keep latent variables, but force `z` and `x` to have the same dimension so the mapping can be inverted exactly. Source: transcript `[34:46]` to `[41:26]`.
- The entire training story becomes the change-of-variables formula: invert the map, evaluate the prior, then correct for local volume with the Jacobian determinant. Source: transcript `[41:26]` to `[1:03:24]`.
- The linear case is the geometry lesson. The determinant is area or volume scaling, so the nonlinear case just localizes that same idea with a Jacobian. Source: transcript `[55:07]` to `[1:08:40]`.
- The lecture ends by reframing flows as Gaussianizing coordinate changes, with inverse CDFs and copulas as the cleanest 1D intuition. Source: transcript `[1:18:12]` to `[1:22:47]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Deterministic decoder.** A flow model replaces the VAE decoder with a deterministic invertible function `f_theta`. That removes the posterior integral entirely. Source: transcript `[40:04]` to `[41:26]`.
- **Same-dimensional latent space.** Invertibility forces `z` and `x` to live in the same space. The cost is that flows do not compress. Source: transcript `[41:37]` to `[42:01]`.
- **Change of variables.** The density of the transformed variable is the prior density at the inverse point, multiplied by the absolute determinant of the inverse Jacobian. Source: transcript `[47:24]` to `[48:47]`, `[1:06:09]` to `[1:08:46]`.
- **Linear intuition.** A linear map sends the unit square or cube to a parallelogram or parallelotope. The determinant is the volume correction. Source: transcript `[55:07]` to `[1:01:12]`.
- **Nonlinear extension.** For neural networks, the local linearization is the Jacobian, so the same determinant logic still works. Source: transcript `[1:04:42]` to `[1:08:40]`.
- **Gaussianizing flows.** A flow can be read as learning a coordinate system in which complicated data becomes simple, ideally Gaussian. Source: transcript `[1:18:12]` to `[1:22:47]`.
- **Continuity matters.** The lecture is explicit that discrete data breaks the clean calculus story, so continuous relaxations or noise are part of the setup. Source: transcript `[1:10:20]` to `[1:13:07]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: flows are not "VAEs with a better encoder." They are a different tradeoff. You give up compression to buy exact likelihood.
- Inference: the lecture's VAE questions are not a digression. They define the pain point that flows eliminate: guessing `z` from `x`.
- Inference: the determinant term is not bookkeeping. It is the entire normalization guarantee.
- Inference: the end-of-lecture CDF argument is doing conceptual work. A flow is a learned inverse-CDF machine in higher dimensions.
- Inference: the discrete-data caveat is the hard boundary of the method. The math is clean only while the variables stay continuous.
