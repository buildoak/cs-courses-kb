# L12 Score Matching and Noise Contrastive Estimation
Lecture 12 removes the partition function from training twice: first by matching score fields, then by turning density estimation into a classification problem against a known noise distribution. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[1:23:07]`.

## Summary
- The score `\nabla_x log p(x)` depends on the energy but not on the partition function, so the model can be trained by matching score fields instead of matching likelihoods. Source: transcript `[27:27]` to `[35:37]`.
- Fisher divergence becomes tractable through integration by parts. After the rewrite, the loss only depends on model-side first and second derivatives. Source: transcript `[41:03]` to `[50:08]`.
- Score matching still gets expensive in high dimensions, so the lecture points to slice score matching and denoising score matching as scalable approximations. Source: transcript `[54:02]` to `[55:50]`.
- Noise contrastive estimation reframes density estimation as classification between data and a known noise distribution, with the partition function treated as a learnable parameter. Source: transcript `[57:55]` to `[1:08:01]`.
- The last section learns the noise distribution itself with a flow model, which makes the contrastive game adversarial in spirit and closer to the GANs discussed earlier. Source: transcript `[1:20:19]` to `[1:23:07]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Score function as a vector field.** The gradient of `log p(x)` tells you how to move `x` to increase likelihood most rapidly. Source: transcript `[28:04]` to `[34:30]`.
- **Fisher divergence.** Score matching compares the gradient fields of the data distribution and the model distribution, not the densities directly. Source: transcript `[35:05]` to `[38:40]`.
- **Integration by parts.** The unknown data score disappears after the rewrite, leaving a tractable objective that the model can optimize. Source: transcript `[41:03]` to `[47:58]`.
- **Trace of the Hessian.** In the multivariate case, the score-matching loss needs the Hessian trace of the log model density. Source: transcript `[48:13]` to `[51:12]`.
- **Slice / denoising approximations.** The lecture uses random directions or small perturbations to avoid full Hessian costs. Source: transcript `[54:02]` to `[55:50]`.
- **Noise contrastive estimation.** NCE trains a discriminator between data and noise, but the discriminator is wired to an energy model instead of an arbitrary MLP. Source: transcript `[57:55]` to `[1:06:04]`.
- **Learnable partition function.** The parameter `Z` is optimized directly; in the perfect-data limit it recovers the true partition function. Source: transcript `[1:03:24]` to `[1:05:47]`.
- **Flow contrastive estimation.** A flow model can serve as a stronger noise distribution, and the resulting adversarial loop becomes a GAN-like two-model game. Source: transcript `[1:20:19]` to `[1:23:07]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: score matching is about shape, not height. The partition function disappears because `\nabla_x log Z_theta = 0`.
- Inference: the ugly term in score matching is the data score, and integration by parts pushes that unknown term onto model-side derivatives.
- Inference: NCE is GAN-like only in the broad sense. The generator side is fixed noise, and the output of training is an energy model, not a sample generator.
- Inference: learning `Z` as an unconstrained scalar is legitimate because the optimum recovers the true normalization constant if optimization and data are perfect.
- Inference: flow contrastive estimation keeps the noise hard by learning it, which is why the classifier keeps getting a useful signal.

## Connections
- To L11: this is the first training route in the EBM section that avoids repeated model sampling during the training loop.
- To diffusion models: the lecture explicitly says score matching is the key building block behind them.
- To L10: NCE looks like a GAN discriminator game, but the object being learned is an energy and a partition-function estimate, not a generator.
- To modern score-based models: the score-field view is the shared representation that later becomes the model itself.
