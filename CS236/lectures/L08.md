# L08 Normalizing Flow Models

Lecture 8 is the architecture lecture: once the change-of-variables math is in place, the whole problem is building invertible layers with tractable Jacobians. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[1:22:47]`.

## Summary
- The lecture starts from the same flow setup as L07, then spends the rest of its time on concrete layer families that make the math practical. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[4:17]`, `[28:31]` to `[1:22:47]`.
- Planar flow is the first example, but the real story begins with architectures that make inversion and determinant computation cheap. Source: transcript `[11:03]` to `[15:47]`, `[23:53]` to `[29:03]`.
- NICE uses additive coupling and preserves volume. RealNVP adds learned scaling and is strictly more flexible. Source: transcript `[29:13]` to `[45:42]`.
- Continuous autoregressive models can be read as flows too. That gives MAF and IAF, which are the same model in opposite directions with opposite speed tradeoffs. Source: transcript `[49:51]` to `[1:06:43]`.
- Parallel WaveNet shows the practical trick: distill a slow autoregressive teacher into a fast IAF student. Source: transcript `[1:06:43]` to `[1:11:36]`.
- The lecture closes with masked invertible convolutions and a second view of flows as Gaussianizing transformations. Source: transcript `[1:14:06]` to `[1:22:47]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Planar flow.** The first nonlinear flow example shows how invertible transformations can still be built from neural nets, but the Jacobian is already the limiting factor. Source: transcript `[11:03]` to `[15:47]`.
- **NICE.** Non-linear Independent Components Estimation uses additive coupling: part of the input passes through unchanged, the rest is shifted. The Jacobian determinant is 1. Source: transcript `[29:13]` to `[36:38]`.
- **RealNVP.** Real-valued Non-Volume Preserving flow keeps the coupling structure but adds scaling, so the determinant is no longer fixed at 1. Source: transcript `[39:14]` to `[45:42]`.
- **Masked autoregressive flow.** A continuous autoregressive model can be viewed as a flow whose Jacobian is lower triangular, so likelihoods are still cheap. Sampling stays sequential. Source: transcript `[49:51]` to `[58:38]`.
- **Inverse autoregressive flow.** Flip the direction and the same model samples in parallel but becomes expensive to invert during training. Source: transcript `[59:09]` to `[1:06:43]`.
- **Parallel WaveNet distillation.** Train an autoregressive teacher by maximum likelihood, then train an IAF student with a KL objective that favors the student's fast sampling path. Source: transcript `[1:06:43]` to `[1:11:36]`.
- **Invertible convolutions.** Masked convolutional kernels recover triangular Jacobians in image models, so convolutional flows can stay tractable. Source: transcript `[1:14:06]` to `[1:17:19]`.
- **Gaussianizing viewpoint.** The lecture ends by treating the inverse flow as a learned Gaussianizer, with copulas as a shallow special case. Source: transcript `[1:17:28]` to `[1:22:47]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: MAF and IAF are not competing ideas. They are the same architecture seen from opposite directions.
- Inference: the best-of-both-worlds trick in Parallel WaveNet is just distillation with the KL direction chosen to match what each model can compute efficiently.
- Inference: flow design is mostly Jacobian engineering. The architecture matters because the determinant has to stay cheap.
- Inference: the lecture quietly turns autoregressive models into a flow family. That is the bridge between sequence modeling and invertible density estimation.
- Inference: the final Gaussianizing picture is the real abstraction. Flows are learned coordinate transforms, not just density estimators.
