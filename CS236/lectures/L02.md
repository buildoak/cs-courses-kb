# L02 Background and Latent Variable Models

Lecture 2 turns the slogan into math: define `p_data`, choose a model family, then figure out how to compare the two without drowning in dimensionality. Source: transcript `[0:05]` to `[6:12]`.

## Summary
- The generative modeling problem starts with samples from an unknown distribution, not with direct access to that distribution itself. Source: transcript `[2:13]` to `[4:18]`.
- High-dimensional data breaks naive tables. Bayes nets survive by assuming conditional independencies that shrink the factorization. Source: transcript `[36:24]` to `[39:11]`.
- Generative and discriminative models are two chain-rule factorizations of the same joint distribution, but they optimize different things. Source: transcript `[40:39]` to `[47:33]`.
- Generative models matter because they can reason about missing inputs and marginalize unobserved variables instead of giving up. Source: transcript `[47:43]` to `[48:00]`, `[1:15:50]` to `[1:19:38]`.
- Continuous variables and latent variables are the bridge to more expressive models. The lecture closes by mixing Bayesian networks with neural nets to motivate VAEs. Source: transcript `[1:13:16]` to `[1:19:38]`.

## Key Concepts
- **`p_data` is hidden.** Training only sees samples, so the task is to fit a model family to the unknown data-generating process. Source: transcript `[2:13]` to `[4:18]`.
- **Model family and loss are the real design choices.** Once the family is chosen, the class turns into a distance-minimization problem between distributions. Source: transcript `[4:35]` to `[6:08]`.
- **Bayesian networks.** Conditional independence assumptions turn a joint distribution into a product of local conditionals and make exact factorization manageable. Source: transcript `[36:24]` to `[39:11]`, slide PDF pp. 7-10.
- **Generative vs discriminative.** Naive Bayes models `p(y)` and `p(x | y)`; logistic regression models `p(y | x)` directly. Source: transcript `[41:28]` to `[57:13]`.
- **Missing data is the superpower.** If some inputs are not observed, a generative model can marginalize them instead of giving up. Source: transcript `[47:43]` to `[48:00]`.
- **Latent variables enlarge the hypothesis class.** A simple hidden `z` can turn a one-Gaussian story into a mixture model or a VAE-style decoder. Source: transcript `[1:15:50]` to `[1:19:38]`.
- **Autoencoders are not yet generators.** They become generative only once the latent space is constrained enough to sample from. Source: transcript `[1:17:54]` to `[1:19:38]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: the lecture is saying "structure is the answer" before it says "neural nets are the answer." Bayes nets are the proof of that.
- Inference: discriminative models are not weaker in the abstract. They are narrower on purpose because they refuse to spend capacity on `p(x)`.
- Inference: the autoencoder section matters because it shows the exact missing ingredient: a structured latent prior.
- Inference: the VAE appears here as a stitching move, not a new religion. It combines a simple latent prior with a neural conditional decoder.
