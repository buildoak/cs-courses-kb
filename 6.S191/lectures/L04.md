# L04 Convolutional Neural Networks

This lecture is the spatial version of the feature-hierarchy story: images as tensors, convolution as local weight sharing, pooling as controlled compression, then end-to-end CNNs for classification and beyond.

## Summary
- The lecture starts by treating images as arrays of numbers and splitting image problems into regression and classification. Source: `[6:14]` to `[8:22]`.
- It shows why fully connected networks are the wrong first move for images: flattening destroys spatial structure and explodes parameter count. Source: `[14:31]` to `[15:24]`.
- It builds convolution from first principles: local filters slide across the image, preserve spatial structure, and learn features instead of hand-coding them. Source: `[18:01]` to `[30:57]`.
- It turns that into a CNN stack: convolutions, nonlinearities, pooling, and depthwise feature reuse. Source: `[30:42]` to `[40:12]`.
- It ends by showing CNNs as reusable feature extractors for classification, detection, segmentation, transfer learning, and medical imaging. Source: `[42:07]` to `[51:16]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Image as tensor.** Grayscale images are 2D arrays of pixel values; color images add a third channel dimension. Source: `[6:14]` to `[7:28]`.
- **Regression vs classification.** Image models either predict a continuous value or a discrete class label. Source: `[7:44]` to `[8:22]`.
- **Feature detection.** Classification works by finding the image features that separate one class from another. Source: `[9:18]` to `[10:11]`.
- **Invariance.** Good vision models must survive lighting, rotation, deformation, and position changes. Source: `[10:53]` to `[11:29]`.
- **Flattening problem.** Fully connected nets erase spatial information and connect every pixel to every neuron. Source: `[14:31]` to `[15:29]`.
- **Convolution.** A local filter slides across the image and reuses the same weights everywhere. Source: `[18:01]` to `[26:29]`.
- **Learned filters.** Filters can behave like edge detectors, sharpeners, or other feature detectors. Source: `[27:09]` to `[28:26]`.
- **CNN stack.** A CNN is convolution + nonlinearity + pooling, repeated in depth. Source: `[30:42]` to `[33:45]`.
- **Receptive field.** Deeper layers see larger spatial context even when each operation stays local. Source: `[31:00]` to `[33:45]`.
- **Max pooling.** Downsampling keeps the strongest activation and trades resolution for invariance. Source: `[39:03]` to `[40:12]`.
- **Softmax head.** The final classifier turns extracted features into probabilities. Source: `[42:07]` to `[42:50]`.
- **Transfer learning.** The CNN can be reused as a feature extractor before the task-specific head. Source: `[47:19]` to `[51:16]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: Convolution is not just a compute trick. It is the inductive bias that says locality and weight sharing are the right way to model images. Source: `[18:01]` to `[28:43]`.
- Inference: The real gain from CNNs is not only accuracy. It is parameter control. The network stops needing every pixel to talk to every other pixel. Source: `[14:31]` to `[15:29]`.
- Inference: Max pooling is deliberate information loss. The lecture treats that loss as the price of invariance and larger effective context. Source: `[39:03]` to `[40:12]`.
- Inference: The learned filters are inspectable. That makes the first stage of perception less opaque than a dense network. Source: `[40:31]` to `[40:49]`.
- Inference: CNNs are not just classifiers. They are reusable representations that can be repurposed for detection and segmentation. Source: `[47:19]` to `[50:54]`.
- Inference: The medical-imaging example matters because it shows CNNs leaving toy demos and entering high-stakes binary diagnosis. Source: `[48:32]` to `[48:38]`.
