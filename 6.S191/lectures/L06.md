# L06 Reinforcement Learning

This lecture is the control version of the deep-learning story: state-action pairs instead of labels, reward instead of class probability, then Q-learning and policy learning as two ways to act in a dynamic environment.

## Summary
- The lecture frames reinforcement learning as the third learning regime after supervised and unsupervised learning. Source: `[0:14]` to `[5:36]`.
- It defines the core vocabulary: agent, environment, state, action, observation, reward, return, discounting, and the Q-function. Source: `[6:01]` to `[14:45]`.
- It shows how Q-learning turns action selection into a neural prediction problem and scales that idea to Atari with Q networks. Source: `[15:13]` to `[29:34]`.
- It then calls out the limits of Q-learning: discrete action spaces, deterministic outputs, and the need for policy learning in continuous spaces. Source: `[30:12]` to `[41:17]`.
- It closes with robotics and Go: policy learning, continuous control, and self-play remove the need for human-game pretraining. Source: `[55:11]` to `[58:18]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Agent.** The decision-maker interacting with the environment. Source: `[6:01]` to `[6:22]`.
- **Environment.** The world that returns observations and rewards after actions. Source: `[6:30]` to `[8:07]`.
- **Reward.** Immediate feedback after an action. Source: `[8:09]` to `[9:47]`.
- **Return.** Discounted sum of future rewards, not just the immediate reward. Source: `[9:49]` to `[14:09]`.
- **Discount factor.** Future rewards are worth less than current rewards. Source: `[9:19]` to `[9:47]`.
- **Q-function.** Expected future return for a given state-action pair. Source: `[10:28]` to `[11:04]`.
- **Optimal action.** Choose the action with the highest Q value. Source: `[12:52]` to `[13:02]`.
- **Q-network.** Neural net that outputs one Q value per discrete action. Source: `[21:49]` to `[26:25]`.
- **Q-learning.** Train the Q network against a target return computed from the next state. Source: `[24:30]` to `[25:47]`.
- **Policy function.** Directly learn an action policy instead of only learning Q values. Source: `[15:41]` to `[16:26]`.
- **Continuous action spaces.** Policy networks handle continuous outputs that Q-learning cannot represent cleanly. Source: `[30:12]` to `[41:17]`.
- **Self-play.** Improve by playing against yourself instead of relying on human demonstrations. Source: `[55:11]` to `[58:18]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: Reward and return are not the same. A locally worse action can still be correct if it improves discounted future return. Source: `[13:44]` to `[14:09]`.
- Inference: Q-learning is elegant because it reduces control to regression over future reward targets. Source: `[21:49]` to `[25:47]`.
- Inference: The hard part is not just prediction. It is action-space structure. Discrete vs continuous changes the whole algorithm choice. Source: `[30:12]` to `[41:17]`.
- Inference: Reward design is an engineering problem. The environment designer defines the reward model, and that shapes behavior. Source: `[33:26]` to `[34:09]`.
- Inference: Policy learning is the escape hatch for continuous control because it can model a probability distribution over actions instead of a fixed argmax. Source: `[40:33]` to `[41:17]`.
- Inference: The Go section is a shift from imitation to self-improvement. The system stops depending on human games and starts improving against itself. Source: `[57:51]` to `[58:18]`.
