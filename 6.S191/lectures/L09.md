# L09 MIT 6.S191 (Liquid AI): Large Language Models

This talk is the back half of LLMs: data, alignment, evaluation, and test-time compute.

## Summary
- Post-training is broken into supervised fine-tuning, preference alignment, and evaluation, with test-time compute as the emerging extension. Source: `[1:05]` to `[2:48]`, `[23:31]` to `[29:32]`, `[41:35]` to `[53:59]`.
- Good data has three dimensions: accuracy, diversity, and complexity. Synthetic data is helpful only until it collapses diversity. Source: `[8:43]` to `[11:00]`.
- The talk gives concrete pipelines for instruction and preference data: back-translation, judge LLMs, decontamination, and UltraFeedback-style selection. Source: `[13:00]` to `[17:54]`.
- For fine-tuning, LoRA and QLoRA are the efficient default, while DPO is the practical preference-alignment choice over PPO. Source: `[20:56]` to `[25:38]`.
- The evaluation section treats quality as a search problem: learning rate, batch size, validation loss, model merging, majority voting, best-of-n, process reward models, and reward-model-based uncertainty all act as control knobs. Source: `[26:39]` to `[29:32]`, `[30:14]` to `[32:37]`, `[41:38]` to `[55:55]`, `[1:06:57]` to `[1:08:09]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Supervised fine-tuning.** Train the model on instruction / answer pairs and teach the chat template. Source: `[1:41]` to `[2:04]`, `[18:03]` to `[19:21]`.
- **Preference alignment.** Train on chosen/rejected answers so the model learns what to prefer. Source: `[2:20]` to `[2:48]`.
- **Accuracy.** The answer has to be correct. Source: `[9:04]` to `[9:44]`.
- **Diversity.** Data should span many interaction types, not just one narrow domain. Source: `[9:46]` to `[10:35]`.
- **Complexity.** Harder prompts with longer reasoning traces improve robustness. Source: `[10:38]` to `[12:47]`.
- **Back translation.** Use a model to reconstruct the question that matches raw text. Source: `[14:01]` to `[14:12]`.
- **Decontamination.** Keep training data away from eval sets like IFEval. Source: `[15:52]` to `[16:06]`.
- **UltraFeedback.** Generate many answers, judge them, keep the best and worst for preference data. Source: `[16:12]` to `[16:55]`.
- **LoRA.** Train a small adapter matrix instead of the full model. Source: `[21:40]` to `[22:19]`.
- **QLoRA.** Quantize the base model to 4-bit, then apply adapters. Source: `[22:30]` to `[23:11]`.
- **PPO / DPO.** PPO is heavier and DPO is the practical default. Source: `[23:33]` to `[25:38]`.
- **Learning rate.** The first tuning knob to check when training goes unstable. Source: `[25:42]` to `[26:02]`.
- **AdamW.** The recommended optimizer baseline. Source: `[27:23]` to `[27:40]`.
- **FlashAttention.** The efficient attention implementation the talk recommends. Source: `[27:43]` to `[28:00]`.
- **Model merging.** Average or fuse checkpoints to build a stronger model. Source: `[30:38]` to `[31:24]`.
- **Majority voting / best-of-n.** Sample several answers, then score or vote on them. Source: `[50:13]` to `[50:46]`.
- **Process reward model.** Score intermediate reasoning steps, not only the final answer. Source: `[51:04]` to `[52:58]`.
- **Reward model uncertainty.** Reward scores can be reused as a confidence signal for safety-critical use cases. Source: `[53:12]` to `[53:59]`, `[1:06:57]` to `[1:08:09]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Good post-training data is not just correct data; it has to preserve enough diversity to avoid flattening the model. Source: `[9:46]` to `[10:35]`.
- DPO is attractive because it is cheap enough to use, not because it is philosophically pure. Source: `[24:57]` to `[25:38]`.
- Reward models do double duty here: they shape alignment during training and can estimate uncertainty later. Source: `[24:16]` to `[25:38]`, `[53:12]` to `[53:59]`.
- Majority voting is a weak proxy for confidence, but best-of-n plus a judge model is already much stronger. Source: `[50:13]` to `[50:46]`.
- Tokenizer design is upstream of post-training. If it is wrong, later tuning cannot fully rescue it. Source: `[1:05:05]` to `[1:06:40]`.
