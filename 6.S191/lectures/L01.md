# L01 Introduction to Deep Learning

This lecture is the base layer: why deep learning works now, what a neuron does, how depth adds expressive power, and how training turns all of that into a usable model.

## Summary
- The lecture frames deep learning as learned feature hierarchy, then motivates why the field took off only once data, compute, and open-source tooling lined up. Source: `[15:27]` to `[19:19]`.
- It builds the core unit from scratch: weighted inputs, a bias, and a nonlinear activation, then scales that into dense layers and deeper networks. Source: `[19:23]` to `[35:24]`.
- It moves from architecture to training: loss, gradients, learning rate, gradient descent, and stochastic variants. Source: `[38:37]` to `[47:49]`.
- It closes on the practical failure modes: overfitting, regularization, dropout, and early stopping. Source: `[48:40]` to `[55:54]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Deep learning.** Learn features from data instead of hand-coding them. Source: `[16:14]` to `[18:29]`.
- **Feature hierarchy.** Faces are built from lines, curves, parts, then whole structures. Source: `[16:23]` to `[17:21]`.
- **Perceptron / neuron.** Multiply inputs by weights, add bias, pass through a nonlinear activation. Source: `[19:23]` to `[20:20]`.
- **Activation function.** Nonlinearity is what makes the model more than a linear map. Source: `[20:08]` to `[20:20]`.
- **Dense / linear layer.** The same neuron math written as a reusable matrix operation. Source: `[30:57]` to `[31:08]`.
- **Deep neural network.** Stack linear layers and nonlinearities to get hierarchical abstraction. Source: `[33:23]` to `[35:24]`.
- **Loss.** Measure how far the prediction is from the target across the dataset. Source: `[38:37]` to `[39:26]`.
- **Gradient descent.** Step in the opposite direction of the gradient to reduce loss. Source: `[42:26]` to `[45:07]`.
- **SGD and minibatches.** Trade exactness for speed by estimating gradients on subsets of data. Source: `[46:18]` to `[48:36]`.
- **Dropout.** Randomly zero hidden activations during training to reduce reliance on any one path. Source: `[51:05]` to `[52:33]`.
- **Early stopping.** Save the checkpoint before validation loss turns up. Source: `[53:01]` to `[55:00]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture is making a historical claim, not a mathematical one: the core algorithms are old, but data, compute, and toolchains made them usable at scale. Source: `[18:32]` to `[19:19]`.
- Depth is not just "more layers". It is hierarchical composition, which increases expressive capacity without changing the basic building block. Source: `[33:23]` to `[35:24]`.
- Learning rate is the actual knife-edge. Too small stalls in shallow minima; too large blows straight past the basin. Source: `[43:32]` to `[45:07]`.
- SGD is the baseline compromise, not the end of the story. Adaptive optimizers win in practice because they handle the learning-rate problem better. Source: `[45:10]` to `[45:58]`.
- Dropout is capacity control, not magic. It forces the network to survive missing pathways and stops it from memorizing a single route through the graph. Source: `[51:20]` to `[52:19]`.
- Early stopping is model selection, not just training discipline. The best checkpoint is often before the training curve finishes looking better. Source: `[53:47]` to `[55:00]`.
