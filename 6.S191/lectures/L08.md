# L08 MIT 6.S191 (Google): Large Language Models

This lecture is the practical LLM boot camp: how auto-regressive decoding, prompt shape, post-training, safety, and agentic tools fit together.

## Summary
- Language models are introduced as fancy autocomplete; the talk builds a count-based Bayesian model first, then scales to auto-regressive decoding and large context windows. Source: `[1:33]` to `[7:21]`, `[13:05]` to `[15:10]`.
- Prompting changes outcomes by moving the model into the right region of its training distribution. Zero-shot, one-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought, and instruction tuning all sit on the same axis. Source: `[9:27]` to `[12:16]`, `[18:38]` to `[22:47]`, `[28:47]` to `[29:31]`.
- Post-training methods matter: instruction tuning, RLHF, constitutional AI, and LoRA / PEFT are the practical ways to move from a base model to a useful assistant. Source: `[23:03]` to `[30:45]`.
- The safety section is blunt: jailbreaks, bias, hallucinations, and rule-breaking are structural problems, so you need external safety circuitry and policy layers. Source: `[31:20]` to `[35:15]`.
- The lecture ends with agentic workflows: ReAct combines reasoning and action, and Toolformer shows how a model can learn to call external tools. Source: `[35:27]` to `[42:37]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Auto-regressive decoding.** Predict one token, feed it back in, repeat. Source: `[2:13]` to `[2:26]`.
- **Bayesian language model.** The lecture starts from n-gram counts and probability dictionaries. Source: `[4:06]` to `[6:20]`.
- **Context window.** Bigger windows let the model condition on more text at once. Source: `[13:41]` to `[14:15]`.
- **Zero-shot / one-shot / few-shot.** Prompting with varying numbers of examples changes accuracy. Source: `[15:28]` to `[16:10]`.
- **Chain-of-thought.** Step-by-step prompting gives the model more room to recover from mistakes. Source: `[20:36]` to `[22:47]`.
- **Instruction tuning.** Continue next-token training on instruction-like data to make the model follow directions. Source: `[28:47]` to `[29:31]`.
- **RLHF.** Human preference data is turned into a reward model. Source: `[29:33]` to `[30:00]`.
- **Constitutional AI.** Rules can substitute for some human preference labeling. Source: `[30:04]` to `[30:30]`.
- **LoRA.** Parameter-efficient fine-tuning changes a small adapter instead of the full model. Source: `[23:03]` to `[24:41]`.
- **External safety circuitry.** Guardrails sit around the model because the prompt can be compromised. Source: `[31:20]` to `[32:42]`.
- **ReAct.** Reasoning and action are fused into one loop. Source: `[36:24]` to `[39:33]`.
- **Toolformer.** The model learns when to call question-answering, calculator, translation, or Wikipedia tools. Source: `[39:35]` to `[42:37]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Prompting is not cosmetic. It is a way to steer the model into a different region of the training distribution. Source: `[18:38]` to `[19:24]`.
- Chain-of-thought works because it creates more surface area for error correction during generation. Source: `[21:42]` to `[22:47]`.
- A model can be useful and unsafe at the same time; the production answer is policy plus model. Source: `[31:20]` to `[35:15]`.
- Agents are an integration pattern, not a new model class. Source: `[35:27]` to `[36:11]`.
- Tool traces are not just inference logs. They can be filtered and recycled into future training data. Source: `[41:45]` to `[42:37]`.
