# L05 Policy Search 1

The lecture leaves Q-learning behind and goes straight at the policy: parameterize it, make it stochastic when the world demands it, and use the score-function trick to optimize it without differentiating through the environment.

## Summary
- Stochastic policies are not optional decoration. In rock-paper-scissors and aliased state spaces, deterministic policies are exploitable or outright impossible. Source: `[11:24]` to `[17:16]`.
- Policy search reframes RL as optimization over theta, where the goal is to maximize the value of a parameterized policy directly. Source: `[17:18]` to `[19:02]`.
- The likelihood-ratio / score-function derivation gives a usable gradient even when the reward is non-differentiable and the dynamics are not explicitly modeled. Source: `[42:47]` to `[56:36]`.
- Softmax and Gaussian policies are the two concrete policy classes the lecture works through because their score functions are easy to compute. Source: `[48:52]` to `[52:56]`.
- REINFORCE turns full trajectories into Monte Carlo policy-gradient updates, then the lecture ends by teeing up baselines as the next variance fix. Source: `[58:23]` to `[1:08:21]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Stochastic policy.** A policy that outputs a distribution over actions instead of a single action. Source: `[11:24]` to `[13:57]`.
- **Aliasing / partial observability.** If two states look identical to the agent, a deterministic policy can be forced into the wrong choice in both. Source: `[14:00]` to `[17:02]`.
- **Policy parameterization.** The policy is represented directly by theta, not derived from a value function. Source: `[19:03]` to `[20:10]`.
- **Score function.** The derivative of `log pi_theta(a|s)`, which is the workhorse of the policy-gradient derivation. Source: `[48:07]` to `[52:56]`.
- **Softmax policy.** A discrete-action policy built from feature dot products and a normalization over actions. Source: `[48:52]` to `[51:25]`.
- **Gaussian policy.** A continuous-action policy with a mean and variance, useful for robotics-style control. Source: `[52:16]` to `[52:56]`.
- **REINFORCE.** The Monte Carlo policy-gradient update `theta <- theta + alpha * score * return`. Source: `[1:03:35]` to `[1:05:01]`.
- **Baseline preview.** The lecture closes by motivating a state-only baseline to reduce gradient variance without changing the expectation. Source: `[1:05:01]` to `[1:08:21]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Stochasticity is sometimes the optimal policy, not just a way to explore. Rock-paper-scissors and aliased states both force that conclusion. Source: `[11:24]` to `[17:16]`.
- The reward does not need to be differentiable. The policy must be differentiable; the reward can be ugly, discontinuous, or just sampled. Source: `[53:02]` to `[56:36]`.
- The derivation does not require a Markov dynamics model. The environment terms drop out when you take the derivative. Source: `[46:47]` to `[48:06]`.
- Temporal structure matters. A reward at time `t` cannot depend on actions taken after `t`, so you can cut variance by only crediting earlier decisions with later rewards they could have caused. Source: `[58:23]` to `[1:03:35]`.
- REINFORCE is unbiased but noisy. The lecture is explicit that the next problem is variance, not correctness. Source: `[1:03:35]` to `[1:08:21]`.
