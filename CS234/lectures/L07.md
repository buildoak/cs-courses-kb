# L07 Policy Gradient Methods, GAE, and Imitation Learning

This lecture spends its energy on variance control, then uses that machinery to bridge from policy gradients into imitation learning. The throughline is simple: better advantage estimates give better gradients, but human demonstrations can still fail badly when the policy drifts off the data distribution.

## Summary
- The lecture revisits n-step advantage estimators and rewrites them as telescoping sums of TD deltas, which is the setup for GAE. Source: `[7:09]` to `[19:12]`.
- Generalized Advantage Estimation is a weighted combination of k-step estimators; `lambda` tunes the bias-variance trade-off, and PPO uses a truncated version in practice. Source: `[14:14]` to `[28:33]`.
- PPO's local-improvement story depends on keeping policy updates close in KL, but the lecture treats that as a practical bound, not a guarantee of monotonic improvement. Source: `[29:06]` to `[30:25]`, `[41:40]` to `[43:03]`.
- Imitation learning is presented as the human-feedback branch of RL: behavior cloning, then DAGGER when compounding errors become the real problem. Source: `[45:26]` to `[1:03:41]`.
- The lecture ends by pointing to maximum entropy inverse reinforcement learning and GAIL as the reward-recovery route beyond plain imitation. Source: `[1:17:21]` to `[1:18:36]`.

## Key Concepts
- **TD delta.** `delta V_t = r_t + gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)` is the basic building block of the advantage rewrite. Source: `[8:37]` to `[9:32]`.
- **Telescoping sum.** Multi-step advantage estimates collapse into sums of deltas because intermediate `V(s_t)` terms cancel. Source: `[10:06]` to `[12:07]`.
- **GAE.** Generalized Advantage Estimation mixes k-step estimators with geometric weights controlled by `lambda`. Source: `[14:14]` to `[19:12]`.
- **Truncated GAE.** PPO does not wait for infinite horizon; it computes a finite-window advantage estimate and updates every `T` steps. Source: `[27:53]` to `[28:23]`.
- **KL bound.** PPO's performance argument assumes the new policy stays close to the old one in action space. Source: `[29:16]` to `[30:25]`.
- **Policy gradient.** Better advantage estimates yield better gradients, which can reduce the amount of data needed. Source: `[20:53]` to `[21:02]`.
- **Behavior cloning.** RL is reduced to supervised learning by mapping states directly to expert actions. Source: `[49:39]` to `[50:37]`.
- **Distribution mismatch.** Small supervised errors can compound across a trajectory, turning `epsilon`-level BC error into `epsilon * T^2`-scale damage. Source: `[57:45]` to `[59:57]`.
- **DAGGER.** The expert relabels the states visited by the learner, then the aggregated dataset is cloned again. Source: `[1:00:01]` to `[1:02:37]`.
- **MaxEnt IRL / GAIL.** If demonstrations are not enough, the next move is to infer a reward model compatible with expert behavior. Source: `[1:17:21]` to `[1:18:36]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- GAE is not a new family of estimators. It is a compact weighting scheme over estimators the lecture already derived. Source: `[14:14]` to `[18:41]`.
- PPO's practical trick is truncation. The theory wants long-horizon estimates, but the implementation needs frequent updates. Source: `[27:27]` to `[28:23]`.
- Behavior cloning can look excellent on the training distribution and still fail badly once one mistake pushes the policy into unfamiliar states. Source: `[57:17]` to `[59:57]`.
- DAGGER's real cost is human time. It fixes compounding error by keeping the expert in the loop, which is exactly why it does not scale cleanly. Source: `[1:02:54]` to `[1:03:24]`.
- MaxEnt IRL does not claim to recover the literal human reward. It recovers a reward compatible with the demos and then picks the maximum-entropy solution among them. Source: `[1:17:26]` to `[1:18:36]`.
