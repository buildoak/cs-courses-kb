# L04 Q-learning and Function Approximation

The lecture leaves tabular control behind and lands in the messy center of deep RL: model-free policy improvement, function approximation, bootstrapping, off-policy learning, and the two DQN tricks that keep the whole thing from drifting apart.

## Summary
- Epsilon-greedy policies are the first fix for the coverage problem: they keep some random action mass so Q estimates see more than one action per state. Source: `[8:14]` to `[15:20]`.
- Monte Carlo control can improve a policy after each episode, and the same logic carries into function approximation by fitting Q to observed returns. Source: `[15:48]` to `[1:00:12]`.
- TD with function approximation is where the warning label appears: bootstrapping, approximation, and off-policy learning can make Q oscillate or fail to converge. Source: `[1:00:24]` to `[1:05:56]`.
- DQN stabilizes Atari learning with experience replay and fixed target weights; the lecture makes replay look like the bigger win. Source: `[1:06:00]` to `[1:14:17]`.
- Deep RL still works in practice despite the theory, and the lecture closes with DQN results plus Double DQN as the next patch for maximization bias. Source: `[1:14:17]` to `[1:18:19]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Epsilon-greedy.** With probability `1 - epsilon`, take the current argmax action; otherwise explore randomly. Source: `[11:02]` to `[15:20]`.
- **Model-free policy iteration.** Estimate Q directly from experience, then extract a better policy from that estimate. Source: `[8:54]` to `[9:17]`, `[15:27]` to `[15:48]`.
- **Monte Carlo control.** Run full episodes, update Q from returns, then improve the policy after each episode. Source: `[15:48]` to `[19:01]`.
- **Function approximation.** Replace a table with a parameterized Q-function and fit it by minimizing mean squared error to sampled targets. Source: `[58:21]` to `[1:00:12]`.
- **TD target.** Bootstrap from the next state instead of waiting for the full return. Source: `[1:00:24]` to `[1:01:22]`.
- **Deadly triad.** Bootstrapping, function approximation, and off-policy learning can destroy convergence guarantees. Source: `[1:03:01]` to `[1:05:02]`.
- **Experience replay.** Store old transitions and sample them later to reduce correlation and reuse data. Source: `[1:06:55]` to `[1:08:18]`.
- **Target network.** Keep a slower-moving copy of Q weights so the bootstrap target is less nonstationary. Source: `[1:08:21]` to `[1:12:29]`.
- **Double DQN.** Reduce maximization bias by changing how the max over next-state actions is computed. Source: `[1:17:00]` to `[1:17:27]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Epsilon-greedy is not a full uncertainty model. It is a cheap way to keep learning about actions you would otherwise never try. Source: `[11:02]` to `[15:20]`.
- The instability warning is structural, not cosmetic. The Bellman operator contracts, but function approximation can expand, so the combined loop can wobble. Source: `[1:03:46]` to `[1:05:02]`.
- DQN did not win because the deep net was magical. The lecture says the replay buffer was the dominant stabilizer, with fixed targets as the second guardrail. Source: `[1:14:17]` to `[1:16:49]`.
- Keeping a second network does not change compute much. It mainly doubles memory because you are storing another parameter set. Source: `[1:12:26]` to `[1:13:55]`.
- The lecture's practical message is blunt: better data reuse can matter more than more expressive function approximators. Source: `[1:15:13]` to `[1:16:49]`.
