# L14 Monte Carlo Tree Search and AlphaZero

This lecture is about using computation to search better, not just to think more. The core move is to stop solving the whole MDP and instead spend the budget on the current state, then let search and self-play do the rest.

## Summary
- The lecture starts with a naïve baseline: roll out from the current state under a default policy, estimate Q by Monte Carlo, and take one improvement step. Source: `[9:38]` to `[13:39]`.
- Monte Carlo Tree Search replaces blind rollouts with a rooted search tree. Instead of enumerating every next state, it samples branches and backs up averages as an approximation to Bellman updates. Source: `[14:02]` to `[20:13]`.
- UCT turns every node into a bandit problem. The algorithm uses upper confidence bounds to decide which action to expand next, which makes the tree intentionally unbalanced. Source: `[24:43]` to `[34:49]`.
- Go is the motivating case because it is adversarial, sparse-reward, and too large for full search. Self-play gives dense signal, and AlphaZero combines that with a policy/value network. Source: `[35:14]` to `[55:15]`.
- The final takeaway is empirical: architecture matters, search matters, and the combination matters more than either piece alone. Source: `[1:04:10]` to `[1:12:29]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Monte Carlo evaluation.** Start from the current state, roll out under a policy, and estimate the Q-value of the first action. Source: `[10:03]` to `[13:22]`.
- **Expectimax / Bellman backups.** States average over successor states; actions take a max over action values. Source: `[14:02]` to `[16:47]`.
- **Monte Carlo Tree Search.** Replace full expansion with sampled branches, then use the tree itself to prioritize where to spend more simulations. Source: `[18:59]` to `[20:13]`.
- **UCT.** Each state node behaves like a bandit; the UCB term pushes exploration toward actions that are promising but underexplored. Source: `[24:43]` to `[34:30]`.
- **Selective expansion.** The tree grows unevenly, because high-value branches get more visits and low-value branches get starved. Source: `[31:39]` to `[34:30]`.
- **Self-play.** Two copies of the same agent produce a well-matched opponent and a much denser reward signal than mismatched play. Source: `[41:50]` to `[46:40]`.
- **Policy network / value network.** AlphaZero uses one network with two heads: one for action priors, one for state value. Source: `[51:04]` to `[55:15]`.
- **Leaf evaluation.** Instead of rolling all the way to terminal every time, the search can stop at a leaf and plug in V(s). Source: `[58:46]` to `[59:39]`.
- **Root policy from visit counts.** After search, the action distribution at the root comes from visit counts plus a temperature. Source: `[1:01:08]` to `[1:03:03]`.
- **Search-train loop.** MCTS generates targets; the network trains on those targets; the next round of self-play gets better. Source: `[1:04:10]` to `[1:05:54]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- MCTS is not "planning everywhere." It is a compute-allocation strategy for one current state. Source: `[8:38]` to `[9:31]`.
- Self-play works because it keeps the game at the right difficulty. If one side is much stronger, reward density collapses. Source: `[43:13]` to `[46:40]`.
- The policy network is not a replacement for search. The lecture shows a large gap between the raw net and net-plus-MCTS. Source: `[1:10:41]` to `[1:11:29]`.
- Better architectures matter a lot. The same training data produces very different results depending on how the network is structured. Source: `[1:09:23]` to `[1:10:21]`.
- AlphaZero is learning to search, not just learning to play Go. The search traces become training data for the network. Source: `[1:04:10]` to `[1:05:54]`.

## Connections
- To bandits: UCT is literally a tree of bandits. Source: `[26:19]` to `[27:19]`.
- To dynamic programming: if the state and action spaces are small, plain Bellman backups are still the right answer. Source: `[21:57]` to `[22:30]`.
- To adversarial games: Go becomes minimax because the opponent is not stochastic, it is strategic. Source: `[38:03]` to `[39:52]`.
- To later DeepMind work: the same search machinery later shows up in AlphaTensor-style algorithm search. Source: `[1:12:37]` to `[1:13:08]`.
