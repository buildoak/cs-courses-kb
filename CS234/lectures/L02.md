# L02 Tabular MDP Planning

This lecture cleans up the discount-factor confusion, then builds the full planning stack: MRP evaluation, MDP policy evaluation, policy iteration, value iteration, and the contraction proof that makes the whole thing work.

## Summary
- The lecture opens by correcting the discount-factor intuition: larger gamma makes long-term rewards matter more, not less. Source: `[0:57]` to `[3:52]`.
- It shows how to evaluate an MRP both analytically with a matrix inverse and iteratively with dynamic programming. Source: `[8:03]` to `[13:42]`.
- It then folds an MDP and a policy into an MRP, which makes policy evaluation a special case of the earlier machinery. Source: `[16:01]` to `[18:22]`.
- Policy iteration and value iteration are both correct in the tabular discounted setting, but they differ in short-term behavior; the Bellman operator contraction proof explains why value iteration still converges when gamma is below 1. Source: `[47:06]` to `[1:05:06]`.
- The lecture ends by showing finite-horizon value iteration and rollout-based evaluation as practical variants. Source: `[1:08:13]` to `[1:11:35]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Discount factor gamma.** A larger gamma weights future reward more heavily. Source: `[0:57]` to `[3:52]`.
- **MRP.** A Markov reward process is a Markov chain plus rewards and discounting. Source: `[16:27]` to `[17:21]`.
- **Analytic solution.** If the model is small enough, `V = (I - gamma P)^-1 R`. Source: `[10:04]` to `[11:29]`.
- **Dynamic programming.** Iterative Bellman backups avoid explicit matrix inversion. Source: `[12:50]` to `[13:42]`.
- **Policy evaluation.** An MDP plus a fixed policy reduces to an MRP. Source: `[16:01]` to `[18:22]`.
- **Policy iteration.** Evaluate, improve, repeat; the policy improves monotonically until it cannot improve. Source: `[47:06]` to `[49:16]`.
- **Value iteration.** Apply the Bellman optimality backup repeatedly, then extract a policy at the end. Source: `[50:04]` to `[57:26]`.
- **Contraction.** The Bellman operator shrinks value-function distance by at most gamma. Source: `[57:46]` to `[1:05:06]`.
- **Finite horizon.** Time-indexed value iteration gives a policy sequence, not just one stationary policy. Source: `[1:08:13]` to `[1:09:03]`.
- **Rollout evaluation.** When the explicit sum is expensive, simulate the policy and average returns. Source: `[1:09:20]` to `[1:11:35]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Value iteration and policy iteration are both asymptotically correct, but the one-state/one-action example shows that finite-time behavior can be completely different. Source: `[5:26]` to `[8:05]`.
- The contraction proof depends on aligning the action choice across the two value functions before bounding the difference. Source: `[1:00:21]` to `[1:04:03]`.
- Policy iteration has monotonic improvement; value iteration does not guarantee that the extracted policy improves at every round. Source: `[1:04:07]` to `[1:05:32]`.
- In finite-horizon problems, the best policy can depend on the time step, so stationary policies are not automatic. Source: `[1:08:13]` to `[1:12:08]`.
- Rollouts are the pragmatic escape hatch when the model sum is too big or the world is easier to simulate than to sum. Source: `[1:09:20]` to `[1:11:35]`.
