# L10 Offline RL and Counterfactual Evaluation

Offline RL is framed as a support problem before it is framed as a learning problem -- the lecture keeps asking whether fixed logged data can support a better policy than imitation learning, and where that hope breaks.

## Summary
- The lecture opens with DPO vs RLHF to make the core offline-learning point: when you extrapolate beyond the behavior policy, the policy can look clever while quietly leaving the data manifold. Source: `[0:05]` to `[5:38]`.
- It asks the real question directly: can offline RL beat imitation learning? Yes, but only if the logged data has enough coverage and the target policy stays inside support. Source: `[5:01]` to `[19:12]`.
- The middle of the lecture splits the task into policy evaluation and policy learning, then walks through model-based evaluation, fitted Q evaluation, and importance sampling as different ways to estimate value from logged data. Source: `[19:39]` to `[1:05:01]`.
- The back half is about what to do when the data is weak: pessimism under uncertainty, support constraints, BCQ, marginalized behavior-supported policy optimization, and conservative Q-learning. Source: `[1:06:00]` to `[1:17:54]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Offline / batch / counterfactual RL.** Learn from fixed historical data, not from fresh interaction with the environment. Source: `[15:29]` to `[16:27]`.
- **Behavior policy.** The policy that generated the dataset; the learned policy has to respect its coverage. Source: `[16:19]` to `[16:52]`.
- **Support overlap.** If the dataset never visits a region, the learned policy has no evidence there. Source: `[1:06:36]` to `[1:09:13]`.
- **Model-based evaluation.** Learn a dynamics or reward model, then plan in the surrogate world. Source: `[21:49]` to `[22:33]`.
- **Fitted Q evaluation.** Evaluate a fixed policy with Bellman backups over logged tuples. Source: `[31:49]` to `[33:21]`.
- **Fitted Q iteration.** The control cousin of fitted Q evaluation, closely related to deep Q-learning. Source: `[32:41]` to `[34:24]`.
- **Importance sampling.** Correct for distribution mismatch with trajectory weights, at the cost of variance. Source: `[38:02]` to `[39:05]`.
- **Per-decision importance sampling.** Move the weights inside the trajectory to soften some long-horizon variance. Source: `[1:03:55]` to `[1:04:23]`.
- **Confounding.** If the features that drove the logging action are hidden, the estimator is biased. Source: `[44:43]` to `[46:01]`.
- **Pessimism under uncertainty.** Penalize uncertain regions instead of trusting extrapolation. Source: `[1:09:18]` to `[1:10:31]`.
- **BCQ / MBSPO / CQL.** Concrete offline methods that constrain the learned policy to the data support or a pessimistic neighborhood around it. Source: `[18:20]` to `[19:12]`, `[1:14:17]` to `[1:17:24]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The real failure mode is not just low data volume -- it is mismatch between the data support and the target policy's reach. Source: `[1:06:36]` to `[1:09:13]`.
- Importance sampling is only honest if the logged action was chosen from observed features. Hidden confounders break the counterfactual story. Source: `[44:43]` to `[46:01]`.
- Optimistic initialization is easy to write down in a table and ugly in a neural net. Source: `[24:27]` to `[27:51]`.
- Model-based offline RL is not automatically safer than model-free offline RL; it just moves the uncertainty into the model and the planner. Source: `[1:16:18]` to `[1:16:41]`.
- Offline RL can beat imitation learning because the dataset may contain better actions than the logging policy actually took. Source: `[18:06]` to `[19:12]`, `[1:15:52]` to `[1:16:03]`.
