# L12 Bayesian Bandits and Thompson Sampling

The lecture starts as a recap of UCB and PAC, then switches to Bayesian bandits: priors, posteriors, Thompson sampling, and the clean idea that you can explore by sampling the world you believe in.

## Summary
- The first half tightens the exploration story from the previous lecture: too-conservative bonuses slow exploration, and optimistic initialization is fragile once function approximation enters. Source: `[0:05]` to `[27:51]`.
- The lecture then revisits PAC, before switching to Bayesian bandits and priors over arm rewards. Source: `[28:47]` to `[46:06]`.
- Thompson sampling implements probability matching by sampling a reward parameter for each arm from the posterior and acting greedily on the sample. Source: `[47:51]` to `[1:02:39]`.
- Bayesian regret is not the same object as frequentist regret, so the two families of bounds are not directly comparable. Source: `[1:03:08]` to `[1:05:22]`.
- The lecture closes with delayed feedback, contextual bandits, the POMDP view, and Gittins as the optimal Bayesian index policy. Source: `[1:05:30]` to `[1:16:39]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Prior.** The belief over each arm's reward parameter before seeing data. Source: `[37:08]` to `[38:07]`.
- **Posterior.** The updated belief after observing rewards. Source: `[45:35]` to `[46:06]`.
- **Conjugate update.** The Beta-Bernoulli example keeps the posterior in the same family after each sample. Source: `[45:35]` to `[46:06]`, `[55:58]` to `[56:26]`.
- **Probability matching.** Sample actions in proportion to how likely they are to be best. Source: `[47:51]` to `[49:51]`.
- **Thompson sampling.** Sample a reward parameter for each arm from the posterior, then pick the argmax. Source: `[49:33]` to `[52:22]`.
- **Bayesian regret.** Expected regret under a prior over the unknown parameters. Source: `[1:03:08]` to `[1:03:46]`.
- **Frequentist regret.** Regret measured against a fixed but unknown arm set. Source: `[1:03:08]` to `[1:03:27]`.
- **Delayed feedback.** Feedback arrives too late to update the next decision immediately. Source: `[1:06:44]` to `[1:07:59]`.
- **Contextual bandit.** A bandit with features on the user and the item, but still no sequential state transitions. Source: `[1:05:45]` to `[1:06:17]`.
- **POMDP view.** The hidden state is the parameter belief, not the environment state. Source: `[1:14:19]` to `[1:15:05]`.
- **Index policy.** A policy that reduces each arm to a single scalar score. Source: `[1:15:13]` to `[1:16:00]`.
- **Gittins index.** The optimal Bayesian index policy for discounted bandits. Source: `[1:16:07]` to `[1:16:39]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Thompson sampling keeps exploring even when no new feedback has arrived, because the posterior is resampled at every decision. Source: `[1:12:15]` to `[1:12:48]`.
- A bad prior can be worse than a deterministic optimism rule; TS is only as good as the prior support. Source: `[1:11:23]` to `[1:12:15]`.
- Bayesian and frequentist regret solve different problems, so the lecture refuses to compare their bounds as if they were interchangeable. Source: `[1:03:08]` to `[1:05:22]`.
- UCB and Thompson sampling are not the same idea in disguise -- UCB is deterministic optimism, TS is posterior randomness. Source: `[1:11:44]` to `[1:12:15]`.
- Gittins is optimal in the Bayesian discounted bandit setting, but the history dependence is exactly why people still reach for Thompson sampling. Source: `[1:15:13]` to `[1:16:39]`.
