# L08 Imitation Learning, MaxEnt IRL, and RLHF

This lecture is the bridge from demonstrations to preference data. It starts with imitation learning, turns demonstrations into a reward-recovery problem, then walks the path from human feedback to the ChatGPT-style RLHF pipeline.

## Summary
- The lecture frames human help as a spectrum: demonstrations, constant coaching, and pairwise preferences sit on different points of the same axis. Source: `[50:51]` to `[58:00]`.
- Maximum entropy inverse reinforcement learning turns expert trajectories into a normalized exponential distribution over trajectories and then fits reward by maximum likelihood. Source: `[18:44]` to `[30:42]`.
- The classical MaxEnt IRL algorithm alternates reward optimization, policy solving, and state-visitation frequency estimation, and the standard version assumes access to the dynamics model. Source: `[41:43]` to `[50:17]`.
- Pairwise preferences are the practical middle ground: less work than DAGGER, richer than demos, and easy to fit with a Bradley-Terry style logistic model. Source: `[57:38]` to `[1:09:20]`.
- RLHF for ChatGPT is laid out as pretraining, supervised fine-tuning, preference collection, reward modeling, and PPO, with the reference model acting as the guardrail. Source: `[1:12:03]` to `[1:13:52]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Expert demonstrations.** The data is just state-action trajectories, not reward labels. Source: `[6:08]` to `[7:17]`.
- **Behavior cloning.** A direct reduction of RL to supervised learning on expert state-action pairs. Source: `[49:39]` to `[50:37]`.
- **Maximum entropy.** Among all reward-compatible trajectory distributions, pick the one with highest entropy. Source: `[18:44]` to `[27:06]`.
- **Lagrange multiplier form.** The max-entropy solution becomes proportional to `exp(r_phi(tau))`. Source: `[23:12]` to `[27:01]`.
- **Reward fitting.** With that structural form in hand, learning the reward becomes maximum likelihood over observed expert trajectories. Source: `[27:19]` to `[30:42]`.
- **State visitation frequencies.** The classical algorithm needs them to update reward parameters. Source: `[43:25]` to `[49:07]`.
- **Dynamics model.** The old formulation needs it for policy solving and visitation counts, but not for the final gradient once those are computed. Source: `[44:41]` to `[49:07]`.
- **Human feedback spectrum.** DAGGER and TAMER are active coaching; pairwise comparisons are the lighter-weight middle. Source: `[53:32]` to `[58:00]`.
- **Bradley-Terry / logistic preference model.** Pairwise preference data becomes a standard cross-entropy problem. Source: `[1:07:36]` to `[1:08:35]`.
- **RLHF pipeline.** Pretrain, supervised fine-tune, collect preferences, learn a reward model, then run PPO. Source: `[1:12:03]` to `[1:13:52]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- MaxEnt IRL does not claim to recover the true human reward. It finds a reward function compatible with the demos and chosen by a maximum-entropy prior. Source: `[50:01]` to `[52:23]`.
- The dynamics model matters for solving the reward-learning loop, not for the reward gradient itself. That split is easy to miss. Source: `[44:41]` to `[49:07]`.
- Pairwise preferences are a sweet spot because they ask for relative judgments, not dense per-step supervision or a full scalar reward. Source: `[57:38]` to `[58:00]`.
- The reward model and the downstream policy can decouple badly. Good reward scores do not guarantee good control. Source: `[56:43]` to `[57:10]`.
- In RLHF, the reference model is not decorative. It keeps the preference-trained reward model from drifting into out-of-distribution territory. Source: `[22:05]` to `[25:25]`.
- Instruction tuning comes first mostly so the model produces sensible responses that humans can compare. Source: `[1:12:03]` to `[1:13:05]`.

## Connections
- To L07: behavior cloning and DAGGER are the immediate baselines; MaxEnt IRL is what comes next when you want to infer the reward rather than mimic actions. Source: `[49:39]` to `[58:00]`.
- To L09: DPO collapses the reward-model plus PPO split that RLHF uses here. Source: `[1:12:03]` to `[1:13:52]`.
- To modern LLM training: supervised fine-tuning is not the end state, it is the reference policy used to make preference learning stable. Source: `[16:19]` to `[17:04]`, `[22:05]` to `[23:20]`.
- To sample efficiency: the backflip example shows that a small amount of preference data can replace enormous amounts of environment interaction. Source: `[1:09:42]` to `[1:11:09]`.
