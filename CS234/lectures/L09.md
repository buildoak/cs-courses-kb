# L09 Direct Preference Optimization

This guest lecture is the DPO story: start from RLHF, write the KL-regularized objective in closed form, cancel the partition function inside the pairwise loss, and train directly on preferences instead of maintaining an explicit reward model.

## Summary
- The lecture begins with an RLHF recap: supervised fine-tuning, preference collection, reward modeling, then PPO with a KL penalty to keep the policy near the reference model. Source: `[5:03]` to `[17:16]`, `[22:05]` to `[25:25]`.
- DPO exploits the closed-form relation between the reward and the optimal policy under the KL-regularized RLHF objective, which lets the method train directly on preferences. Source: `[27:43]` to `[33:18]`.
- The partition function cancels inside the pairwise loss, so DPO becomes a clean logistic objective over preferred vs dispreferred responses. Source: `[31:00]` to `[33:18]`.
- The guest speakers argue that DPO scales: open-model leaderboards, Mistral, and LLaMA 3 all point in the same direction. Source: `[40:39]` to `[41:22]`.
- The main failure modes are verbosity drift, reward hacking, and non-transitive preferences, which push the discussion toward Nash-style objectives and multi-objective DPO. Source: `[45:10]` to `[46:53]`, `[59:39]` to `[1:04:13]`, `[1:14:14]` to `[1:18:28]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Bradley-Terry model.** Pairwise preferences are modeled as a probability that one response beats another. Source: `[2:32]` to `[5:03]`.
- **SFT reference model.** The supervised fine-tuned model is the reference distribution for RLHF and DPO. Source: `[16:17]` to `[17:16]`.
- **Preference pairs.** DPO and RLHF both train from `yw`/`yl` comparisons, not scalar labels over the full space. Source: `[5:27]` to `[6:26]`, `[31:19]` to `[32:48]`.
- **KL penalty / beta.** The temperature term controls how far the optimized policy can move from the reference model. Source: `[27:43]` to `[30:01]`, `[1:02:36]` to `[1:03:03]`.
- **Partition function.** The intractable normalizer disappears when the reward difference is written as a preference loss. Source: `[29:05]` to `[33:18]`.
- **Implicit reward.** DPO fits an implicit reward function whose optimal policy can be extracted analytically. Source: `[42:51]` to `[43:32]`.
- **RewardBench.** The lecture uses reward benchmarks to argue that DPO's implicit reward is competitive with classic RLHF. Source: `[43:48]` to `[44:51]`.
- **Reward hacking.** DPO can push probability mass far outside the training distribution, especially toward verbose outputs. Source: `[45:10]` to `[46:53]`.
- **Exploration.** The important exploration happens when you collect preference data, not while you optimize the policy. Source: `[55:08]` to `[55:57]`.
- **Nash optimization.** When preferences are not transitive, the lecture points to direct Nash-style objectives instead of scalar reward maximization. Source: `[1:00:04]` to `[1:01:54]`, `[1:14:14]` to `[1:15:56]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- DPO and PPO are not competing goals. Under the same implicit reward and perfect optimization, they land on the same policy; in practice, the optimization path changes the outcome. Source: `[43:01]` to `[43:44]`.
- The lecture's strongest claim is not "DPO is simpler." It is that the implicit reward extracted by DPO seems good enough to match explicit reward models at scale. Source: `[43:48]` to `[44:51]`.
- What looks like a weakness in DPO can act as regularization. The imperfect optimizer can keep the model from following a brittle reward model too aggressively. Source: `[43:37]` to `[45:04]`.
- Reward-model quality and downstream policy quality can diverge badly. High reward-model scores can coexist with catastrophic verbosity or reward hacking. Source: `[45:56]` to `[46:53]`, `[59:12]` to `[59:37]`.
- The lecture treats preference collection as the real exploration budget. If you sample the wrong training responses, no amount of later policy optimization saves you. Source: `[55:08]` to `[55:57]`.
- Classical reward maximization is not the only objective. If preferences are cyclic or partial-order only, a Nash-style view is the cleaner formulation. Source: `[1:00:04]` to `[1:02:08]`.
