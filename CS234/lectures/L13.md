# L13 Efficient Exploration in MDPs

The lecture treats exploration as a computation problem before it treats it as a learning problem. The through-line is simple: use structure, or use priors, or use meta-learning, but do not waste samples on blind search.

## Summary
- The opening story is the Breakout grading problem: if you can learn a fast exploratory policy, you can replace slow human grading with an agent that probes the environment and checks rubric items. Source: `[7:42]` to `[9:49]`.
- The lecture lifts bandit ideas to tabular MDPs. MBIE-EB adds optimism bonuses to Bellman backups, which pushes the policy toward underexplored state-action pairs and gives a PAC-style guarantee. Source: `[10:17]` to `[20:26]`.
- The Simulation Lemma is the bridge from model error to value error. If reward and transition estimates are bounded, the Q-function error is bounded too. Source: `[21:39]` to `[31:57]`.
- Bayesian exploration comes next: PSRL samples a whole MDP from a posterior, plans in that sampled world, and commits for the episode to avoid thrashing. Source: `[34:14]` to `[54:05]`.
- The back half moves into structure and generalization: contextual bandits, linear UCB, pseudo-counts, deep exploration, and meta-exploration via DREAM and decision pre-trained transformers. Source: `[55:44]` to `[1:09:11]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Optimism under uncertainty.** MBIE-EB adds a bonus to Q backups that gets larger when visit counts are small. Source: `[11:23]` to `[17:18]`.
- **PAC in MDPs.** The theorem says the policy only makes finitely many substantial mistakes, but the bound is conservative enough to be almost comic. Source: `[18:13]` to `[20:23]`.
- **Simulation Lemma.** Bound the reward-model error and dynamics-model error, and you get a bound on the Q-function error for a fixed policy. Source: `[21:39]` to `[31:57]`.
- **Bayesian MDPs.** The tabular case lets you keep conjugate priors over rewards and transitions, then sample a complete MDP from the posterior. Source: `[34:14]` to `[41:33]`.
- **PSRL.** Sample once per episode, solve the sampled MDP, act on it for the whole episode, then update the posterior. Source: `[38:00]` to `[39:01]`.
- **Dirichlet dynamics model.** Multinomial transitions get a Dirichlet prior, which keeps posterior updates closed-form. Source: `[39:24]` to `[41:33]`.
- **Commitment matters.** If you resample too often inside one episode, the agent can thrash between incompatible MDP beliefs. Source: `[46:03]` to `[48:37]`.
- **Seed sampling.** In concurrent RL, different agents commit to different sampled MDPs so they coordinate exploration instead of duplicating it. Source: `[49:11]` to `[55:18]`.
- **Contextual bandits and linear UCB.** Feature sharing across actions prevents regret from exploding when the action space gets large. Source: `[55:44]` to `[1:01:03]`.
- **Pseudo-counts.** Deep RL needs an uncertainty surrogate that generalizes beyond raw counts, because Atari-style state spaces are effectively infinite. Source: `[1:01:08]` to `[1:04:54]`.
- **Meta-exploration.** DREAM and decision pre-trained transformers try to learn exploration structure across many tasks and approximate the effect of Thompson sampling. Source: `[1:06:01]` to `[1:09:11]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Exploration is not just "try more things." It is "spend computation where information value is highest." Source: `[14:02]` to `[17:18]`, `[25:06]` to `[26:08]`.
- The PAC proof is less interesting than the Simulation Lemma that supports it. The lemma is the reusable bit. Source: `[21:39]` to `[31:57]`.
- Bayesian updating inside an episode can be a bug, not a feature. Too-frequent posterior refreshes make the policy unstable. Source: `[46:03]` to `[48:37]`.
- Deep exploration needs structure. Count bonuses, uncertainty sets, and last-layer Bayesian tricks are all ways to smuggle structure back into large models. Source: `[1:01:08]` to `[1:05:39]`.
- Meta-learning can discover the same inductive bias you would otherwise hard-code, which is the point of the DPT example. Source: `[1:06:20]` to `[1:09:11]`.
