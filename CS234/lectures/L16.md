# L16 Value Alignment and RL Safety

The final lecture is half course recap, half research agenda: RL is useful when actions change the data you later see, but that same property is why safety, distribution shift, and robust deployment stay hard.

## Summary
- The lecture opens with quiz review, then restates the course thesis: RL is about learning through experience to make good long-term decisions, and you should only use it when the problem actually has that shape. Source: `[17:12]` to `[18:58]`.
- Value alignment is framed as a real tension between autonomy, paternalism, and broader moral theory, not as a simple "obey the user" problem. Source: `[3:40]` to `[8:13]`.
- The lecture closes the loop on the LLM alignment pipeline: human reward / preference data, PPO, and DPO are all part of the same story. Source: `[11:45]` to `[12:15]`.
- Safety-critical examples are handled with simulators, trusted regions, and reward penalties for unsafe or untrusted states; the COVID-testing example adds delayed outcomes, budget limits, and fairness constraints. Source: `[40:51]` to `[51:40]`.
- The final section is a future-work map: better formulations than plain MDPs, hybrid offline-online learning, richer feedback, multi-agent settings, and more robust off-the-shelf methods. Source: `[1:02:53]` to `[1:09:25]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Autonomy.** The lecture treats autonomy as a genuine objective, not a rhetorical garnish. Source: `[4:21]` to `[8:13]`.
- **Paternalism.** A system may block or steer choices if the objective includes protection or long-term welfare, even when that conflicts with immediate preference. Source: `[5:14]` to `[5:35]`.
- **Preference elicitation.** Human comparisons over prompt-output pairs are a reward signal, not just a ranking trick. Source: `[11:45]` to `[11:53]`.
- **PPO.** The optimization step used after collecting human preferences in the alignment pipeline. Source: `[11:51]` to `[12:15]`.
- **DPO.** The other fine-tuning method the course already covered, used here as part of the same alignment story. Source: `[12:12]` to `[12:15]`.
- **Distribution shift.** The core RL problem: new policies change the state-action distribution you later learn from. Source: `[52:52]` to `[57:16]`.
- **Simulator.** A high-fidelity but imperfect surrogate that lets you optimize offline before risking the real system. Source: `[41:48]` to `[42:28]`.
- **Trusted region.** A state-space region the policy can optimize in with more confidence. Source: `[44:53]` to `[46:40]`.
- **Pessimism.** Penalize uncertain or unsafe regions to avoid optimistic extrapolation. Source: `[45:02]` to `[46:40]`, `[57:01]` to `[57:16]`.
- **Model / value / policy.** Three different places to represent uncertainty; the lecture argues models are often the cleanest because prediction is narrower than action selection. Source: `[57:39]` to `[1:00:21]`.
- **Data vs computation efficiency.** In simulation, the two can trade off; in real systems with latency and forced decisions, they cannot. Source: `[1:01:07]` to `[1:02:53]`.
- **Hybrid offline-online learning.** Many real systems have a large offline corpus plus a small online budget. Source: `[54:07]` to `[54:20]`, `[1:04:42]` to `[1:04:53]`.
- **Multi-agent settings.** Real environments often include teachers, adversaries, and cooperative stakeholders. Source: `[1:07:28]` to `[1:07:45]`.
- **Richer feedback.** Thumbs, preference pairs, and language feedback are all plausible successors to scalar reward. Source: `[1:06:39]` to `[1:07:06]`.
- **System identification.** One alternative decomposition: learn the dynamics first, then plan. Source: `[1:07:45]` to `[1:08:12]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Safety here is mostly objective design. In plasma control, the lecture does not wait for a perfect theorem; it shapes the reward to push the policy away from risky or untrusted regions. Source: `[44:53]` to `[46:40]`.
- Constraining search can erase distribution shift. AlphaTensor is presented as a case where the search space is restricted to correct algorithms, so the optimizer only ranks valid candidates. Source: `[36:31]` to `[38:23]`.
- Delayed rewards change what counts as usable supervision. In COVID border testing, the downstream outcome matters more, but it arrives too late to optimize directly, so short-term proxies become necessary. Source: `[48:50]` to `[51:40]`.
- Models are uncertainty containers. The lecture argues that uncertainty is easier to represent at the prediction layer than at the policy layer because prediction is a narrower problem. Source: `[58:50]` to `[1:00:02]`.
- Data and compute only look separate in toy settings. In simulation, more compute can stand in for more samples; in real systems, the hidden action is often "whatever happens because you delayed." Source: `[1:01:07]` to `[1:02:47]`.
- The endgame is not one algorithm. The future-work list is really an engineering checklist: robust defaults, automatic model selection, better abstractions, and a more honest representation of real constraints. Source: `[1:03:09]` to `[1:09:25]`.
