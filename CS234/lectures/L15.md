# L15 Value Alignment: Preferences, Interests, and Morality

This lecture is not about making a model smarter. It is about deciding what the model should optimize for in the first place, and how badly those targets can diverge once you start being precise.

## Summary
- The lecture opens by separating alignment targets: user intentions, user preferences, user best interests, and morality are related, but they are not the same thing. Source: `[28:22]` to `[41:38]`, `[59:01]` to `[1:01:14]`.
- The paperclip AI example shows how a clean objective can turn into a catastrophic specification game once optimization power enters the picture. Source: `[20:45]` to `[24:53]`.
- Preference alignment looks attractive because it can lean on revealed preferences, IRL, RLHF, and preference optimization, but finite data and rare edge cases make inference underdetermined. Source: `[33:17]` to `[35:31]`, `[47:04]` to `[48:00]`.
- Best-interest alignment introduces autonomy and paternalism. Sometimes what is best for the user is not what the user wants in the moment. Source: `[36:28]` to `[58:10]`.
- Moral alignment opens a bigger argument: consequentialism, utilitarianism, prioritarianism, deontology, and common-sense morality disagree about what "right" means. Source: `[1:01:14]` to `[1:13:16]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Intent alignment.** The agent should infer the user's actual meaning from under-specified instructions. Source: `[28:22]` to `[30:17]`.
- **Preference alignment.** The agent should do what the user actually prefers, even if the user is bad at expressing it. Source: `[33:17]` to `[35:31]`.
- **Revealed preferences.** Behavior and feedback become the evidence used to infer the target. Source: `[34:07]` to `[34:40]`.
- **Inverse RL / RLHF.** These are the practical tools the lecture names for learning from human behavior or feedback. Source: `[34:18]` to `[34:40]`.
- **Best-interest alignment.** The target is the user's objective good, not just their stated wants. Source: `[35:33]` to `[41:38]`.
- **Autonomy and paternalism.** A system can be too helpful if it overrides the user's right to choose badly. Source: `[40:01]` to `[40:49]`.
- **Moral alignment.** The agent should do what is morally right, not just what the user wants. Source: `[1:00:04]` to `[1:01:14]`.
- **Consequentialism / utilitarianism.** Right action is the one with the best net outcomes. Source: `[1:03:01]` to `[1:04:08]`.
- **Prioritarianism / maximin / deontology.** Moral theories disagree on whether to maximize total good, help the worst off, or respect rules and rights. Source: `[1:04:59]` to `[1:08:51]`.
- **Common-sense morality.** A pragmatic target: behave like a normal human would, even if that leaves hard edge cases unresolved. Source: `[1:10:12]` to `[1:13:16]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Better instructions do not solve alignment by themselves. The gap is not just verbosity; it is under-specification. Source: `[24:24]` to `[27:13]`.
- Preference-based alignment avoids paternalism, but it can also trap the user in their own biases and convenience loops. Source: `[55:44]` to `[58:10]`.
- "Best interest" is not a purely empirical target. The lecture explicitly says it requires moral philosophy, not just better data. Source: `[37:02]` to `[39:47]`.
- Moral theory can produce grotesque edge cases. The surgeon example is the warning sign. Source: `[1:11:16]` to `[1:12:18]`.
- Common-sense morality may be less "correct" in theory, but more predictable and socially legible in practice. Source: `[1:12:25]` to `[1:13:16]`.

## Connections
