# L03 Policy Evaluation

The lecture moves from planning with models to learning from experience. It compares Monte Carlo, TD(0), certainty equivalence, and batch evaluation, then uses a tiny two-state example to show that finite-data fixed points can diverge sharply.

## Summary
- The lecture shifts from model-based planning to policy evaluation from experience under a fixed policy. Source: `[8:48]` to `[11:49]`.
- Monte Carlo policy evaluation averages complete returns, which makes it simple and model-free but episodic. Source: `[14:20]` to `[18:07]`.
- Temporal difference learning bootstraps from the next-state estimate and trades lower variance for stronger Markov structure. Source: `[36:57]` to `[1:00:30]`.
- Certainty equivalence builds a maximum-likelihood model from data, then runs dynamic programming on that model. Source: `[1:02:22]` to `[1:03:21]`.
- In batch evaluation, Monte Carlo and TD(0) can converge to different answers on the same finite dataset. Source: `[1:05:34]` to `[1:18:53]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Return G.** The discounted sum of rewards from a state onward. Source: `[12:12]` to `[12:32]`.
- **Bootstrapping.** Use an estimated value inside the target to form the next estimate. Source: `[13:37]` to `[14:17]`.
- **First-visit MC.** Update a state once per episode, on its first occurrence. Source: `[19:42]` to `[21:28]`.
- **Every-visit MC.** Update every time the state appears in the trajectory. Source: `[22:55]` to `[23:35]`.
- **Incremental MC.** Maintain a running average with a decaying step size. Source: `[23:35]` to `[35:07]`.
- **TD(0).** Update with `r + gamma V(s')` instead of a full return. Source: `[36:57]` to `[48:15]`.
- **Certainty equivalence.** Fit the MDP model from data, then ignore model error and plan with it. Source: `[1:02:22]` to `[1:03:08]`.
- **Batch policy evaluation.** Reuse the same dataset until the evaluation procedure reaches its batch fixed point. Source: `[1:05:34]` to `[1:18:53]`.
- **Q-learning.** The lecture explicitly calls Q-learning the control version of TD learning. Source: `[11:57]` to `[12:03]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Monte Carlo is model-free in a strong sense: if the features are not fully Markov, it still just averages observed returns. Source: `[16:43]` to `[18:07]`.
- TD is not just a noisy Monte Carlo variant. On batch data, it converges to the dynamic-programming solution of the maximum-likelihood MDP. Source: `[1:15:13]` to `[1:18:53]`.
- The two-state example splits the methods cleanly: TD gets `V(B)=0.75` and `V(A)=0.75`, while Monte Carlo gets `V(B)=0.75` and `V(A)=0`. Source: `[1:08:38]` to `[1:18:53]`.
- Rare states are the weak point for Monte Carlo. If you barely see a state, you barely update it. Source: `[21:33]` to `[22:44]`.
- Batch TD propagates information through the Markov structure; Monte Carlo only fits the returns it has seen. Source: `[1:18:00]` to `[1:18:53]`.

## Connections
- To L02: Monte Carlo and TD are the data-driven counterparts to Bellman-style dynamic programming. Source: `[12:39]` to `[13:06]`, `[1:18:12]` to `[1:18:53]`.
