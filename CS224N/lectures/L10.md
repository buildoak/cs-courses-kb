# L10 Post-training (RLHF, SFT, DPO)

This lecture is the bridge from a pretrained decoder to a model that actually follows instructions.

## Summary
- The lecture starts from the scale story: pretraining made the GPT family useful enough that zero-shot and few-shot prompting became viable. Source: `[0:05]` to `[10:39]`.
- Prompt templates and in-context examples are treated as real control surfaces, not cosmetic tricks. The model is still just completing text, but the prompt can coerce it into answering questions, summarizing, or doing light reasoning. Source: `[9:38]` to `[18:40]`.
- Instruction fine-tuning reframes the target from "predict the next token" to "respond to user intent across many tasks," using large collections of instruction-output pairs. Source: `[22:29]` to `[26:05]`.
- RLHF adds a learned reward model on top of instruction tuning, then optimizes the policy against that preference signal. Source: `[41:43]` to `[55:53]`.
- DPO removes the explicit reinforcement-learning loop and turns preference optimization into a simpler direct fit, which is why the lecture treats it as the practical successor in many open-source stacks. Source: `[56:51]` to `[1:19:18]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Zero-shot prompting.** Use the pretrained model directly, with no examples, and rely on the fact that scale has made the model surprisingly capable. Source: `[8:52]` to `[9:33]`.
- **Few-shot prompting.** Provide a few demonstrations in the prompt and let the model infer the pattern. Source: `[9:41]` to `[10:19]`.
- **In-context learning.** The model adapts to the task from the prompt itself, without parameter updates. Source: `[9:41]` to `[10:19]`.
- **Prompt templates.** Small changes in prompt wording can change behavior materially, so prompt design is part of the system. Source: `[16:46]` to `[18:40]`.
- **Chain-of-thought prompting.** A prompt can ask the model to expose intermediate reasoning or a stepwise answer. Source: `[17:48]` to `[18:40]`.
- **Instruction fine-tuning.** Train on many instruction-output pairs across tasks so the model generalizes to user intent instead of one narrow benchmark. Source: `[23:35]` to `[25:17]`.
- **Reward model.** A separate model predicts how much a human would like an answer. Source: `[42:05]` to `[44:18]`.
- **RLHF.** Instruction tuning + reward modeling + reinforcement learning over preferences. Source: `[41:43]` to `[55:53]`.
- **DPO.** Fit preference data directly with a classification-style objective, skipping the RL step. Source: `[58:10]` to `[1:00:00]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The model can look "aligned" before it really is. Prompting can coerce useful behavior out of a pretrained model without changing what the model was trained to do. Source: `[9:41]` to `[10:19]`, `[22:35]` to `[23:17]`.
- Instruction tuning is a scale change, not a new architecture. The lecture keeps the same decoder-only model and changes the data and objective around it. Source: `[23:35]` to `[25:01]`.
- Reward modeling is mostly a compression of human preferences into a scalar. The lecture is explicit that the expensive part is the human comparison data, not a clever new architecture. Source: `[42:05]` to `[44:18]`.
- DPO works because the preference signal is already the extra information. Once you have the comparisons, the RL loop is not the only way to use them. Source: `[58:10]` to `[59:53]`.
- The lecture treats ChatGPT as an accumulation of mundane steps, not magic: more data, better tuning, then preference optimization. Source: `[1:13:56]` to `[1:14:22]`.
