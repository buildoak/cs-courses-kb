# L01 Intro and Word Vectors

Lecture 1 starts with the course thesis and then keeps narrowing the lens until word meaning becomes a training objective.

## Summary
- The lecture frames CS224N as bottom-up NLP: learn the structure of human language, then build systems that actually work, not just theory sketches. Source: `[0:41]` to `[7:05]`.
- Word meaning is introduced as a distributional problem, not a dictionary problem: words pick up meaning from contexts, and word2vec is the first concrete model of that idea. Source: `[11:22]` to `[47:41]`.
- Word2vec is presented as a simple 2013 method that learns vectors from a corpus by maximizing nearby-word probabilities from random initialization. Source: `[47:07]` to `[1:04:20]`.
- The loss is converted into average negative log likelihood, then turned into a softmax over dot products so the model can be trained with gradients. Source: `[52:14]` to `[59:49]`.
- The lecture ends by deriving the center-vector gradient and showing the observed-minus-expected form that makes the update feel statistical and geometric at once. Source: `[1:07:00]` to `[1:19:57]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Corpus.** A body of text, not a single sentence, and the raw material for learning word vectors. Source: `[47:44]` to `[49:03]`.
- **Word type vs token.** One vector per type, while each token is one occurrence in context. Source: `[49:07]` to `[50:15]`.
- **Center word / outside words.** Word2vec walks a window over the corpus and predicts nearby words from the center. Source: `[49:25]` to `[51:20]`.
- **Distributional semantics.** Meaning comes from contexts and co-occurrence, not from a hand-built symbol table. Source: `[11:22]` to `[35:36]`.
- **Dot product as similarity.** A higher dot product means a higher co-occurrence score, which is then normalized into a probability. Source: `[56:03]` to `[59:05]`.
- **Softmax.** The exponentiate-plus-normalize trick that turns arbitrary real scores into a probability distribution over context words. Source: `[57:01]` to `[59:49]`.
- **Gradient descent.** Randomly initialize the vectors, compute gradients, and keep stepping downhill until the probabilities stop improving. Source: `[1:00:33]` to `[1:04:20]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The similarity relation is deliberately weird. `the` has to be close to basically any noun, so vector similarity is really "can these words live next to each other?". Source: `[56:14]` to `[56:46]`.
- The model is tiny in architecture but huge in parameter count. The lecture's 400,000-word, 100-dimension example already lands at 80 million parameters. Source: `[1:02:30]` to `[1:03:29]`.
- The gradient formula is not just algebraic cleanup. It becomes "observed minus expected", which is why the update reads like statistics wearing a geometry costume. Source: `[1:17:06]` to `[1:19:10]`.
- The lecture treats the result as magical on purpose: a simple objective over a large corpus really does recover semantic structure. Source: `[1:03:54]` to `[1:04:20]`.
