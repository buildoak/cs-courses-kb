# L08: Self-Attention and Transformers

Lecture 8 replaces recurrence with attention. The lecture is mostly about turning a clever idea into a stable, trainable architecture.

## Summary
- RNNs are limited by linear interaction distance and sequential dependence: far-apart tokens interact slowly, and GPU parallelism is poor. Source: `[4:38]` to `[8:45]`.
- Self-attention fixes that by letting each token query all others directly, so the whole sequence can be processed in parallel. Source: `[10:06]` to `[13:29]`.
- Position has to be added back in, because pure self-attention is permutation-invariant. The lecture presents sinusoidal and learned position embeddings as two ways to do it. Source: `[22:52]` to `[27:24]`.
- The transformer block is self-attention plus masking, feed-forward layers, residual connections, and layer normalization, repeated across layers. Source: `[39:13]` to `[40:44]`, `[58:20]` to `[1:07:23]`.
- Transformers win because they scale and parallelize better than RNNs, even though the self-attention cost is quadratic in sequence length. Source: `[1:13:31]` to `[1:16:49]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Linear interaction distance.** The number of sequential steps needed before two tokens can influence each other in an RNN. Source: `[4:38]` to `[7:22]`.
- **Self-attention.** A set-to-set operation that builds each token representation from the whole sequence. Source: `[10:06]` to `[11:51]`.
- **Query, key, value.** Three learned projections used to score relevance and build weighted outputs. Source: `[15:06]` to `[17:13]`.
- **Softmax weights.** Attention scores become probabilities over positions, then weighted sums of values. Source: `[16:44]` to `[17:30]`.
- **Position embeddings.** Extra vectors added to token embeddings so the model knows sequence order. Source: `[22:58]` to `[27:24]`, `[39:31]` to `[39:59]`.
- **Sinusoidal position representations.** A fixed, periodic encoding alternative for positions. Source: `[24:25]` to `[25:35]`.
- **Learned position embeddings.** A trainable position table that is easy to use but capped by maximum sequence length. Source: `[25:54]` to `[26:56]`.
- **Masked self-attention.** The causal version used in decoders so future tokens stay hidden. Source: `[40:08]` to `[41:08]`.
- **Multi-head attention.** Several attention heads with different parameters, letting the model attend in multiple ways at once. Source: `[43:37]` to `[52:38]`.
- **Residual connections.** Shortcut paths that preserve gradient flow and make each block easier to optimize. Source: `[58:51]` to `[1:00:20]`.
- **Layer normalization.** Per-token normalization across dimensions, used to stabilize training without batch dependence. Source: `[1:01:02]` to `[1:06:40]`.
- **Cross-attention.** Decoder queries over encoder keys and values in the full transformer encoder-decoder. Source: `[1:10:24]` to `[1:12:39]`.
- **Quadratic sequence cost.** Self-attention compares all pairs, so the cost grows as sequence length squared. Source: `[1:14:24]` to `[1:16:12]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Self-attention solves two different problems at once: long-range dependency modeling and GPU parallelization. The lecture frames those as separate complaints against RNNs, then knocks them out together. Source: `[7:24]` to `[11:44]`.
- The position fix is not cosmetic. Without it, `Zuko made his uncle` and `his uncle made Zuko` would collapse to the same representation. Source: `[22:21]` to `[23:00]`.
- Multiple heads are not just "more attention." They are a way to let the model specialize different views of the same sequence, and the lecture suggests there is some redundancy rather than perfect separation. Source: `[43:37]` to `[44:52]`.
- Residual connections are optimization scaffolding, not decorative wiring. The lecture treats them as gradient highways and as an identity bias at initialization. Source: `[58:51]` to `[1:00:20]`.
- Layer norm is per token, not across the batch, which is why it avoids the train/test coupling problems of batch norm. Source: `[1:04:29]` to `[1:06:40]`.
- The lecture is notably skeptical about "fix all the things" transformer variants. The original architecture still works unusually well, despite many proposed improvements. Source: `[1:16:17]` to `[1:16:49]`.

## Connections
- This is the explicit continuation of the attention idea from L07. The lecture says attention will reappear here as self-attention.
- The encoder is just the bidirectional version of the block: remove masking and keep the rest.
- The decoder keeps masking because generation cannot look ahead.
- The cross-attention block is the transformer version of seq2seq attention from L07.
- The lecture sets up pretraining in L09 by showing that once the architecture is stable, scale is the next lever.

## Referenced Papers
- **Vaswani et al. (2017), Attention Is All You Need.** The lecture names this as the original transformer paper and uses it as the reference design. Source: `[1:10:04]` to `[1:10:24]`.
- **Bahdanau, Cho, and Bengio (2014), Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate.** The attention origin story is still the conceptual starting point for the transformer discussion. Source: `[10:06]` to `[13:29]`, `[1:10:24]` to `[1:12:39]`.
