# L13 Speech Brain-Computer Interfaces

This lecture is about decoding noisy neural activity into speech, then asking what happens when the decoder starts touching inner speech.

## Summary
- The lecture starts from a human problem, not a gadget demo: restore communication and movement for people with severe impairment, using Howard, T12, and Neuralink as the motivating examples. Source: `[0:05]` to `[5:30]`.
- Scalp EEG is real but coarse. The real signal lives in motor cortex, where single-neuron spikes and tuning curves can be measured and decoded. Source: `[5:57]` to `[21:13]`.
- The speech pipeline is two-stage: neural features -> phonemes -> words, with CTC handling monotonic alignment and GRUs plus beam search handling real-time decoding. Source: `[49:27]` to `[1:03:32]`.
- The decoder uses two language-model budgets: a fast n-gram model for streaming and a transformer LM for reranking. Success is measured with word error rate. Source: `[1:02:01]` to `[1:04:05]`.
- The lecture ends on multimodal BCIs, better arrays, and the privacy problem of decoding inner speech. That is the real frontier. Source: `[1:04:46]` to `[1:12:37]`.

## Key Concepts
- **EEG.** Scalp-level brain recording. Good for broad signal, bad for fine speech decoding. Source: `[6:54]` to `[11:17]`.
- **Motor cortex.** The region targeted for direct recording because it controls movement and speech-related signals. Source: `[11:20]` to `[12:23]`.
- **Spike train.** The sequence of sharp firing events produced by a neuron. Source: `[13:11]` to `[14:08]`.
- **Tuning curve.** A neuron-specific firing-rate profile over movement direction, often approximated with a cosine. Source: `[16:51]` to `[17:44]`.
- **Spatial vs temporal resolution.** The lecture uses this axis pair to compare EEG, MRI, and single-neuron recordings. Source: `[22:44]` to `[24:30]`.
- **Intracortical arrays.** Implanted electrodes that give higher-resolution signals than EEG. Source: `[26:33]` to `[27:40]`, `[41:58]` to `[44:31]`.
- **CTC.** Connectionist Temporal Classification. It handles long input / short output alignment with blank tokens and repeated symbols. Source: `[53:48]` to `[55:31]`.
- **GRU.** A simpler recurrent decoder than an LSTM, chosen here because the data set is small and the system has to run in real time. Source: `[57:40]` to `[58:00]`.
- **Beam search.** Used to recover the most likely phoneme and word sequences from decoder probabilities. Source: `[58:51]` to `[1:00:45]`.
- **Language model rescoring.** An n-gram LM handles fast online pruning; a transformer LM reranks final sentence hypotheses. Source: `[1:00:52]` to `[1:03:32]`.
- **Word error rate.** The evaluation metric for the speech decoder. Source: `[1:04:03]` to `[1:04:09]`.
- **Inner speech.** The speculative frontier, and the place where the technical problem turns into a privacy question. Source: `[1:07:11]` to `[1:10:37]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The central bottleneck is sensor fidelity, not model fashion. Better decoding starts with better recording geometry. Source: `[10:14]` to `[11:17]`, `[22:11]` to `[24:30]`.
- CTC is the right fit because speech production is mostly monotonic and the input-output length mismatch is huge. The architecture follows the data shape. Source: `[53:07]` to `[55:31]`.
- The GRU is not a downgrade. It is the right latency/data tradeoff for a real-time medical system. Source: `[56:15]` to `[58:00]`.
- The language model is doing two jobs with two latency budgets: fast pruning during decoding and slower reranking after the candidate set is built. Source: `[1:00:52]` to `[1:03:32]`.
- Inner-speech decoding changes the category of the system. It is no longer just assistive communication; it becomes a privacy and consent problem. Source: `[1:07:11]` to `[1:11:50]`.
