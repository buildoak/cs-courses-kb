# L16 ConvNets, Tree Recursive Neural Networks and Constituency Parsing

This is the pre-transformer lecture where local feature detectors and tree structure still look like serious answers.

## Summary
- The lecture explains how CNNs for NLP slide n-gram filters over word vectors and then max-pool them into sentence-level features. Source: transcript `[4:05]` to `[14:19]`.
- Yoon Kim's sentence-classification CNN is the canonical success case: simple architecture, strong sentiment results, and a clean demonstration that CNNs can work on text. Source: transcript `[18:31]` to `[28:57]`.
- Fine-tuning embeddings is useful but risky, so Kim's two-channel trick keeps both static and trainable word vectors. Source: transcript `[21:42]` to `[24:45]`.
- Tree recursive neural networks handle compositional meaning and negation better than flat models, but transformers still win on flexible information flow. Source: transcript `[42:47]` to `[55:02]`, `[1:05:24]` to `[1:11:46]`.
- The lecture ends with the right question: can we keep semantic structure without losing transformer flexibility? Source: transcript `[1:11:03]` to `[1:11:46]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Convolution.** A learned filter slides over token embeddings to detect local patterns. Source: transcript `[6:26]` to `[9:05]`.
- **Max pooling.** Collapse a feature map to detect whether a pattern appears anywhere in the sentence. Source: transcript `[11:07]` to `[13:45]`.
- **Padding, stride, dilation.** Controls the receptive field and how much context each filter sees. Source: transcript `[10:05]` to `[18:16]`.
- **Sentence classification.** The task used to show CNNs work in NLP. Source: transcript `[19:13]` to `[20:47]`.
- **Tree recursive neural network.** Composition follows a constituency tree rather than a flat sequence. Source: transcript `[42:47]` to `[55:02]`.
- **Recursive neural tensor network.** A stronger tree model that can represent compositional interactions like negation. Source: transcript `[54:32]` to `[1:03:09]`.
- **Negation and compositionality.** The lecture's stress test for whether a model actually understands phrase structure. Source: transcript `[1:05:24]` to `[1:11:29]`.
- **Transformers.** The later architecture that wins on global information flow and generality. Source: transcript `[1:10:39]` to `[1:11:46]`.
- **Constituency parsing.** The structured backbone that makes tree composition possible. Source: schedule page and transcript `[42:47]` to `[1:11:46]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Max pooling is not just compression. It encodes a feature-detector worldview: "did the pattern fire anywhere?" Source: transcript `[11:23]` to `[13:45]`.
- The CNN is linguistically crude on purpose. It is a pragmatic local hypothesis space, not a claim about how language is built. Source: transcript `[5:35]` to `[6:20]`, `[18:31]` to `[19:06]`.
- Kim's two-channel setup is a practical compromise between stable pretrained geometry and task adaptation. Source: transcript `[21:42]` to `[24:45]`.
- Negation is the real compositionality test. The RNTN is interesting because it gets cases like "not dull" right in a way flat models often miss. Source: transcript `[1:05:24]` to `[1:08:34]`.
- Transformers beat tree models on breadth, but the lecture refuses to say structure is irrelevant. The unresolved question is whether hybrid models can keep both semantic discipline and flexibility. Source: transcript `[1:10:39]` to `[1:11:46]`.
