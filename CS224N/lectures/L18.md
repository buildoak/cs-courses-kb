# L18 NLP, Linguistics, and Philosophy

This is the course's philosophical landing pad. It asks what the models are doing, and what kind of world they are doing it in.

## Summary
- The lecture recaps the course arc: word vectors, neural NLP, LMs/RNNs/LSTMs, transformers, pretraining, post-training, benchmarking, reasoning, and responsible NLP. Source: transcript `[1:00]` to `[4:09]`, slide deck slides 4-8.
- Distributional semantics remains the engine under the hood, but the lecture keeps pointing out that multilingual and low-resource NLP are still badly underserved. Source: transcript `[2:12]` to `[2:33]`, `[8:58]` to `[11:00]`.
- Symbolic systems and linguistics still matter because they give us questions about compositionality, reference, and systematic generalization. Source: transcript `[26:03]` to `[39:24]`, slide deck slides 4, 10, 33-44.
- The lecture draws a line between denotational semantics and use theory, then argues that meaning is gradient rather than binary. Source: transcript `[48:27]` to `[1:00:24]`, slide deck slides 45-47.
- The ending shifts hard into present-day AI risk: concentration of power, disinformation, surveillance, and the need for broad education and open source. Source: transcript `[1:06:42]` to `[1:15:59]`, slide deck slides 56-58.

## Key Concepts
- **Distributional semantics.** Meaning comes from context and usage, not just dictionary-like denotation. Source: transcript `[2:12]` to `[2:28]`, slide deck slides 4-5.
- **Symbolic systems.** Systems of meaningful symbols that represent the world, including language, logic, and programming languages. Source: transcript `[26:03]` to `[28:07]`, slide deck slides 33-34.
- **Compositionality.** The meaning of a larger expression depends on the meanings of its parts and how they are combined. Source: transcript `[37:18]` to `[39:24]`, `[48:27]` to `[55:02]`, slide deck slides 37-40.
- **Systematic generalization.** The ability to recombine learned pieces in new ways. Source: transcript `[37:20]` to `[39:24]`, `[56:18]` to `[56:47]`.
- **Denotational semantics.** Traditional model-theoretic meaning, where words map to things in the world. Source: transcript `[48:27]` to `[49:24]`, slide deck slides 45-46.
- **Use theory of meaning.** Meaning comes from how a word is used and what it connects to. Source: transcript `[57:19]` to `[1:00:24]`, slide deck slides 45-47.
- **Semantic parsing.** A formal route from language to meaning representations. Source: transcript `[54:50]` to `[55:25]`, slide deck slides 39-40.
- **Low-resource and multilingual NLP.** The lecture calls out the gap between English-centric progress and the rest of the world. Source: transcript `[8:58]` to `[11:00]`.
- **AI risk.** The lecture focuses less on sci-fi singularity and more on current harms, concentration, and manipulation. Source: transcript `[1:06:42]` to `[1:15:59]`, slide deck slides 56-58.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture is pro-neural and pro-linguistics at the same time. Linguistics is not a rival stack; it is a source of useful distinctions and test cases. Source: transcript `[37:18]` to `[39:24]`, `[52:32]` to `[56:56]`.
- Meaning is treated as gradient. The lecture refuses the binary "understands / does not understand" framing. Source: transcript `[57:19]` to `[1:00:24]`.
- Human language may be symbolic because symbols are reliable under noise, not because hand-built symbolic AI is the whole answer. Source: transcript `[41:34]` to `[43:26]`, `[1:00:00]` to `[1:00:24]`.
- The lecture keeps returning to the same gap: fluent LLM behavior is not the same thing as systematic compositional understanding. Source: transcript `[56:09]` to `[56:56]`, `[1:11:12]` to `[1:11:25]`.
- The real AI danger here is present-day power, not a speculative future singularity. That is the lecture's sharpest political claim. Source: transcript `[1:06:42]` to `[1:15:59]`, slide deck slides 56-58.
