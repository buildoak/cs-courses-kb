# L11 Benchmarking and Evaluation

This lecture is about the uncomfortable truth that models are only as useful as the way you score them.

## Summary
- Evaluation is not one thing. Training, development, model selection, deployment, and publication all need different metrics and different tradeoffs. Source: `[1:03]` to `[5:59]`.
- Closed-ended NLP tasks can still use ordinary machine-learning metrics like accuracy, precision, recall, and F1. Source: `[8:05]` to `[11:29]`.
- Open-ended generation is harder: BLEU and ROUGE are fast and historically useful, but they miss semantics and can be gamed by length or paraphrase. Source: `[19:59]` to `[25:06]`.
- LLM-era evaluation shifts toward reference-free judging, with AlpacaEval, MT-Bench, HELM, and the Hugging Face Open LLM Leaderboard sitting in the middle of the current stack. Source: `[31:54]` to `[56:27]`.
- The real hazards are contamination, prompt sensitivity, self-bias, and a benchmark monoculture that overweights English and accuracy. Source: `[50:31]` to `[1:12:07]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Training vs development vs deployment.** Each phase needs a different kind of evaluation metric. Source: `[1:03]` to `[5:59]`.
- **Closed-ended tasks.** Classification-style problems with a small answer space, such as sentiment, entailment, POS tagging, and NER. Source: `[8:05]` to `[11:29]`.
- **Open-ended tasks.** Generation-style problems such as summarization and chat, where there is no single correct answer. Source: `[19:11]` to `[20:36]`.
- **BLEU.** A precision-oriented n-gram overlap metric used heavily in translation. Source: `[21:17]` to `[23:30]`.
- **ROUGE.** A recall-oriented overlap metric used heavily in summarization. Source: `[21:47]` to `[23:30]`.
- **BERTScore.** A stronger semantic metric than raw overlap, and still used. Source: `[31:45]` to `[31:52]`.
- **Reference-free evaluation.** Ask a model, usually GPT-4, to judge model outputs directly. Source: `[31:54]` to `[32:46]`.
- **MMLU.** The canonical broad academic benchmark for LLM knowledge and reasoning. Source: `[7:03]` to `[7:28]`, `[56:31]` to `[58:27]`.
- **HELM / Open LLM Leaderboard.** Benchmark suites that aggregate many tasks rather than one score. Source: `[56:10]` to `[56:27]`.
- **Contamination.** Test-set leakage or benchmark saturation that makes results look much better than they should. Source: `[1:07:12]` to `[1:10:58]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The metric has to match the phase. A production metric must be trustworthy and absolute; a training metric only needs to be cheap and differentiable. Source: `[1:03]` to `[5:59]`.
- BLEU and ROUGE fail in opposite ways: they can miss paraphrases and still reward the wrong answer if the overlap is superficial. Source: `[23:03]` to `[25:06]`.
- Human evaluation is still the gold standard, but it is not comparable across papers unless the protocol is identical. Source: `[33:00]` to `[35:40]`.
- AlpacaEval needed reweighting because prompt style alone could swing win rates dramatically. Source: `[50:43]` to `[53:56]`.
- Self-bias exists, but the lecture's point is more nuanced: judges do favor themselves, yet the broad ranking remains fairly stable. Source: `[54:10]` to `[54:56]`.
- Contamination and benchmark overfitting are hard to disentangle, which is why private and dynamic test sets matter. Source: `[1:07:12]` to `[1:10:58]`.
- The field still overfits English and accuracy. That is a benchmark-choice problem, not a model-only problem. Source: `[1:11:13]` to `[1:12:07]`.
