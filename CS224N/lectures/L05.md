# L05: Recurrent Neural Networks

## Summary
- Language models predict the next word, and perplexity is the course's standard evaluation metric for that task.
- N-gram language models are simple and fast, but sparsity, backoff, and context limits make them brittle.
- Fixed-window neural language models improve generalization, but they still throw away most of the context.
- RNNs replace the fixed window with a recurrent hidden state, but sequential computation and gradient pathologies make them hard to train.
- LSTMs become the practical fix: they keep long-range state better, work with teacher forcing, and dominate the lecture's later examples.

## Key Concepts
- **Perplexity:** exponentiated cross-entropy, used as the standard LM score.
- **N-grams:** unigram, bigram, trigram, and higher-order count-based language models.
- **Smoothing and backoff:** count hacks that keep probabilities from collapsing to zero.
- **Fixed-window neural LM:** embeddings plus an MLP over a short context window.
- **Recurrent neural network:** one set of weights reused across time steps.
- **Teacher forcing:** train on the observed next token, not the model's own sampled output.
- **Truncated BPTT:** backpropagate through only part of a long sequence for speed.
- **LSTM:** hidden state plus cell state, with gates that preserve useful memory.
- **Bidirectional LSTM:** useful for encoders, not for free-form generation.
- **Gradient clipping:** a practical stabilizer for exploding gradients.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The old n-gram story is really a story about scarcity. Better scaling helps, but better representation matters too.
- RNNs are not magic unlimited-memory models. In practice, the hidden state still forgets, and truncated training makes the context window explicit again.
- The lecture treats dropout as a kind of ensemble effect, not just random noise injection.
- Sequential compute is a real systems problem. RNNs are conceptually elegant and operationally awkward because they force a loop.
- LSTM history matters. The model was proposed early, ignored for years, then became dominant only when the hardware and training culture caught up.

## Connections
- This lecture feeds directly into the assignment work on sequence models and optimization.
- The decoder in seq2seq is just an RNN language model conditioned on extra input.
- Teacher forcing here becomes the training pattern for machine translation in the next lecture.
- Bidirectional encoders and stacked LSTMs become important once the course shifts from pure language modeling to sequence transduction.

## Referenced Papers
- **Bengio et al. (2003)** - neural language modeling over fixed context windows.
- **Bengio et al. (2007), Greedy Layer-Wise Training of Deep Networks** - the lecture's example of the deep-learning resurgence.
- **Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997)** - the original LSTM paper.
- **Gers, Schmidhuber, and Cummins (2000), Learning to Forget: Continual Prediction with LSTM** - the follow-up that adds the crucial forget-gate behavior used in modern LSTMs.
- **Srivastava et al. (2014), Dropout: A Simple Way to Prevent Neural Networks from Overfitting** - the regularizer the lecture keeps returning to.
