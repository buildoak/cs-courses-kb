# L03 Neural Classifiers and Backpropagation

Lecture 3 turns the math crank: activation functions, matrix calculus, backpropagation, and the software machinery that hides the pain.

## Summary
- The lecture frames neural classifiers as stacked logistic-regression-like units that learn intermediate representations, not just output decisions. Source: `[3:26]` to `[6:59]`, `[17:04]` to `[18:31]`.
- Nonlinearities matter because plain matrix multiplies collapse into one linear map; sigmoid, tanh, ReLU, leaky ReLU, and newer activations create actual representational power. Source: `[8:01]` to `[16:57]`.
- Matrix calculus enters through gradients, Jacobians, the chain rule, and the shape convention that makes gradients fit the parameter tensors. Source: `[19:26]` to `[44:33]`.
- Backpropagation is explained as a forward pass plus a reverse pass that reuses shared computations instead of recomputing each gradient from scratch. Source: `[45:00]` to `[1:02:38]`.
- Automatic differentiation, gradient checking, and modern deep-learning frameworks are the practical payoff, with Theano as the cautionary historical example. Source: `[1:02:43]` to `[1:11:16]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Layered computation.** `Wx + b` followed by a nonlinearity is the basic feedforward unit. Source: `[6:10]` to `[7:10]`.
- **Activation functions.** Threshold units have no slope; sigmoid, tanh, and ReLU-style activations restore gradient flow. Source: `[8:09]` to `[14:40]`.
- **Function approximation.** Neural nets need nonlinearities because multiple linear maps alone do not increase representational power. Source: `[15:00]` to `[16:44]`.
- **Gradients and Jacobians.** A scalar derivative becomes a gradient vector; a vector-to-vector map becomes a Jacobian matrix. Source: `[23:08]` to `[27:19]`.
- **Shape convention.** In theory you can keep Jacobian shapes; in practice you store gradients in the same shape as the parameters so subtraction is easy. Source: `[38:25]` to `[44:33]`.
- **Upstream vs local gradient.** Backprop passes an error signal backward and multiplies it by each node's local derivative. Source: `[48:17]` to `[49:15]`.
- **Computation graphs.** Forward pass computes values, backward pass walks the DAG in reverse, and shared work is done once. Source: `[45:40]` to `[1:02:38]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Stacked linear layers are useless for representational power, but they can still matter for optimization. That distinction is easy to miss and the lecture makes it explicit. Source: `[16:02]` to `[16:34]`.
- The shape convention is an engineering compromise, not pure math. It exists because you want gradients that can be subtracted from parameters without extra reshaping pain. Source: `[39:18]` to `[44:33]`.
- Backprop is not "magic". It is chain rule plus memoization of intermediate results. Source: `[45:00]` to `[45:33]`.
- A max node is a gradient router: one branch gets the gradient, the others get nothing. Source: `[52:07]` to `[57:40]`.
- Numerical gradient checking is too slow for training but perfect for debugging custom layers. Source: `[1:08:18]` to `[1:11:16]`.
