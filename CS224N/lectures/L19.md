# L19 Model Interpretability & Editing

This lecture is a refusal of pretty explanations. The real question is whether we can build a usable bridge between human vocabulary and machine state.

## Summary
- The talk opens with Move 37 and treats interpretability as translation: build overlap between human and machine representations, not just post-hoc explanations. Source: `[0:05]` to `[7:39]`.
- Saliency maps, Integrated Gradients, and SHAP are useful ideas, but the lecture shows how easily they fail sanity checks and hypothesis tests. Visual appeal is not evidence. Source: `[9:51]` to `[22:49]`.
- Model editing is a separate problem from localization. ROME/MEMIT-style tracing can identify where facts seem to live, but that does not predict where edits will actually work. Source: `[23:47]` to `[34:04]`.
- For emergent RL behavior, the lecture splits the problem into observation and intervention: cluster latent behavior, then intervene with concept bottlenecks and simple graphs. Source: `[36:27]` to `[54:56]`.
- The closing argument is that human concepts get you halfway. The next step is discovering machine-native concepts and learning how to translate them back. Source: `[55:13]` to `[1:11:34]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Representational overlap.** Communication with models requires shared vocabulary, not just shared performance. Source: `[6:29]` to `[7:34]`.
- **Saliency maps.** Pixel or feature attributions based on local sensitivity. Source: `[9:51]` to `[10:56]`.
- **Integrated Gradients.** Path-integral attribution over a baseline-to-input trajectory. Source: `[14:17]` to `[15:10]`.
- **SHAP.** Shapley-value attribution framed as feature contribution accounting. Source: `[14:17]` to `[15:10]`.
- **Sanity checks.** Randomization tests that reveal some saliency methods ignore model or data. Source: `[12:08]` to `[13:28]`.
- **Hypothesis testing for explanations.** An attribution only matters if it can validate or invalidate a claim about function shape better than chance. Source: `[15:21]` to `[20:45]`.
- **Causal tracing.** Intervention-based tracing of factual recall through layers and tokens. Source: `[25:28]` to `[27:10]`.
- **Model editing.** Updating a model's factual associations, not just locating them. Source: `[23:47]` to `[24:16]`.
- **Emergent behavior discovery.** Use latent embeddings and clustering to expose behaviors in multi-agent systems. Source: `[37:49]` to `[43:23]`.
- **Concept bottleneck models.** Route prediction through human concepts so you can zero out or intervene on them. Source: `[44:10]` to `[45:29]`.
- **Concept activation vectors.** Translate between machine state and human concepts in activation space. Source: `[1:06:20]` to `[1:07:08]`.
- **Machine-native concepts.** Some useful concepts are not already in human vocabulary; Move 37 is the canonical example. Source: `[4:48]` to `[5:29]`, `[1:07:32]` to `[1:07:56]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- A saliency map can look stable and still be functionally useless. The lecture's sanity-check result is a direct warning against trusting visual intuition. Source: `[12:08]` to `[13:28]`.
- "Where a fact is found" and "where an edit works" are different questions. The ROME/MEMIT story shows that localization can be nearly orthogonal to editing success. Source: `[27:24]` to `[34:04]`.
- Simple interventions beat clever overlays when the goal is causal understanding. In the cooking game, orientation mattered more than position because it predicted the next move. Source: `[47:39]` to `[49:05]`.
- Human concept labels are useful scaffolding, not the final ontology. The AlphaZero section argues for learning concepts we do not yet have words for. Source: `[55:40]` to `[1:01:45]`, `[1:06:20]` to `[1:07:56]`.
- Interpretability inherited a neuron-centric bias from neuroscience tooling. That bias may be a measurement artifact, not a truth about model structure. Source: `[1:09:23]` to `[1:10:47]`.

## Connections
- To earlier CS224N analysis material, this lecture turns interpretability from "look at the attention map" into "state a falsifiable claim, then test it." Source: `[9:09]` to `[9:45]`, `[15:21]` to `[20:45]`.
- To the transformer and pretraining lectures, it says hidden representations are only useful if we can translate them into concepts that survive intervention. Source: `[55:13]` to `[1:01:45]`.
- To RL and agents, interpretability becomes observational science plus controlled experiments, not static chart reading. Source: `[36:01]` to `[54:56]`.
- To AI safety, the lecture frames interpretability as a way to surface surprise before deployment, not as a guarantee of alignment. Source: `[6:08]` to `[6:26]`, `[1:04:56]` to `[1:05:41]`.
- To final projects, the standard is clear: if your explanation method cannot survive a negative control, it is a demo, not evidence. Source: `[12:08]` to `[20:45]`.

## Referenced Papers
- **Sanity Checks for Saliency Maps.** The lecture's first warning shot: visual saliency can stay the same even when the model is randomized.
- **Axiomatic Attribution for Deep Networks.** The Integrated Gradients paper behind the IG baseline-to-input framing.
- **A Unified Approach to Interpreting Model Predictions.** The SHAP paper that rephrases attribution as Shapley-value accounting.
- **Locating and Editing Factual Associations in GPT.** The ROME paper behind causal tracing and mid-layer factual editing.
- **Mass-Editing Memory in a Transformer.** The MEMIT extension that scales editing to many facts.
- **Concept Bottleneck Models.** The concept-intervention architecture used to make interventions explicit.
- **Interpretability Beyond Feature Attribution: Quantitative Testing with Concept Activation Vectors (TCAV).** The concept-vector framing for translating human concepts into activation space.
- **Bridging the Human-AI Knowledge Gap: Concept Discovery and Transfer in AlphaZero.** The AlphaZero paper on discovering and transferring machine-native chess concepts.
