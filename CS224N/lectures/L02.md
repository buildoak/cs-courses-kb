# L02 Word2Vec Variants, GloVe, and Senses

Lecture 2 spends most of its time on optimization and evaluation, then uses word vectors to show why sense ambiguity is the real tax on the whole setup.

## Summary
- The lecture opens with optimization basics: gradient descent, then stochastic gradient descent and mini-batches because full-batch updates are too expensive. Source: `[3:05]` to `[8:04]`.
- Word2vec comes back as two families of models, CBOW and skip-gram, with negative sampling as the practical shortcut around the full softmax. Source: `[26:55]` to `[33:22]`.
- GloVe is introduced as a co-occurrence model where ratios of probabilities become linear meaning components in vector space. Source: `[41:01]` to `[45:14]`.
- Evaluation splits into intrinsic checks like analogies and similarity, and extrinsic checks like named entity recognition. Source: `[47:43]` to `[52:11]`.
- The lecture then argues that one vector per word is usually an average over senses, using bank, jaguar, pike, and field as the stress tests. Source: `[52:23]` to `[1:03:00]`.

## Key Concepts
- **SGD / mini-batches.** Noise in the gradient is not just tolerated; it often helps neural optimization move faster and better. Source: `[6:53]` to `[8:04]`.
- **CBOW vs skip-gram.** CBOW predicts the center word from context; skip-gram predicts context from the center. Source: `[26:55]` to `[27:24]`.
- **Negative sampling.** Replace the full softmax denominator with a few positive/negative logistic regression-style comparisons. Source: `[27:30]` to `[33:22]`.
- **Unigram^3/4 sampling.** The standard word2vec trick that nudges negative samples toward less frequent words without making sampling fully uniform. Source: `[32:21]` to `[33:22]`.
- **GloVe.** Start from co-occurrence counts, take logs, and model ratios so vector differences encode meaning differences. Source: `[41:01]` to `[45:14]`.
- **Intrinsic vs extrinsic evaluation.** A nice analogy score is not the same thing as a useful downstream model. Source: `[47:43]` to `[48:17]`.
- **Polysemy.** A single surface form can hide several related or unrelated senses, and forcing hard sense boundaries is often artificial. Source: `[52:23]` to `[1:00:35]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- SGD noise is framed as a feature, not just an approximation error. The lecture explicitly says the noise can help neural nets optimize better. Source: `[7:39]` to `[8:04]`.
- The `3/4` exponent is a compromise knob. It shifts negative sampling toward common-but-not-too-common words instead of blindly matching corpus frequency. Source: `[32:21]` to `[33:22]`.
- GloVe is not just "factorize a matrix". The point is to make co-occurrence ratios line up with linear directions in the vector space. Source: `[42:01]` to `[44:42]`.
- The lecture treats sense inventory as a leaky abstraction. Dictionaries want crisp senses, but actual usage gives you a continuum. Source: `[58:24]` to `[1:00:35]`.
- The sparse-coding result is a small surprise: even if the surface vector is an average, high-dimensional structure still lets you recover useful sense vectors. Source: `[1:01:17]` to `[1:02:44]`.
