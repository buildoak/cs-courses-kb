# L04: Dependency Parsing

## Summary
- Dependency grammar is the main structural lens here. Phrase structure is introduced, but the lecture spends most of its time on heads, dependents, and trees.
- Hand-written grammar rules break on ambiguity and coverage. Treebanks and annotated dependency data are the practical replacement.
- Transition-based parsing turns parsing into local `SHIFT`, `LEFT-ARC`, and `RIGHT-ARC` decisions over a stack and buffer.
- Neural dependency parsing keeps the transition system but replaces sparse symbolic features with dense embeddings and a small classifier.
- Graph-based parsing scores all possible head-dependent pairs, then enforces tree structure with a global optimization step.

## Key Concepts
- **Dependency grammar:** a sentence is a tree of asymmetric head-dependent relations, usually with a single root.
- **Phrase structure vs dependency structure:** phrase structure builds nested constituents; dependency structure tracks what modifies what.
- **Treebanks:** annotated dependency data are both training data and evaluation infrastructure.
- **Transition-based parsing:** stack/buffer parsing reduces the problem to a sequence of local actions.
- **Projective vs nonprojective trees:** the simple parser handles projective structures; nonprojective cases need more machinery.
- **Neural feature vectors:** words, POS tags, and dependency labels become learned embeddings that feed an MLP.
- **Graph-based parsing:** all candidate arcs get scored, then the best tree is recovered with a tree constraint.
- **UAS / LAS:** unlabeled and labeled attachment are the core parser metrics.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture is historical on purpose. Dependency grammar is older than phrase-structure grammar, and the Chomsky hierarchy was originally an argument about human language, not just formal language theory.
- The big neural win was not only better accuracy. It also removed the expensive symbolic feature computation that made older parsers slow.
- Dependency parsing is useful outside syntax. The lecture uses biomedical relation extraction as the concrete example.
- Treebanks are infrastructure, not just training data. Once you have them, you get reusable resources, evaluation, and cross-lingual comparison.
- Graph-based parsing exists because local decisions are not always enough. If you want the best tree under global constraints, you score the whole sentence.

## Referenced Papers
- **Nivre (2003), An Efficient Algorithm for Projective Dependency Parsing** - the projective transition-based line the lecture builds on.
- **Nivre (2007), Incremental Non-Projective Dependency Parsing** - the nonprojective counterpart worth knowing once projective parsing stops being enough.
- **Chen & Manning (2014), A Fast and Accurate Dependency Parser using Neural Networks** - the neural transition-based parser.
- **Dozat & Manning (2017), Deep Biaffine Attention for Neural Dependency Parsing** - the graph-based neural parser that closes the lecture.
