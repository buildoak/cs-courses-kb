# L06: Sequence to Sequence Models

## Summary
- This lecture moves from RNN language modeling to neural machine translation.
- Seq2seq is the encoder-decoder pattern: one network reads the source sentence, another generates the target sentence.
- Training is end-to-end on parallel sentence pairs, with losses backpropagated through both encoder and decoder.
- The whole point is to handle reordering and length mismatch across languages without bolting rules onto the source side.
- Neural machine translation was good enough to replace large statistical phrase-based systems.

## Key Concepts
- **Sequence-to-sequence:** a general architecture for mapping one sequence to another.
- **Encoder-decoder:** the encoder compresses the source, the decoder generates the target.
- **Conditional language model:** the decoder is a language model conditioned on the source representation.
- **Final hidden state conditioning:** the source encoder's output initializes or conditions the decoder.
- **Parallel corpus:** paired source-target sentences for training.
- **End-to-end backpropagation:** gradients flow through the entire translation system.
- **Bidirectional encoder:** a useful but optional improvement over a plain forward encoder.
- **Stacked LSTMs:** common in practical translation systems.
- **Length and word-order mismatch:** the main reason seq2seq beats a naive source-side deep network.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Seq2seq is not just "a deeper net." The important move is changing the interface from fixed-size classification to variable-length transduction.
- The decoder is not a new kind of model. It is the same next-token generator from the previous lecture, only conditioned on source context.
- The lecture makes the bottleneck visible: compressing a source sentence into one state is already an information-loss decision.
- The appeal of end-to-end training is alignment. One loss at the output forces the entire system to optimize for the final task instead of local heuristics.
- Neural MT won because it was simpler to train, easier to generalize, and better at the actual task.
