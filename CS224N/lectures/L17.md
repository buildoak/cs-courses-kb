# L17 Human-Centered NLP

This lecture is the human-sized correction to model worship.

## Summary
- Human-centered NLP means designing NLP systems around human needs, preferences, and harms, not just benchmark scores. Source: slide deck slides 2, 7-10.
- The lecture says evaluation must account for user-specific attributes, interaction goals, cognitive load, and model shortcuts. Source: slide deck slides 15-20.
- Social impact is not a side quest. The lecture ties human-centered NLP to bias, toxicity, accessibility, and domain-specific deployment risks. Source: slide deck slides 25-33.
- Learning from human feedback is the main practical path: labels, natural language feedback, parameter updates, RLHF, AI feedback, and principle-based critique all sit on the same spectrum. Source: slide deck slides 34-68.
- Reward hacking and unreliable preferences are not edge cases. They are the lecture's closing warning. Source: slide deck slides 64-67.

## Key Concepts
- **Human-centered NLP.** NLP systems built to match human needs and preferences, with ethics and social impact in view. Source: slide deck slides 7-10.
- **Human-centered evaluation.** Evaluation that measures the right users, tasks, harms, and cognitive load instead of a single global score. Source: slide deck slides 17-20.
- **Self-selection bias.** Data collected from volunteers or platform users may not reflect the people the system will actually serve. Source: slide deck slides 10-12.
- **Reporting bias.** What people say or write is not proportional to what exists in the world. Source: slide deck slide 11.
- **Human-in-the-loop training.** End users, crowd workers, and developers can all provide feedback at different stages. Source: slide deck slides 12-14.
- **RLHF.** Reinforcement learning from human feedback, used as one route to incorporate preferences directly into models. Source: slide deck slides 17, 19, 34-40.
- **RLAIF.** Reinforcement learning from AI feedback, presented as a scaling path beyond purely human labels. Source: slide deck slides 58-61.
- **Constitutional AI.** Learning harmlessness from feedback expressed as principles rather than direct human rankings. Source: slide deck slides 58-60.
- **ConstitutionMaker.** Converting feedback into principles for critique and model updates. Source: slide deck slides 62-63.
- **Reward hacking.** Optimizers exploit incomplete reward representations. Source: slide deck slides 64-67.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture is not saying "add some human labels." It treats the whole lifecycle as human-centered: collection, evaluation, deployment, feedback, and post-deployment correction. Source: slide deck slides 7, 10, 12-14, 17-20, 34-68.
- Human-friendly feedback and model-friendly feedback are not the same thing. That mismatch is structural, not incidental. Source: slide deck slides 40-41.
- The model can be good while the feedback pipeline is still wrong. The lecture repeatedly separates architecture quality from data/annotation quality. Source: slide deck slides 10-14, 64-67.
- AI feedback is not a replacement for human values. It is a scaling trick that still inherits the limits of the underlying human specification. Source: slide deck slides 58-63.
- The lecture treats accessibility and inclusion as evaluation problems, not just product polish. Source: slide deck slides 7-10, 25-33.
