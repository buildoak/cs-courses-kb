# L14 Reasoning and Agents

This lecture treats reasoning as a prompting problem first, then as a trajectory problem once the model starts acting.

## Summary
- The lecture begins by splitting reasoning into deductive, inductive, and abductive forms, then narrows to the informal multi-step reasoning that LMs can sometimes expose. Source: `[0:05]` to `[4:32]`.
- Chain-of-thought, self-consistency, and least-to-most prompting are the first layer. They improve behavior without changing weights. Source: `[4:14]` to `[9:54]`.
- Orca and REST are the second layer. They use explanation traces and iterative self-training to distill reasoning behavior into smaller models. Source: `[10:34]` to `[20:10]`.
- The lecture then asks whether rationales are faithful or just post-hoc stories, and uses counterfactual and corruption tests to check. The answer is mixed. Source: `[20:34]` to `[29:17]`.
- The second half reframes agents as trajectory modeling in text, then pushes that framing through MiniWoB, WebArena, WebLINX, and multimodal models such as LLaVA and Pix2Struct. The lecture ends by saying the prompting gap is still wide. Source: `[39:18]` to `[1:03:32]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Deductive reasoning.** Rule + premise -> conclusion. Source: `[1:28]` to `[2:00]`.
- **Inductive reasoning.** Observation -> likely conclusion. Source: `[2:00]` to `[2:32]`.
- **Abductive reasoning.** Observation -> possible explanation. Source: `[2:32]` to `[2:57]`.
- **Chain-of-thought prompting.** Ask the model to show intermediate reasoning before the answer. Source: `[4:14]` to `[4:53]`.
- **Self-consistency.** Sample multiple rationales and vote on the most common answer. Source: `[5:03]` to `[6:12]`.
- **Least-to-most prompting.** Break a problem into subproblems before solving them. Source: `[7:35]` to `[9:54]`.
- **Orca.** Distill a smaller model from GPT-4 explanation traces. Source: `[10:34]` to `[16:13]`.
- **REST.** Reinforced self-training on model-generated rationales. Source: `[16:45]` to `[20:10]`.
- **Faithfulness.** Whether the rationale actually caused the answer, or merely explains it after the fact. Source: `[20:49]` to `[24:44]`.
- **Counterfactual tasks.** Corrupt or alter the reasoning chain to test whether the answer changes. Source: `[21:34]` to `[29:17]`.
- **Trajectory modeling.** Treat action sequences as the object to predict, not just text completions. Source: `[39:18]` to `[42:47]`.
- **MiniWoB / WebArena / WebLINX.** Benchmark environments for browser and web agents, from simple sandboxes to real websites. Source: `[43:06]` to `[46:27]`.
- **Synthetic demonstrations.** Generate and relabel trajectories to bootstrap better agent training data. Source: `[48:02]` to `[53:20]`.
- **LLaVA and Pix2Struct.** Multimodal models that use images and screenshots to close the prompting gap. Source: `[54:23]` to `[57:30]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Self-consistency is more than a prompt trick. It is latent ensembling over multiple rationales. Source: `[5:03]` to `[6:12]`.
- A fluent rationale is not proof of reasoning. The early-exit and corruption tests are there because post-hoc explanations can look persuasive while being causally irrelevant. Source: `[20:49]` to `[24:44]`.
- Turning control into next-action prediction is elegant, but it collapses once the environment gets long-horizon, multi-tab, or stateful. Source: `[39:18]` to `[46:27]`, `[58:47]` to `[1:00:23]`.
- Synthetic trajectories are not free. They depend on a second model to name the goal and a filter to decide whether the trajectory was actually useful. Source: `[48:02]` to `[53:20]`.
- Better state representations help, but they do not solve planning. LLaVA and Pix2Struct improve input handling; they do not magically fix long-horizon control. Source: `[54:23]` to `[1:00:23]`.
