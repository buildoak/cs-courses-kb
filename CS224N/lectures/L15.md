# L15 Life after DPO

This lecture asks what alignment looks like after DPO becomes the default starting point.

## Summary
- RLHF is a pipeline: instruction tuning, preference data, reward models, and policy updates. DPO is the simplest modern approximation of that pipeline. Source: `[0:05]` to `[15:55]`.
- The lecture traces the open-alignment stack through ShareGPT, Open Assistant, Vicuna, Llama 2 backlash, and the move from synthetic to human preference data. Source: `[15:32]` to `[20:30]`.
- The central technical question is not just "PPO or DPO?" but "offline or online?" and "how fresh is the data and reward loop?" Source: `[47:11]` to `[53:27]`.
- Self-rewarding models and D2PO are attempts to refresh preference data and reward models without reintroducing the full PPO stack. Source: `[49:55]` to `[53:27]`.
- The lecture ends with a practical list of open problems: data scarcity, reward hacking, more preference formats, and models small enough for academics to run. Source: `[54:27]` to `[1:08:25]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Instruction fine-tuning.** Train a model to follow instructions, usually on question-answer style data. Source: `[7:22]` to `[9:22]`.
- **Supervised fine-tuning.** Domain-specific adaptation that shares machinery with instruction tuning but has a narrower target. Source: `[7:22]` to `[8:24]`.
- **RLHF.** Reinforcement learning from human feedback, usually implemented through reward models and preference data. Source: `[8:04]` to `[10:45]`.
- **Bradley-Terry preference model.** The pairwise preference distribution that gets compressed into a scalar reward. Source: `[10:49]` to `[11:45]`.
- **DPO.** Direct Preference Optimization. A much simpler preference objective than PPO-style RLHF. Source: `[12:06]` to `[14:59]`.
- **ShareGPT / Open Assistant.** Early human-data sources that unlocked open alignment work. Source: `[15:32]` to `[18:37]`.
- **Reward model.** A learned scorer that estimates which answer humans would prefer. Source: `[25:12]` to `[30:22]`.
- **PPO.** The heavier RL-based alignment recipe that can beat DPO, but is slower and harder to debug. Source: `[39:47]` to `[44:55]`.
- **Online preference optimization.** Refresh the policy, labels, or reward model over time instead of training from static comparisons once. Source: `[47:11]` to `[53:27]`.
- **Self-rewarding language models.** Use the model itself, or a judge model, to relabel preference data. Source: `[49:55]` to `[50:20]`.
- **D2PO.** Discriminator-Guided DPO with Response Evaluation Models. A more online variant of DPO. Source: `[50:53]` to `[53:27]`.
- **Reward hacking.** A powerful optimizer exploits whatever the reward representation misses. Source: `[1:07:16]` to `[1:08:25]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- DPO won because it lowered the operational barrier, not because it solved every alignment problem. Source: `[12:54]` to `[13:54]`.
- The real fight is about data freshness. A lot of the gains come from relabeling or retraining reward models, not from changing one loss term. Source: `[47:11]` to `[53:27]`.
- Reward models are not neutral truth machines. They are compressed human preferences with all the failure modes that implies. Source: `[25:12]` to `[30:22]`.
- Open alignment progress is bottlenecked by the cost of fresh human data more than by a shortage of clever loss functions. Source: `[15:32]` to `[20:30]`, `[1:04:23]` to `[1:05:37]`.
- Industry stacks are pragmatic mixtures of SFT, rejection sampling, PPO, and DPO, chosen stage by stage. Source: `[54:27]` to `[1:00:00]`.
