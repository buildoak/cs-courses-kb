# L07: Attention, Final Projects and LLM Intro

Lecture 7 starts with BLEU and ends with project realism. The technical spine is the move from fixed MT evaluation to attention-backed translation.

## Summary
- BLEU turns translation quality into reference-overlap on 1-4 grams plus a brevity penalty. It is cheap, imperfect, and still the workhorse because human evaluation is too slow for training loops. Source: `[1:39]` to `[8:12]`.
- Statistical phrase-based and syntax-based MT had started to stall by the mid-2010s, then neural MT jumped from "not quite best" in 2015 to clearly better by 2016. Source: `[8:40]` to `[11:54]`.
- Attention shows up as the fix for the seq2seq bottleneck: instead of forcing one encoder vector to carry the whole source sentence, the decoder queries the source states directly at each step. Source: `[12:00]` to `[24:07]`.
- The lecture then compares dot-product, bilinear/multiplicative, and additive attention, treating attention as a soft lookup over a memory bank rather than a hard dictionary hit. Source: `[25:25]` to `[35:07]`.
- The closing segment shifts to project proposals: state the data, evaluation, and baselines; add ethical considerations; do not ship a hand-wave. Source: `[53:26]` to `[1:17:37]`.

## Key Concepts
- **BLEU.** Automatic machine-translation evaluation based on overlapping n-grams with reference translations, plus a brevity penalty. Source: `[2:05]` to `[8:12]`.
- **Reference translations.** Human-written translations used as the comparison set for scoring MT outputs. Source: `[3:27]` to `[5:24]`.
- **N-gram overlap.** BLEU scores translations by how many 1- to 4-gram matches it can find against the references. Source: `[3:36]` to `[4:05]`.
- **Brevity penalty.** A defense against the degenerate strategy of producing too-short translations. Source: `[7:28]` to `[7:41]`.
- **Attention.** A decoder-side mechanism that looks back into encoder states instead of compressing everything into one vector. Source: `[12:17]` to `[15:10]`.
- **Dot-product attention.** The simplest attention score: compare decoder state and encoder state directly, then softmax the scores. Source: `[19:29]` to `[21:24]`.
- **Bilinear / multiplicative attention.** Attention with a learned matrix between query and key, giving more flexibility than a plain dot product. Source: `[25:59]` to `[31:25]`.
- **Additive attention.** Attention scores from a small neural net over the query and key, rather than a single bilinear form. Source: `[32:00]` to `[33:07]`.
- **Encoder bottleneck.** The bad idea of stuffing all source information into one hidden vector. Source: `[13:30]` to `[14:02]`.
- **Baselines.** The lecture treats a baseline as a required part of serious project work, not optional garnish. Source: `[58:09]` to `[59:18]`.
- **Ethical considerations.** The project proposal now asks for a paragraph on deployment risks and mitigations. Source: `[55:45]` to `[56:22]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- BLEU is useful because it is fast enough to sit inside iteration loops. The lecture keeps the human standard in view, but it optimizes for engineering throughput. Source: `[3:06]` to `[3:23]`, `[7:50]` to `[8:03]`.
- The real failure mode of older MT was not only weak models. It was the mismatch between variable-length translation and a single fixed source representation. Source: `[13:30]` to `[15:01]`, `[23:04]` to `[23:40]`.
- Attention is doing two jobs at once: it improves translation quality and exposes alignments that humans can inspect. Source: `[23:40]` to `[24:59]`.
- The lecture treats project quality as a measurement problem. If you cannot name the data, the metric, and the baseline, the project is not ready. Source: `[54:41]` to `[59:18]`.
- Attention is presented as a general technique, not a one-off MT trick. The lecture explicitly says it will reappear in transformers. Source: `[33:45]` to `[35:07]`.

## Referenced Papers
- **Papineni et al. (2002), BLEU.** The lecture names BLEU as the original automatic MT metric and explains its n-gram overlap design. Source: `[2:15]` to `[2:29]`.
- **Bahdanau, Cho, and Bengio (2014), Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate.** The Montreal attention NMT line is presented as the compute-efficient alternative that beat the large LSTM system. Source: `[22:20]` to `[23:04]`.
- **Wu et al. (2016), Google's Neural Machine Translation System: Bridging the Gap between Human and Machine Translation.** The lecture uses Google NMT as the proof that attention-era translation had moved the field. Source: `[21:49]` to `[23:04]`.
