# L09: Pretraining

Lecture 9 moves from architecture to scale. The core idea is simple: pretrain on reconstruction, then fine-tune or prompt on downstream tasks.

## Summary
- Finite vocabularies break on morphology, typos, and rare words, so the lecture starts with subword tokenization as a way to cover arbitrary text. Source: `[2:19]` to `[14:47]`.
- Pretraining expands from word vectors to whole-network training, giving contextual representations and much better initialization for downstream tasks. Source: `[15:03]` to `[19:33]`.
- Reconstruction objectives do the heavy lifting: mask tokens or spans, then force the model to rebuild the missing text. Source: `[19:35]` to `[26:42]`, `[59:18]` to `[1:00:31]`.
- Encoder-only, encoder-decoder, and decoder-only models pretrain differently, which is why BERT, T5, and GPT behave differently at inference time. Source: `[40:48]` to `[1:04:00]`.
- The lecture closes by showing that scale, in-context learning, chain-of-thought prompting, and compute-optimal training are now central research questions. Source: `[1:07:03]` to `[1:18:38]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Subword tokenization.** Split words into reusable pieces so unseen forms can still be represented. Source: `[6:02]` to `[8:51]`.
- **UNK collapse.** The old fallback of mapping everything unknown to one token, which throws away too much information. Source: `[4:14]` to `[4:26]`.
- **Contextual representations.** Word meaning depends on the surrounding sentence, not just the surface form. Source: `[16:51]` to `[18:49]`.
- **Pretraining / fine-tuning.** First learn from raw text at scale, then adapt to a task. Source: `[17:03]` to `[19:33]`.
- **Masked language modeling.** BERT-style fill-in-the-blank pretraining over subword tokens. Source: `[40:48]` to `[48:13]`.
- **Next sentence prediction.** The extra BERT objective the lecture says RoBERTa later showed was unnecessary. Source: `[47:37]` to `[48:13]`, `[55:05]` to `[55:19]`.
- **Span corruption.** T5-style masking of chunks rather than isolated tokens. Source: `[59:18]` to `[1:00:31]`.
- **Decoder-only language modeling.** GPT-style next-token prediction with causal masking. Source: `[1:03:04]` to `[1:04:00]`.
- **In-context learning.** Tasks emerge from examples in the prompt without gradient updates. Source: `[1:07:32]` to `[1:08:41]`.
- **Chain of thought.** Prompting with intermediate reasoning steps to improve accuracy on harder problems. Source: `[1:11:52]` to `[1:13:47]`.
- **Chinchilla scaling.** More data can beat more parameters; GPT-3 was oversized relative to compute-optimal training. Source: `[1:10:43]` to `[1:11:39]`.
- **Parameter-efficient fine-tuning.** Add small task-specific changes instead of retraining every weight. Source: `[56:11]` to `[58:34]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The pretraining objective is not just compression. It forces the model to learn syntax, semantics, coreference, sentiment, and trivia because those are all useful for reconstructing text. Source: `[20:07]` to `[23:26]`.
- The main value of pretraining is not magical zero-shot behavior. It is a better starting point and a dramatic reduction in labeled-data requirements. Source: `[18:53]` to `[19:33]`, `[25:52]` to `[26:42]`.
- BERT and GPT are complements, not substitutes. BERT is strong at filling in blanks and representation learning; GPT is strong at generation. Source: `[53:31]` to `[54:13]`, `[1:03:22]` to `[1:04:00]`.
- In-context learning looks like a new capability, but the lecture treats it as scale exposing pattern completion behavior that was already latent in the model. Source: `[1:07:32]` to `[1:09:54]`.
- Chinchilla is the lecture's warning shot against parameter-count worship. Compute allocation matters more than raw size. Source: `[1:10:43]` to `[1:11:39]`.
- Chain-of-thought is presented as explicit scratchpad computation: the model gets more room to think because it is allowed to generate the reasoning steps. Source: `[1:11:52]` to `[1:13:47]`.

## Connections
- This is the scale-era continuation of the distributional hypothesis from L01: meaning is still context, but now the context is used to train the whole network.
- BERT and RoBERTa connect to the encoder side of L08.
- T5 connects to the encoder-decoder side of L08 and uses span corruption instead of plain next-token prediction.
- GPT, GPT-2, and GPT-3 connect to the decoder-only side of L08 and are the direct ancestors of modern chat-style LLMs.
- The lecture also ties back to the project advice from L07: the practical answer now is usually "use pretrained models and adapt them," not "train from scratch."

## Referenced Papers
- **Mikolov et al. (2013), word2vec.** Used as the contrast case: static embeddings are not enough once context matters. Source: `[15:03]` to `[18:49]`.
- **Devlin et al. (2018), BERT.** The encoder-only pretraining baseline with masked language modeling and next sentence prediction. Source: `[40:48]` to `[48:13]`.
- **Liu et al. (2019), RoBERTa.** The lecture cites it as the cleaner drop-in replacement that drops next sentence prediction. Source: `[55:05]` to `[55:19]`.
- **Raffel et al. (2020), T5.** The span-corruption encoder-decoder model. Source: `[59:18]` to `[1:00:31]`.
- **Radford et al. (2018), GPT.** The original decoder-only pretrained model. Source: `[1:03:04]` to `[1:04:28]`.
- **Radford et al. (2019), GPT-2.** The larger decoder-only model that made long coherent generation look practical. Source: `[1:05:43]` to `[1:06:31]`.
- **Brown et al. (2020), GPT-3.** The jump to in-context learning and few-shot prompting. Source: `[1:07:03]` to `[1:09:54]`.
- **Hoffmann et al. (2022), Chinchilla.** The compute-optimal scaling result that says GPT-3 was too large for its training budget. Source: `[1:10:43]` to `[1:11:39]`.
- **Gururangan et al. (2020), Don't Stop Pretraining.** The lecture explicitly names the paper as the continuation-pretraining trick. Source: `[1:17:03]` to `[1:17:20]`.
