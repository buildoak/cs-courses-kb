# L03 Modern SQL

This lecture is about the SQL surface area that matters once you already know `SELECT *`. The point is portability, expressiveness, and knowing where the standards and vendor behavior diverge.

## Summary
- SQL is treated as the durable application-level language for relational systems, but the lecture shows that engines disagree on basic behavior. Source: `[0:30]` to `[8:34]`, `[15:44]` to `[18:35]`.
- SQL's core mismatch with the relational model is bag semantics: duplicates and order are allowed unless you ask otherwise. Source: `[9:23]` to `[10:15]`.
- Aggregation, `GROUP BY`, and `HAVING` are the first advanced tools; they derive new facts from existing tuples. Source: `[11:23]` to `[21:42]`.
- Strings and dates are where portability breaks fastest. Source: `[21:45]` to `[41:35]`.
- Window functions, nested queries, lateral joins, and CTEs are the actual power tools for hard queries. Source: `[43:10]` to `[1:12:24]`.
- `EXPLAIN` is how you see whether the engine rewrote your query into something sensible. Source: `[56:49]` to `[1:03:07]`.

## Key Concepts
- **DML / DDL / DCL.** SQL is split into data manipulation, data definition, and data control. Source: `[8:34]` to `[9:21]`.
- **Bags vs sets.** SQL keeps duplicates by default; the relational model does not. Source: `[9:23]` to `[10:15]`.
- **Aggregation.** `COUNT`, `AVG`, `MIN`, `MAX`, `SUM`, and user-defined aggregates compress many tuples into one scalar. Source: `[11:23]` to `[12:00]`.
- **`GROUP BY`.** Grouping makes non-aggregated columns legal again by explicitly partitioning the input. Source: `[13:22]` to `[19:29]`.
- **`HAVING`.** This is the post-aggregation filter, not a normal `WHERE`. Source: `[19:41]` to `[21:23]`.
- **Strings.** `LIKE`, case sensitivity, quoting, and concatenation vary across systems. Source: `[21:45]` to `[26:46]`.
- **Dates and time.** `now`, `current timestamp`, casts, time zones, and date arithmetic are all engine-specific pain points. Source: `[27:12]` to `[41:35]`.
- **Output shaping.** `DISTINCT`, `ORDER BY`, `LIMIT`, and pagination are the basic control knobs on output. Source: `[41:48]` to `[43:05]`.
- **Window functions.** `row_number`, `rank`, `partition by`, and `order by` inside `OVER` handle per-group ordering problems. Source: `[43:10]` to `[49:58]`.
- **Nested queries.** Inner queries can appear in `SELECT`, `FROM`, `WHERE`, and `ORDER BY`. Source: `[50:07]` to `[55:31]`.
- **`IN`, `ANY`, `EXISTS`.** These are different faces of subquery matching. Source: `[56:00]` to `[57:20]`.
- **Lateral joins.** `LATERAL` and `CROSS APPLY` let later subqueries see earlier results. Source: `[1:06:24]` to `[1:09:29]`.
- **CTEs.** Common table expressions are named query blocks that behave like scoped temp tables. Source: `[1:10:53]` to `[1:15:28]`.
- **`EXPLAIN`.** Reading the plan matters because the optimizer may rewrite your SQL into joins or nested loops. Source: `[57:04]` to `[1:03:07]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The hardest SQL bugs are portability bugs. Syntax is the easy part; semantics drift across engines even on things like dates and string comparisons.
- `GROUP BY` is not cosmetic. It is the point where the query becomes well-defined again after aggregation.
- Window functions are the clean answer to ranking problems like "second highest grade per course." They encode ordering without collapsing the result set.
- Lateral joins and CTEs feel like temp tables, but the optimizer is still free to rewrite them. The abstraction is about meaning, not guaranteed execution order.
- The lecture's practical rule is simple: keep the work in one query when you can, then inspect the plan instead of guessing.
