# L15 Query Planning & Optimization

This lecture is about paying for plans before you run them: statistics, join order, and the optimizer's cost model.

## Summary
- The optimizer starts with catalog metadata and statistics, because cost-based planning is only as good as the data it has about the data. Source: `[3:49]` to `[8:29]`, `[1:09:05]` to `[1:10:05]`.
- System R-style bottom-up dynamic programming is the canonical join-order strategy: build plans from smaller subplans, keep the best costs, and reuse them. Source: `[32:37]` to `[39:36]`, `[53:13]` to `[53:38]`.
- Left-deep plans are the practical search target because they are easier to pipeline, while bushy plans expand the search space very quickly. Source: `[34:18]` to `[36:55]`.
- Cost models usually reduce everything to CPU and IO currencies, then feed those estimates into the optimizer's search. Source: `[45:16]` to `[46:32]`, `[1:06:08]` to `[1:08:01]`.
- Histograms, sketches, and sampling are the statistical tools that turn raw table scans into selectivity estimates, but correlation and skew make the math fragile. Source: `[1:10:07]` to `[1:17:22]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Catalog.** The optimizer needs schema, index, and statistics metadata before it can guess anything about cost. Source: `[3:49]` to `[5:01]`, `[1:09:05]` to `[1:09:29]`.
- **Selectivity.** A predicate's selectivity is the fraction of rows expected to survive it. Source: `[1:10:17]` to `[1:11:15]`.
- **Cardinality.** Estimated row counts are the input to join ordering and operator cost estimates. Source: `[1:10:41]` to `[1:11:15]`.
- **Histogram.** The lecture uses histogram buckets to approximate counts for predicate values. Source: `[1:10:36]` to `[1:11:01]`.
- **Equi-depth histogram.** Buckets with equal counts but variable ranges trade precision for smaller metadata. Source: `[1:16:17]` to `[1:16:44]`.
- **Sketches.** Approximate-count structures can replace or augment histograms for cost estimation. Source: `[1:15:52]` to `[1:17:22]`.
- **Sampling.** The optimizer can run background scans and use sampled statistics instead of exact counts. Source: `[1:09:29]` to `[1:09:54]`, `[1:17:22]` to `[1:18:18]`.
- **Independence assumption.** Many optimizers multiply predicate probabilities as if predicates were independent, which is often wrong. Source: `[1:12:25]` to `[1:12:44]`.
- **Correlated statistics.** Some columns need joint statistics because separate estimates can miss by orders of magnitude. Source: `[1:13:48]` to `[1:14:20]`.
- **Bottom-up optimization.** System R starts from small subplans and builds up, choosing the lowest-cost path as it goes. Source: `[32:37]` to `[34:18]`.
- **Left-deep tree.** A join order where each new join extends the right side of an already-built chain. Source: `[34:46]` to `[35:17]`.
- **Bushy plan.** A tree where two joined subtrees can be combined later, which broadens the search space. Source: `[35:01]` to `[35:29]`.
- **Memo table.** The optimizer stores subplan costs so that later branches can reuse them instead of recomputing everything. Source: `[53:03]` to `[53:38]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Inference: Cost-based optimization is not really about arithmetic. It is a search problem where arithmetic is the pruning heuristic.
- Inference: The optimizer does not need perfect estimates. It needs estimates good enough to avoid obviously bad branches.
- Inference: The expensive part is not always the formula. It is getting the statistics pipeline, memoization, and search bounds to line up.
- Inference: Correlation is the quiet killer. Uniformity assumptions are simple, but they break as soon as real data clusters.
- Inference: Bushy plans are not "worse" in the abstract. They are just too expensive to search unless the optimizer has a very good reason.
- Inference: The catalog is not just schema storage. It is the optimizer's memory of the world.
