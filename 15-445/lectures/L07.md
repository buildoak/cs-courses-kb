# L07 Memory & Disk I/O Management

This lecture is about how the DBMS keeps the illusion that memory is bigger than RAM. The real topic is control: spatial locality, temporal locality, eviction, pinning, and who gets to decide what stays hot.

## Summary
- The DBMS wants related data laid out together on disk so one I/O can pull in work that will be used together. It also wants hot pages to stay in memory long enough to pay off. Source: `[2:56]` to `[4:10]`.
- The buffer pool manager is the DBMS-owned bridge between disk pages and in-memory frames. It maps page IDs to locations and hands the execution engine pointers to page contents. Source: `[5:19]` to `[6:33]`.
- The page table and its metadata are doing real work: dirty flags track writes, pin counts keep in-use pages from being evicted, and latches prevent races on replacements. Source: `[12:19]` to `[16:26]`.
- LRU and clock are the baseline replacement policies, but sequential flooding shows why naive recency can be wrong for analytical scans. Source: `[33:54]` to `[41:12]`.
- The lecture is blunt about `mmap` and the OS page cache: the DBMS knows more about query intent, durability, and future access, so it should keep control. Source: `[28:48]` to `[31:45]`, `[59:07]` to `[1:03:42]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Spatial locality.** Put related pages near each other so a single read does more useful work. Source: `[2:56]` to `[3:20]`.
- **Temporal locality.** Keep a page in memory if more queries or operations are likely to need it soon. Source: `[3:44]` to `[4:00]`.
- **Buffer pool.** The DBMS-managed cache of page frames in memory. Source: `[5:02]` to `[5:25]`.
- **Page ID to frame mapping.** The page table tells the system where a page currently lives in the buffer pool. Source: `[12:19]` to `[12:46]`.
- **Pinning.** A page cannot be evicted while a caller still holds it. Source: `[13:41]` to `[16:26]`.
- **Dirty bit.** A page that has been modified has to be written back before eviction. Source: `[13:32]` to `[14:09]`, `[51:02]` to `[53:32]`.
- **LRU.** The least-recently used page is the default eviction candidate when exact history is cheap enough to maintain. Source: `[34:40]` to `[35:44]`.
- **Clock.** An approximate LRU policy that uses a per-page reference bit instead of exact timestamps. Source: `[36:15]` to `[39:26]`.
- **Sequential flooding.** A scan can make the most recently used page the one you actually want to evict. Source: `[39:40]` to `[41:12]`.
- **OS page cache vs DBMS buffer pool.** The OS cannot see query semantics or logging state, so the DBMS often does better with direct control. Source: `[59:07]` to `[1:03:42]`.
- **Prefetching.** Works when the access pattern is predictable; hurts when the access path is not. Source: `[1:11:46]` to `[1:14:17]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The buffer pool is not just a cache. It is a policy engine that encodes workload knowledge the OS does not have. Source: `[30:15]` to `[31:45]`.
- Smarter eviction is not always better. If the policy costs more than the I/O it saves, the system loses. Source: `[33:09]` to `[33:24]`.
- Recency alone is a weak signal under scans. The lecture shows why OLAP workloads can invert the meaning of "hot". Source: `[39:40]` to `[41:12]`.
- Dirty-page handling is entangled with logging. You cannot treat eviction as a pure memory decision. Source: `[33:25]` to `[33:39]`, `[51:41]` to `[53:32]`.
- `mmap` looks elegant until you need observability, flush control, and DBMS-specific policy. Then it becomes a liability. Source: `[28:48]` to `[31:45]`.
- Prefetch only helps when the plan can predict the future with enough confidence to justify the extra I/O. Source: `[1:11:46]` to `[1:14:17]`.
