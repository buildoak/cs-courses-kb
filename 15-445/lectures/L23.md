# L23 Distributed OLTP Database Systems

This lecture is the hard part of “one logical database”: deciding when a transaction is allowed to commit when no single machine can see the whole world.

## Summary
- Atomic commit and consensus are the same ordering problem viewed from two communities. The database world asks who may commit; the distributed-systems world asks which state machine entry is next. Source: `[43:08]` to `[44:43]`.
- Two-phase commit is the baseline protocol: prepare, vote, then commit or abort. The coordinator logs inbound and outbound messages so recovery can reconstruct which phase each node reached. Source: `[44:48]` to `[58:23]`.
- Early acknowledgement after prepare is the common latency optimization. Early prepare voting exists too, but the lecture treats it as rare because the client usually cannot promise that the current statement is the last statement. Source: `[58:31]` to `[1:01:15]`.
- Paxos, Raft, and viewstamped replication avoid the blocking hole by switching to majority agreement and leadership. That is why the lecture keeps comparing them back to 2PC. Source: `[45:52]` to `[46:23]`, `[1:06:03]` to `[1:16:17]`.
- Spanner, CockroachDB, TiDB, and Yugabyte show up as the practical systems that pay for leases, heartbeats, and clock discipline instead of pretending coordination is free. Source: `[1:04:08]` to `[1:15:38]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Atomic commit.** The distributed version of “may this transaction be acknowledged as committed?” Source: `[43:08]` to `[43:25]`.
- **Consensus.** Agreement on the commit order of transactions across replicas. Source: `[43:13]` to `[43:45]`.
- **Coordinator.** The node that collects votes and decides the final outcome. Source: `[44:48]` to `[45:28]`.
- **Participant.** A node that votes yes or no based on its own local state. Source: `[53:03]` to `[54:52]`.
- **Prepare phase.** The vote-collection phase of 2PC. Source: `[52:16]` to `[52:27]`.
- **Commit phase.** The phase where the coordinator tells everyone to apply the transaction. Source: `[52:23]` to `[52:47]`.
- **Synchronous commit.** Flush before ack. Source: `[52:31]` to `[52:47]`.
- **Early acknowledgement.** Ack after prepare, before the final commit broadcast finishes. Source: `[59:48]` to `[1:01:15]`.
- **Viewstamped replication.** Barbara Liskov’s predecessor to the Paxos family. Source: `[45:52]` to `[46:05]`.
- **Paxos.** Majority-based commit / replication protocol. Source: `[46:01]` to `[46:23]`, `[1:06:03]` to `[1:10:18]`.
- **Raft.** The simplified Paxos-style protocol the lecture uses for comparison. Source: `[44:31]` to `[44:43]`, `[1:16:07]` to `[1:16:17]`.
- **Leader lease.** A time-bounded leadership claim that reduces uncertainty about who may propose. Source: `[1:14:09]` to `[1:15:38]`.
- **CAP theorem.** The partition story underneath the lecture’s discussion of availability and blocking. Source: `[15:16]` to `[15:19]`, `[23:58]` to `[24:43]`.
- **Hybrid logical clocks.** The timing workaround the lecture mentions through CockroachDB. Source: `[1:04:32]` to `[1:04:53]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- 2PC is really about commit order, not message choreography. The messages are just how the database records the order everyone agreed to. Source: `[43:08]` to `[44:18]`.
- The painful case is not the happy path. It is recovery after prepare, when the transaction might already be committed but the coordinator or participant has not fully learned the outcome yet. Source: `[55:01]` to `[57:06]`.
- Early ack is safe because once every participant has voted yes, the transaction is committed even if the last acknowledgement is still in flight. Source: `[59:48]` to `[1:01:15]`.
- Paxos and 2PC are closer than people admit. The lecture explicitly treats 2PC as a degenerate case of Paxos once you think in terms of commit order and quorum. Source: `[1:06:03]` to `[1:06:14]`.
- Time sync is an implementation budget, not a theorem. Spanner pays for atomic clocks; CockroachDB approximates the same thing with hybrid logical clocks and leases. Source: `[1:04:08]` to `[1:15:38]`.
