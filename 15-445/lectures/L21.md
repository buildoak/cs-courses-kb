# L21 Distributed Transactional Databases

This lecture collapses distributed OLTP into one question: who gets to say the transaction committed? Once you have more than one node, commit order is the system.

## Summary
- Two-phase commit is the base atomic-commit protocol: prepare, then commit. The coordinator waits for participants to vote before it tells the client the transaction succeeded. Source: `[43:08]` to `[45:28]`, `[51:28]` to `[52:47]`.
- Participants make local decisions based on their own concurrency-control or constraint checks. One abort vote kills the transaction immediately. Source: `[53:03]` to `[54:52]`.
- 2PC is durable because each node logs its inbound and outbound messages. If a node crashes mid-protocol, the log tells it where to resume. Source: `[55:01]` to `[57:06]`.
- Consensus protocols solve the same ordering problem as commit protocols. The lecture treats 2PC, Paxos, and Raft as variants of the same agreement question. Source: `[43:08]` to `[46:23]`, `[1:16:00]` to `[1:16:17]`.
- CAP is the failure-mode frame for the whole lecture: when the network partitions, you pick consistency or availability. Split brain is the thing that happens when you do not. Source: `[1:17:11]` to `[1:20:42]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Atomic commit.** The distributed version of "may this transaction be acknowledged as committed?" Source: `[43:08]` to `[43:25]`.
- **2PC.** Two-phase commit, with a prepare phase and a commit phase. Source: `[44:45]` to `[45:28]`.
- **Coordinator.** The node that asks participants to vote and then decides the final outcome. Source: `[51:28]` to `[52:47]`.
- **Participant.** A node that votes yes or no based on its local state. Source: `[53:03]` to `[54:52]`.
- **Synchronous commit.** Wait for flushes before acknowledging success. Source: `[52:27]` to `[52:47]`.
- **Early ack.** Return success to the client after prepare, but finish the protocol in the background. Source: `[59:48]` to `[1:01:15]`.
- **Paxos / Raft.** Consensus protocols that make leader election and replication safe under failures. Source: `[44:23]` to `[46:23]`, `[1:16:00]` to `[1:16:17]`.
- **Viewstamped replication.** Barbara Liskov's alternative that the lecture cites alongside Paxos. Source: `[45:52]` to `[46:10]`.
- **CAP theorem.** Under a partition, a system cannot keep both consistency and availability. Source: `[1:17:11]` to `[1:20:42]`.
- **Split brain.** Both sides of a partition think they are primary and accept writes independently. Source: `[1:18:48]` to `[1:19:42]`.
- **Vector clocks.** A client-visible conflict-resolution scheme that shifts complexity to application code. Source: `[1:21:00]` to `[1:21:54]`.
- **Leases.** Time-bounded leadership, used by systems like Spanner to reduce leader ambiguity. Source: `[1:15:21]` to `[1:15:38]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- 2PC and consensus are the same problem in disguise: both are deciding the order in which the cluster is allowed to commit. Source: `[43:08]` to `[43:45]`.
- The painful part of 2PC is not the happy path. It is crash recovery after prepare, when nobody knows yet whether the transaction is committed. Source: `[55:01]` to `[57:06]`, `[1:02:01]` to `[1:02:40]`.
- A participant's vote is local. It is based on its own locks, validation, and constraint checks, not on what the other nodes see. Source: `[53:03]` to `[54:12]`.
- Early ack lowers latency, but it does not remove the need to finish the protocol durably. Source: `[59:48]` to `[1:01:15]`.
- CAP is a failure story, not a day-to-day operation story. In normal operation you still choose a latency/consistency point. Source: `[1:22:00]` to `[1:22:50]`.
- Vector clocks are technically elegant and operationally miserable. The lecture's verdict is basically "do not make ordinary developers resolve distributed conflicts by hand." Source: `[1:21:00]` to `[1:21:54]`.
