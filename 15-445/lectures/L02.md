# L02 Relational Model and Algebra

This lecture is the actual bedrock. It explains why the relational model exists, how it separates logical meaning from physical storage, and why relational algebra is the manipulation layer underneath SQL.

## Summary
- The relational model is the answer to schema brittleness: keep application code away from physical layout and let the database absorb representation changes. Source: `[23:29]` to `[27:19]`, `[30:25]` to `[33:07]`.
- The model has three pillars: relations plus schemas, integrity constraints, and a manipulation language. Source: `[34:37]` to `[35:00]`, `[44:24]` to `[47:49]`.
- Relations are unordered sets of tuples with atomic values; primary keys and foreign keys encode identity and relationships. Source: `[38:08]` to `[45:18]`.
- Relational algebra starts with `select` and `project`, then extends to `union`, `intersection`, `difference`, cartesian product, and joins. Source: `[47:46]` to `[59:35]`.
- The lecture ends by showing that JSON and vector features are pressure on the edges, not a replacement for relations. Source: `[1:05:25]` to `[1:14:47]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Data model.** A high-level abstraction for representing data and relationships in a database. Source: `[17:31]` to `[17:47]`.
- **Schema.** The structure that says what tables or collections exist, what attributes they have, and what types those attributes use. Source: `[18:16]` to `[19:15]`.
- **Data independence.** Physical, logical, and external schemas let the application stay stable while storage or presentation changes underneath. Source: `[35:01]` to `[37:35]`.
- **Relation / tuple / attribute.** A relation is an unordered set; a tuple is one entity instance; attributes are the fields that describe it. Source: `[38:08]` to `[39:59]`.
- **Atomicity and null.** The original model wanted scalar values only, but `null` survives as an explicit unknown. Source: `[39:03]` to `[39:37]`.
- **Primary key.** A set of attributes that uniquely identifies a tuple. Source: `[40:02]` to `[41:20]`.
- **Identity columns.** Systems may synthesize keys when you do not want a natural key. Source: `[41:28]` to `[42:01]`.
- **Foreign keys.** These connect one relation to another through values, not pointers. Source: `[42:07]` to `[44:22]`.
- **Constraints.** `UNIQUE`, `NOT NULL`, `CHECK`, and assertions encode allowed data shapes. Source: `[44:24]` to `[46:13]`.
- **DML.** The lecture contrasts procedural query execution with declarative algebra. Source: `[46:15]` to `[47:44]`.
- **`select` and `project`.** `select` filters tuples; `project` reshapes and computes output attributes. Source: `[47:46]` to `[51:20]`.
- **Set operators.** `union`, `intersection`, and `difference` only work when relation shapes match. Source: `[51:24]` to `[55:09]`.
- **`cross join` and join.** Cartesian product is the naive basis; join is the useful version that matches on shared attributes. Source: `[55:25]` to `[59:35]`.
- **Modern extensions.** JSON/document types and vector indexes are treated as additions that the relational model can absorb. Source: `[1:05:25]` to `[1:12:24]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The real invention is not "tables." It is the boundary that keeps physical storage out of application code.
- The model turns schema change from a code rewrite into a database concern. That is the point.
- Duplicate handling and row order are not core relational promises. That ambiguity is what gives the optimizer room to work.
- The lecture treats JSON and vectors as evidence of relational dominance, not evidence against it. The model keeps expanding around new data shapes.
- Joins are the real performance battlefield. Everything else is setup.
