# L20 Distributed Database Architectures

This lecture is about where the data lives and how much pain you accept when the cluster changes. The real topic is partitioning, metadata, and rebalancing, not SQL.

## Summary
- Distributed databases differ from parallel databases because the nodes are far apart and failures matter. Source: `[2:14]` to `[4:18]`.
- Shared-nothing is the classic architecture: each node owns its own CPU, memory, and disk. It is fast for local work, but capacity scaling means data movement. Source: `[13:14]` to `[17:39]`.
- Partitioning is the main control surface. The lecture walks through table, vertical, horizontal, hash, range, and predicate partitioning, plus partition-key choice. Source: `[17:57]` to `[21:12]`, `[30:25]` to `[41:01]`.
- Consistent hashing reduces the amount of data that has to move when nodes are added or removed. Source: `[46:17]` to `[48:18]`.
- Replication improves availability but adds coordination. Dynamo and Cassandra are the concrete examples the lecture keeps returning to. Source: `[48:38]` to `[53:18]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Distributed DB.** A system where nodes can be geographically or operationally far apart, so communication cost and failure modes dominate. Source: `[4:10]` to `[4:18]`.
- **Shared-nothing.** Each node has local CPU, memory, and disk, and nodes coordinate only over the network. Source: `[13:14]` to `[13:36]`.
- **Metadata catalog.** A routing layer that tells clients which node owns a key or partition. Source: `[18:19]` to `[18:36]`, `[43:33]` to `[43:52]`.
- **Partitioning.** The mechanism that decides which node owns which rows or columns. Source: `[17:57]` to `[21:12]`.
- **Hash partitioning.** Good for even spread, but painful when the number of partitions changes. Source: `[38:22]` to `[41:01]`, `[45:51]` to `[46:05]`.
- **Range partitioning.** Useful when the key has structure and the range boundaries match the workload. Source: `[38:45]` to `[39:10]`.
- **Predicate partitioning.** Manual partitioning by workload or value class. Source: `[38:45]` to `[39:10]`.
- **Consistent hashing.** A way to add and remove partitions without rebalancing everything. Source: `[46:17]` to `[48:18]`.
- **Replication factor.** The number of copies maintained for availability and fault tolerance. Source: `[48:38]` to `[48:58]`.
- **Hierarchical replication.** The newer DynamoDB direction the lecture contrasts with older consistent-hashing layouts. Source: `[49:21]` to `[49:31]`.
- **Hot partition.** A partition that gets more traffic than its neighbors and forces scaling or re-sharding. Source: `[45:05]` to `[45:45]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Shared-nothing is not just an architecture slogan. It is a data-movement contract: add nodes, and you pay in copying, not just in compute. Source: `[16:14]` to `[16:49]`.
- The hard problem is often rebalancing, not query execution. Once data sits in the right node, the local work is easy; moving it there is what hurts. Source: `[16:14]` to `[17:02]`, `[45:51]` to `[46:05]`.
- Partition-key choice is a performance decision, not just a schema decision. A bad key forces scatter/gather and blows up latency. Source: `[39:44]` to `[41:01]`.
- Consistent hashing is about minimizing churn, not eliminating it. You still move some data, just not the whole cluster. Source: `[46:17]` to `[48:18]`.
- Replication and partitioning are coupled. Once you add replicas, you need a plan for authoritative copies, cache locality, and coordination. Source: `[48:38]` to `[49:31]`, `[52:57]` to `[53:18]`.
- The catalog is effectively a distributed access-path selector. It is the database equivalent of route planning. Source: `[18:19]` to `[18:36]`, `[43:33]` to `[43:52]`.

## Connections
- To `L19`: local durability does not solve cluster placement. Recovery keeps one node safe; partitioning decides which node should own the bytes in the first place. Source: `[13:14]` to `[17:39]`, and `L19` `[6:10]` to `[11:37]`.
- To `L21`: distributed OLTP adds commit and consensus protocols on top of the partition/replica layout. Source: `[48:38]` to `[49:31]` and `L21` `[43:08]` to `[46:23]`.

- To caching and locality: consistent hashing is the same trick used to keep caches and shards sticky under membership change. Source: `[46:17]` to `[48:18]`, `[49:16]` to `[49:31]`.
