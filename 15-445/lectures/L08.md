# L08 Hash Tables

This lecture is about hash tables as DBMS machinery, not as a first-year data structure. The real question is how to keep equality lookups fast when collisions, growth, and deletions stop being theoretical.

## Summary
- Hash tables show up inside database systems as unordered maps, page-directory structures, and equality indexes. They are not just a language runtime primitive. Source: `[4:47]` to `[8:35]`, `[10:53]` to `[13:18]`.
- Static hashing is simple, but collisions and load factor pressure make it brittle once the table fills up. Source: `[13:51]` to `[24:13]`, `[27:29]` to `[29:02]`.
- Open addressing variants like linear probing, quadratic probing, Robin Hood hashing, and hopscotch hashing trade different insert and lookup costs. Source: `[24:33]` to `[25:56]`, `[33:13]` to `[35:30]`.
- Cuckoo hashing flips the tradeoff: lookups and deletions are O(1), but inserts can bounce keys around until a slot opens up. Source: `[46:04]` to `[47:00]`.
- Dynamic hashing schemes such as chain hashing, extendible hashing, and linear hashing avoid full-table rehashing by growing more locally. Source: `[51:46]` to `[1:05:57]`.
- Hash indexes are equality tools. They do not replace ordered indexes for range predicates. Source: `[1:09:05]` to `[1:09:54]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Hash table.** An unordered structure that maps keys to positions quickly on average. Source: `[4:47]` to `[5:20]`.
- **Collision.** Two different keys map to the same slot or bucket. Source: `[15:13]` to `[18:33]`.
- **Load factor.** The filled percentage of the table, which determines when growth becomes necessary. Source: `[27:29]` to `[29:02]`.
- **Linear probing.** Probe forward until you find the key or a free slot. Source: `[24:33]` to `[25:56]`.
- **Quadratic probing.** A probing strategy that changes the step size instead of walking linearly. Source: `[25:45]` to `[25:51]`.
- **Robin Hood hashing.** A way to reduce variance in probe lengths by balancing how far keys have traveled. Source: `[25:10]` to `[25:21]`, `[33:13]` to `[33:24]`.
- **Cuckoo hashing.** Use multiple hash functions and relocate victims until the table makes room. Source: `[46:04]` to `[48:00]`.
- **Chain hashing.** Keep overflow chains off the primary bucket array. Source: `[51:46]` to `[53:07]`.
- **Extendible hashing.** Split only the overflowing bucket and update the directory. Source: `[58:01]` to `[1:02:14]`.
- **Linear hashing.** Split according to a global progression instead of always splitting the overflowing bucket. Source: `[1:02:33]` to `[1:05:57]`.
- **Tombstones.** Deleted entries that stay behind so probe chains still work. Source: `[39:12]` to `[40:24]`.
- **Equality-only index.** A hash index is useful when the predicate is exact match, not order. Source: `[1:09:19]` to `[1:09:54]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The real design axis is not just average O(1). It is how the table fails when it gets full. Source: `[27:29]` to `[29:02]`.
- Cuckoo hashing moves pain from reads to writes. That is a deliberate systems tradeoff, not a free win. Source: `[46:04]` to `[47:00]`.
- Dynamic hashing is about localizing reorganization, not making rehashing disappear. Source: `[51:46]` to `[1:05:57]`.
- Tombstones are metadata debt. They keep probing correct, but they also inflate the load factor picture. Source: `[37:31]` to `[40:24]`.
- Hash indexes are narrow tools. If the query pattern changes from equality to range, the index stops being useful immediately. Source: `[1:09:05]` to `[1:09:54]`.
- The lecture keeps showing the same pattern: the data structure is easy, the maintenance policy is the hard part. Source: `[13:51]` to `[24:13]`, `[51:46]` to `[1:05:57]`.
