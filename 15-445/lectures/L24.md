# L24 Distributed OLAP Database Systems

The lecture is about moving the data once, not three times: shape the facts, keep joins shallow, and only shuffle when the query forces you.

## Summary
- Star schemas and snowflake schemas are the first OLAP design choice. Denormalize for shallow joins, or normalize for storage efficiency and cleaner updates. Source: `[9:38]` to `[15:15]`.
- Distributed OLAP keeps the single-node query-plan model, but every pipeline breaker can become a network boundary. That is why the lecture introduces a shuffle layer and a coordinator above the workers. Source: `[17:00]` to `[21:05]`.
- Broadcast joins and shuffle joins are the two main distributed join shapes. Broadcast copies the small side everywhere; shuffle repartitions both sides on the join key. Source: `[47:46]` to `[51:42]`.
- OLAP systems usually avoid global indexes. Zone maps and similar metadata pruning are the cheap path, because point-query B-trees are the wrong fit for scan-heavy workloads. Source: `[51:56]` to `[52:38]`.
- Snowflake, BigQuery, Spark, Redshift, Databricks, and serverless systems all answer the same storage/compute question differently. The lecture ends by tracing that path through shared disk, object stores, data lakes, and lakehouses. Source: `[1:04:16]` to `[1:12:58]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Star schema.** A fact table with denormalized dimension tables one hop away. Source: `[9:38]` to `[11:05]`.
- **Snowflake schema.** A more normalized version of the same fact/dimension pattern, with multiple hops through dimension tables. Source: `[11:05]` to `[15:15]`.
- **Fact table.** The big table that holds the events or transactions the analytics query is really about. Source: `[10:00]` to `[10:18]`.
- **Dimension table.** The metadata table that enriches the fact table with attributes. Source: `[9:38]` to `[10:10]`.
- **Vectorized execution.** The lecture treats this as one of the reasons OLAP stopped being painfully slow in row stores. Source: `[10:26]` to `[10:36]`.
- **Pipeline breaker.** A stage boundary where the operator has to stop and hand data to the next phase. Source: `[18:00]` to `[20:23]`.
- **Shuffle.** The distributed version of an exchange operator. Source: `[19:58]` to `[21:35]`, `[58:56]` to `[1:02:00]`.
- **Broadcast join.** A join that replicates the small side so every node can finish locally. Source: `[47:46]` to `[49:13]`.
- **Shuffle join.** A repartitioning join that realigns both sides on the join key. Source: `[49:32]` to `[51:11]`.
- **Shared disk.** Compute and storage are separated, but the shared storage layer stays authoritative. Source: `[19:00]` to `[19:15]`, `[1:04:16]` to `[1:05:01]`.
- **Shared nothing.** Each worker holds its own data locally and the cluster coordinates with shuffles. Source: `[18:52]` to `[19:15]`, `[20:39]` to `[21:05]`.
- **Zone maps.** Min/max summaries that let the engine prune files or chunks early. Source: `[51:56]` to `[52:38]`.
- **Managed storage.** The DBMS controls what lands in the storage layer. Source: `[1:10:03]` to `[1:10:40]`.
- **Data lake.** Files land directly in object storage and the catalog becomes more advisory. Source: `[1:10:43]` to `[1:11:47]`.
- **Lakehouse.** A catalog-managed layer over object storage that keeps some of the database discipline while tolerating external files. Source: `[1:11:50]` to `[1:12:23]`.
- **Serverless database.** A system that evicts idle compute and reloads state from shared storage on demand. Source: `[1:05:03]` to `[1:09:04]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Snowflake’s bet was not “better SQL.” It was cloud-native shared disk with compute/storage separation, plus a managed catalog on top. Source: `[1:04:16]` to `[1:05:01]`.
- MapReduce was a detour. The lecture argues that the important primitive was the shuffle, and BigQuery/Spark later rediscovered it inside a SQL-shaped system. Source: `[21:08]` to `[24:04]`, `[58:56]` to `[1:00:31]`.
- The expensive part of OLAP is often not the join algorithm itself. It is the data movement around it, which is why BigQuery cares so much about shuffle infrastructure. Source: `[58:56]` to `[1:00:31]`.
- Serverless is a cost model change, not the removal of servers. The compute instance still exists; it just gets evicted when idle and reconstructed from storage when needed. Source: `[1:05:03]` to `[1:09:04]`.
- Data lakes weaken managed storage, which means the catalog and pruning metadata become a separate correctness problem instead of something the DBMS can infer directly. Source: `[1:10:43]` to `[1:12:23]`.
- The lecture keeps coming back to the same rule: if you can keep the join local, everything gets easier. If you cannot, you pay in shuffles, broadcast copies, or a full repartition. Source: `[47:46]` to `[51:42]`.

## Connections
- To `L22` and `L23`: distributed OLAP uses the same data-placement instincts as distributed OLTP, but the pain shows up as shuffles and pipeline breakers instead of commit protocols. Source: `L22` `[30:25]` to `[49:31]`, `L23` `[43:08]` to `[58:23]`.
- To query execution: the shuffle is the distributed version of the exchange operator already familiar from single-node plans. Source: `[21:31]` to `[21:35]`, `[58:56]` to `[1:02:00]`.
- To storage lectures: row-store vs column-store is really a “what do you keep hot?” question, and OLAP pushes hard toward scan-friendly layout. Source: `[10:26]` to `[10:36]`, `[51:56]` to `[52:38]`.
- To cloud architecture: Snowflake and Databricks are not just products; they are different answers to who owns compute, storage, and metadata. Source: `[1:04:16]` to `[1:12:58]`.
