# L09 B+ Trees

This lecture turns the B+ tree into an implementation problem. The structure is simple on paper. The hard part is making splits, merges, sibling traversal, and concurrency behave on real pages under mutation.

## Summary
- B+ trees are the default ordered index because they are balanced, page-friendly, and efficient for both point lookups and sequential access. Source: `[11:53]` to `[14:02]`.
- The modern production version is shaped by Lehman-Yao style sibling pointers, which make concurrent access and range scans practical. Source: `[10:06]` to `[10:57]`, `[16:35]` to `[17:24]`.
- Inserts and deletes are split/merge operations that can ripple upward and change the root. Source: `[29:48]` to `[36:32]`.
- Concurrency is the real implementation tax: latching, latch crabbing, and safe split/merge rules matter as much as search complexity. Source: `[40:20]` to `[49:26]`.
- Range scans are where B+ trees beat hash tables. Leaf-level sibling pointers turn ordered access into a cheap linear walk. Source: `[23:28]` to `[27:28]`, `[50:45]` to `[57:17]`.
- Real systems bend the textbook definition with overflow pages, deduplication, and different merge thresholds. Source: `[52:14]` to `[1:05:57]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Ordered n-way tree.** The B+ tree stores keys in sorted order and uses fanout to keep the height small. Source: `[11:57]` to `[13:03]`.
- **Fanout.** The number of pointers or branches per node, which is large when pages are large. Source: `[12:11]` to `[12:55]`.
- **Root, internal nodes, leaves.** The tree has an entry point, routing nodes, and leaf pages that hold the searchable payload. Source: `[14:40]` to `[15:09]`.
- **Sibling pointers.** Leaf pages point to adjacent leaf pages so scans do not need to climb back to parents. Source: `[16:35]` to `[17:24]`.
- **Separator keys.** Internal nodes store routing keys that tell the search path where to descend. Source: `[15:37]` to `[16:10]`.
- **Half-full invariant.** Non-root nodes stay at least half full in the textbook form of the tree. Source: `[13:06]` to `[13:41]`.
- **Insert split.** When a node is full, split it and propagate separator changes upward. Source: `[29:48]` to `[33:30]`.
- **Delete merge.** When a node falls below the threshold, borrow from a sibling or merge and continue upward if needed. Source: `[35:08]` to `[36:32]`.
- **Latch crabbing.** Traverse while holding and releasing latches carefully so concurrent readers and writers stay safe. Source: `[40:20]` to `[49:26]`.
- **Secondary index vs index-organized storage.** Some systems store payloads at the leaf level; others keep only record identifiers in the leaves. Source: `[23:28]` to `[24:20]`.
- **Range scan.** The killer feature: once you land on the first leaf, sibling pointers let you walk the rest in order. Source: `[27:24]` to `[27:28]`, `[50:45]` to `[57:17]`.
- **Overflow pages.** A pragmatic alternative to immediate split/merge churn in some implementations. Source: `[52:14]` to `[54:37]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The tree is not just an algorithm. It is a page layout. That is why fanout, page size, and sibling pointers matter so much. Source: `[12:11]` to `[14:02]`, `[16:35]` to `[17:24]`.
- Sibling pointers are doing two jobs at once: they accelerate range scans and make concurrent rebalancing less painful. Source: `[16:35]` to `[17:24]`, `[40:20]` to `[49:26]`.
- The hard work is not lookup. It is mutation under concurrency. Splits and merges are where real systems earn their complexity. Source: `[29:48]` to `[36:32]`, `[40:20]` to `[49:26]`.
- Textbook B+ trees and production "B-trees" are not the same thing. Systems like PostgreSQL use pragmatic deviations to get performance. Source: `[10:26]` to `[10:57]`, `[14:14]` to `[14:31]`.
- Ordered access is why B+ trees exist next to hash tables. Hash tables win equality, but they cannot replace ordered traversal. Source: `[23:54]` to `[27:28]`, `[1:09:05]` to `[1:09:54]` in `L08`.
- The lecture keeps returning to the same constraint: page behavior drives the data structure, not the other way around. Source: `[12:40]` to `[14:02]`, `[52:14]` to `[57:17]`.

## Connections
- To `L07`: B+ trees depend on hot root pages and predictable leaf access, so the buffer pool policy matters directly. Source: `L09` `[11:57]` to `[14:02]`, `L07` `[33:54]` to `[41:12]`.
- To `L08`: hash tables are the right answer for equality predicates; B+ trees are the right answer for ordered and range predicates. Source: `L09` `[23:28]` to `[27:28]`, `L08` `[1:09:05]` to `[1:09:54]`.
- To query planning: access path selection is really a choice between hash-based equality access and order-preserving traversal. Source: `L09` `[23:28]` to `[27:28]`, `L08` `[1:09:05]` to `[1:09:54]`.
- To transactions: latches keep structural changes safe while concurrent readers continue to traverse the tree. Source: `[40:20]` to `[49:26]`.
