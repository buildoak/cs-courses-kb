# L14 Strategic Games: Search, Planning, and Grounded Dialogue

This lecture says the missing half of AI is search. Learning makes a policy; search makes it think before it moves.

## Summary
- Search beats raw parameter scaling by a ridiculous margin in poker. Adding test-time search cuts exploitability far more than simply making the model bigger. Source: `[0:45]` to `[2:27]`.
- AlphaGo Zero makes the same point in Go: once you remove Monte Carlo tree search at test time, the system is no longer superhuman. Source: `[5:28]` to `[6:25]`.
- Richard Sutton's Bitter Lesson is the framing device: the general methods that leverage computation are search and learning, and search still has room to grow. Source: `[9:54]` to `[10:39]`.
- Cicero applies the idea to Diplomacy by combining strategic reasoning with grounded natural-language dialogue. It was trained on 50,000 human games and placed in the top 10% of human players. Source: `[13:01]` to `[24:18]`, `[43:46]` to `[45:04]`.
- The real target is not a game-specific bot. It is generality: an agent that can do Diplomacy, Go, poker, writing, and math. Source: `[43:46]` to `[44:20]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Search as test-time compute.** The lecture keeps returning to the same move: spend more compute after training to choose better actions. Source: `[0:45]` to `[2:27]`, `[9:39]` to `[10:53]`.
- **Monte Carlo tree search.** The canonical search method in Go and one of the lecture's reference points for better inference-time planning. Source: `[5:28]` to `[6:25]`, `[8:50]` to `[9:08]`.
- **Pluribus.** Poker becomes dramatically stronger once search is added at decision time. Source: `[3:51]` to `[5:20]`.
- **Diplomacy as grounded dialogue.** The game is built around private negotiation, promises, support, and trust. Source: `[15:14]` to `[20:24]`.
- **Self-play limits.** Two-player zero-sum self-play converges to Minimax, but that guarantee breaks once cooperation enters the picture. Source: `[20:57]` to `[22:21]`.
- **Human data matters.** Cicero needs human games because a fully self-play-trained bot would invent a private gibberish language. Source: `[22:01]` to `[22:21]`.
- **Anchored policies.** Cicero predicts what players will do, then plans with a rationality prior before generating dialogue. Source: `[24:30]` to `[26:48]`.
- **piKL planning.** The planning stage nudges action predictions toward higher expected value while keeping the model grounded in the current state. Source: `[25:22]` to `[26:06]`.
- **Controllable dialogue.** Messages are generated conditionally on intended actions, then filtered for grounding and value. Source: `[26:09]` to `[27:21]`.
- **Generality as the endpoint.** Games should be benchmarks, not the goal itself. Source: `[43:46]` to `[44:20]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Search is not a 10x trick. In the poker example, it is closer to a 100,000x substitution for brute-force parameter scaling. Source: `[1:32]` to `[1:54]`.
- Chain-of-thought is treated as search-like behavior. The same inference-time-compute logic shows up outside classical tree search. Source: `[11:40]` to `[12:24]`.
- Diplomacy is not mainly a deception benchmark. It is a trust benchmark under incentives to betray. Source: `[20:14]` to `[20:31]`.
- The important distinction is not "game AI" versus "language AI". It is whether the system can use compute at test time to reason about the next move. Source: `[10:03]` to `[10:39]`, `[11:51]` to `[12:24]`.
- Cicero's result matters less as a game win than as a proof that strategic reasoning and natural language can be coupled in one agent. Source: `[23:03]` to `[24:18]`, `[43:46]` to `[45:04]`.
