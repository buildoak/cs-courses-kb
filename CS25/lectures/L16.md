# L16 Common Sense Reasoning: Maieutic Prompting, Symbolic Distillation, and Moral Models

This lecture argues that commonsense is not a scale-only problem. The fix is to make the model explain itself, score the explanation graph for consistency, and then filter the noisy knowledge into something smaller and better. Source: `[2:27]` to `[8:47]`, `[20:34]` to `[29:09]`, `[31:14]` to `[53:21]`.

## Summary
- GPT-3 is sometimes impressive, but the lecture opens by showing how easily it contradicts itself on simple commonsense questions. The point is not that the model is useless. The point is that it is brittle. Source: `[2:27]` to `[3:44]`.
- Maieutic prompting turns that brittleness into a graph problem: ask the model to explain true and false answers, recurse on the explanations, and keep only the branches that stay logically integral. Source: `[4:09]` to `[8:47]`.
- On CommonsenseQA 2.0 and related benchmarks, Maieutic prompting beats vanilla prompting, chain-of-thought, self-consistency, and even a supervised T5 baseline. Source: `[9:11]` to `[10:33]`.
- Symbolic knowledge distillation uses GPT-3 as a loose teacher and RoBERTa as a critic to generate a cleaner commonsense graph than the raw teacher alone. Smaller, filtered data beats bigger, noisier data. Source: `[20:34]` to `[30:03]`.
- Delphi and Commonsense Norm Bank extend the same pattern to moral and social judgments. The lecture treats norms as something to model explicitly, not something to hide behind a blacklist. Source: `[31:14]` to `[53:21]`, `[1:00:36]` to `[1:03:19]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Commonsense.** The lecture defines commonsense as basic practical knowledge about everyday situations that is widely shared, but not universal. Context can always flip what counts as common sense. Source: `[12:51]` to `[14:30]`.
- **ATOMIC / COMET.** ATOMIC is the human-crowdsourced commonsense graph. COMET turns it into a generative inference model that can produce preconditions, postconditions, and other event-centric relations. Source: `[15:05]` to `[19:05]`.
- **Maieutic prompting.** The model is asked to explain both a true answer and a false answer, then recursively explain those explanations. Logical consistency is the pruning rule. Source: `[4:09]` to `[7:10]`.
- **Belief and pairwise consistency.** Node confidence is computed from conditional probabilities. Pairwise contradictions come from an off-the-shelf NLI model. Source: `[7:13]` to `[7:55]`.
- **Max-SAT search.** The graph is turned into a constraint optimization problem, and a classical SAT solver selects labels that maximize global consistency. Source: `[7:59]` to `[8:47]`.
- **Symbolic knowledge distillation.** GPT-3 samples candidate commonsense facts, RoBERTa filters bad ones, and the resulting graph is both larger and more accurate than the human-authored subset. Source: `[20:34]` to `[29:40]`.
- **Commonsense Norm Bank.** The norms setup collects 1.7 million ethical judgments across social, cultural, and moral situations, then reformats them into QA. Source: `[36:31]` to `[41:38]`.
- **Delphi.** A commonsense model for moral and safety judgments, built on Unicorn and trained with explicit normative data. Source: `[31:14]` to `[42:57]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The strongest move in the lecture is not "ask the model again." It is "force the model to justify itself, then punish inconsistency." Source: `[4:09]` to `[8:47]`.
- The lecture keeps proving the same uncomfortable point: a smaller, cleaner teacher can beat a larger, looser one. Quantity alone does not win here. Source: `[20:34]` to `[29:09]`.
- Moral modeling is framed as descriptive ethics, not as a machine judge. The goal is to make the implicit explicit enough to study and filter. Source: `[46:01]` to `[47:19]`, `[1:08:20]` to `[1:09:31]`.
- The bias problem is not an edge case. Crowd-worker values leak into the model, so pluralism is the only defensible answer the talk can offer. Source: `[47:43]` to `[48:56]`, `[1:11:11]` to `[1:14:37]`.
- The practical safety win is a filter layer: a commonsense model can sit in front of another model and reject dangerous or toxic output before it escapes. Source: `[1:00:36]` to `[1:03:19]`.
