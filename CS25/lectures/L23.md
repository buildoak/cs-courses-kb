# L23 No Language Left Behind: Scaling Human-Centered Machine Translation

This lecture is a data-engineering talk disguised as a translation talk. The model matters, but the pipeline is the product.

## Summary
- NLLB starts from a blunt question: how do you double language coverage from roughly 100 languages to 200 without lowering quality? Source: `[7:57]` to `[8:16]`.
- FLORES-200 is the evaluation spine: many-to-many, document-level, low-resource-first, and aligned to language standards instead of convenient news-only benchmarks. Source: `[7:26]` to `[12:37]`.
- The training pipeline combines NLLB-Seed, public bitext, mining from web text, and back translation to build usable supervision where almost none existed. Source: `[13:05]` to `[26:02]`.
- Model quality comes from the interaction of sentence encoders, multilingual distillation, MoE capacity control, and curriculum learning. Source: `[15:04]` to `[30:23]`.
- Human eval and toxicity work make the real point: translation quality is about usability and safety, not just BLEU. Source: `[33:31]` to `[38:19]`.

## Key Concepts
- **FLORES-101 / FLORES-200.** The evaluation set expands the community benchmark from a small low-resource suite to a 200-language many-to-many one. Source: `[7:26]` to `[9:50]`, `[30:25]` to `[32:39]`.
- **Document-level context.** Translators see whole documents, not isolated sentences, so the benchmark can capture context-sensitive errors. Source: `[9:30]` to `[9:57]`.
- **Language standardization.** Low-resource languages often have competing orthographies or regional norms, so the first step is deciding what "correct" means. Source: `[10:07]` to `[12:37]`.
- **NLLB-Seed.** A small, high-quality seed set for languages with almost no existing data, used to bootstrap the rest of the pipeline. Source: `[13:10]` to `[13:59]`.
- **Mining pipeline.** WikiMatrix, CCMatrix, and large-scale web mining turn raw monolingual text into mined bitext. Source: `[14:28]` to `[15:04]`, `[19:58]` to `[20:33]`.
- **Sentence encoders.** Multilingual alignment quality depends on the encoder; bad embeddings make mined bitext noisy. Source: `[15:04]` to `[17:58]`.
- **Multilingual distillation.** A shared teacher model distills into family-specific low-resource students while keeping one embedding space for mining. Source: `[16:46]` to `[17:58]`.
- **Language identification.** LID is part of the data pipeline, not a side tool; better language ID improves every downstream corpus. Source: `[19:10]` to `[21:31]`.
- **Mixture of experts.** MoE helps scale and reduce language interference, but it also overfits faster on low-resource languages. Source: `[27:04]` to `[29:13]`.
- **Curriculum learning.** Low-resource languages are introduced later so they do not overfit immediately. Source: `[29:26]` to `[30:23]`.
- **Human evaluation and toxicity.** Safety is multilingual and culturally specific, so toxicity lists had to be built for all 200 languages. Source: `[36:10]` to `[38:19]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The limiting factor is often monolingual data, not model size. Many languages simply do not have enough text online to mine from. Source: `[21:53]` to `[22:32]`.
- Data cleaning is model work. The lecture treats language ID, filtering, and standardization as direct contributors to model quality. Source: `[19:10]` to `[21:31]`.
- Distillation is not only about compression. It is how the team preserves a shared space so different low-resource encoders can mine against each other. Source: `[16:46]` to `[17:58]`.
- MoE is a double-edged sword. Extra capacity helps scale, but low-resource languages overfit so fast that training can become pointless without regularization. Source: `[27:24]` to `[29:13]`.
- Human evaluation is not just nicer BLEU. It is the only way to calibrate cross-language comparability once the benchmark spans many scripts and norms. Source: `[33:31]` to `[35:52]`.
- Toxicity is not universal. A term that is benign in one language can be a slur in another, so safety data has to be language-specific. Source: `[36:37]` to `[38:09]`.
- The open-source pipeline matters as much as the paper. The talk repeatedly returns to reproducibility and community reuse. Source: `[40:22]` to `[41:39]`.
