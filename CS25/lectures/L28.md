# L28 Aligning Open Language Models

This lecture is the alignment story in fast motion: ChatGPT makes RLHF unavoidable, open-source copies chase the recipe, and evaluation tooling becomes the real steering wheel.

## Summary
- RLHF is framed as necessary but not sufficient for ChatGPT-class products; pretraining still does most of the work, but alignment changes what the model is willing to do. Source: `[5:11]` to `[6:03]`.
- The first open wave is basically synthetic-data bootstrapping plus small base models: Alpaca, Vicuna, Koala, Dolly, OpenAssistant, and QLoRA all push the field forward. Source: `[13:08]` to `[27:06]`.
- ShareGPT and later consented datasets like LMSYS-Chat-1M and WildChat matter because diversity beats narrow instruction seeds. Source: `[17:07]` to `[19:14]`.
- Evaluation quickly becomes the bottleneck: ChatBotArena, AlpacaEval, MT Bench, and the Open LLM Leaderboard create fast but imperfect feedback loops. Source: `[32:22]` to `[38:18]`.
- DPO is the big simplifier, but the talk still ends up showing that PPO often wins on the margins. Source: `[42:01]` to `[50:37]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Alignment.** Training a model to mirror user desires across safety, style, and usefulness. Source: `[9:58]` to `[10:07]`.
- **Instruction fine-tuning vs supervised fine-tuning.** Instruction tuning targets response to formatted prompts; SFT is broader task-specific training. Source: `[10:11]` to `[10:28]`.
- **Preference fine-tuning.** The umbrella for RLHF-style methods and DPO-style direct preference methods. Source: `[10:35]` to `[11:08]`.
- **Chat templates and system prompts.** Open models learn to behave like chat systems once the prompt format carries role and system information. Source: `[13:42]` to `[14:10]`.
- **Self-Instruct / Alpaca.** Seed prompts from a stronger model generate a much larger instruction corpus. Source: `[15:00]` to `[16:38]`.
- **ShareGPT / WildChat / LMSYS-Chat-1M.** Real conversational traces give more diversity than early self-instruct bootstraps. Source: `[17:07]` to `[19:14]`.
- **OpenAssistant.** Human-written prompts, responses, and ratings become one of the first genuinely successful open alignment datasets and models. Source: `[22:49]` to `[24:27]`.
- **QLoRA.** Memory reduction makes fine-tuning accessible on smaller hardware. Source: `[24:51]` to `[27:06]`.
- **RLHF.** The classic policy / reward model / KL-constrained optimization loop. Source: `[42:01]` to `[43:37]`.
- **DPO.** Direct gradient ascent on log probabilities stands in for explicit reward modeling. Source: `[43:48]` to `[45:07]`.
- **Evaluation stack.** ChatBotArena, AlpacaEval, MT Bench, Open LLM Leaderboard, and WildBench all solve a different version of "what is better?" Source: `[32:22]` to `[39:40]`.
- **Safety classifiers.** Llama Guard / output filters separate refusal detection from the generative model itself. Source: `[40:11]` to `[40:49]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The field's progress is as much about data release mechanics and licensing as it is about algorithms. Llama 1's delta files and Llama 2's licensing materially shaped adoption. Source: `[20:10]` to `[21:10]`.
- The evaluation layer became the engineering loop because human eval is too slow and expensive, but judge models come with their own biases. Source: `[33:14]` to `[38:18]`, `[39:48]` to `[40:49]`.
- DPO is popular partly because it is simple to implement and works with existing log-prob infrastructure, not because it is theoretically settled. Source: `[44:30]` to `[45:07]`.
- PPO keeps winning some comparisons, especially when groups control the dataset and optimization carefully. Source: `[49:01]` to `[50:37]`.
- Alignment is not just safety. The talk explicitly ties RLHF to user experience, code, and math improvements. Source: `[1:02:03]` to `[1:02:30]`.
- Most of the leverage sits in the 7B/13B regime. The lecture keeps returning to small-model alignment because that is where open iteration is actually practical. Source: `[59:04]` to `[59:25]`.
- More open human preference data is the real bottleneck. Anthropic HH, UltraFeedback, Nectar, and OpenAssistant all sit on top of that scarcity. Source: `[57:54]` to `[58:25]`.

## Connections
- To L22: the judge problem shows up again, except now the judges are GPT-4, humans, and leaderboards rather than a chatbot preference dataset.
- To L23: dataset choice is destiny. ShareGPT, OpenAssistant, UltraFeedback, and evaluation set design drive the apparent progress the way FLORES did for translation.
- To L24: once alignment and judges exist, the system boundary expands. What counts as "the model" is already becoming a stack of filters, policies, and tools.
