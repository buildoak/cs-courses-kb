# L39 Transformers United: Multimodal World Models for Drug Discovery

This is a simulator talk, not a classifier talk.

## Summary
- The talk defines a world model as a system that can simulate patient biology and answer counterfactual questions like "what happens if I give this drug to this patient?"
- The data stack is deliberately multimodal and spatially aligned: H&E, 16-plex protein immunofluorescence, 1,000-plex spatial transcriptomics, and whole-exome sequencing.
- A masked-autoencoder backbone with aggressive masking forces the model to infer cell state from sparse context and to translate between modalities.
- The same machinery is used for counterfactual drug simulation, tumor-marker prediction, patient embedding, and latent clustering by genetics.
- The closing argument is scale plus integration: more modalities, more organs, more patients, and interpretability on top.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The useful move is not concatenation. It is learning one modality well enough to predict another.
- High masking ratios are a feature: the model learns to stay useful with almost no direct evidence.
- Synthetic neighborhoods are more valuable than generic synthetic data because they preserve real spatial context.
- H&E can recover patient genetics in latent space because paired multimodal training forces biology into the representation.
- The main challenge is not just prediction. It is choosing the right intervention and the right scale of simulation.
