# L13 Emergent Abilities: Scale, Data, and Prompting

This lecture treats emergence as a practical question, not a mystical one: when does scaling flip a model from useless to useful, and when does the evaluation itself create the illusion of a threshold?

## Summary
- Emergence is defined operationally: if small models are at chance and large models beat chance, the ability counts as emergent. Source: `[2:35]` to `[5:05]`.
- The lecture tracks three axes of scale: parameters, compute, and data. Better data can move emergence earlier just as much as bigger models can. Source: `[2:49]` to `[3:28]`, `[18:00]` to `[21:13]`.
- BIG-bench and MMLU are used to show threshold behavior, including cases where medium models get worse before large models recover the behavior. Source: `[6:34]` to `[10:25]`, `[13:24]` to `[14:00]`.
- RLHF, few-shot prompting, and chain-of-thought are treated as emergent behaviors too, not just as implementation tricks. Source: `[14:08]` to `[15:53]`, `[25:15]` to `[26:58]`, `[38:00]` to `[54:22]`.
- The field shift is social as well as technical: scaling plus prompting changed what people count as state of the art. Source: `[25:15]` to `[27:31]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Three axes of scale.** Parameters, training FLOPS, and training data are the basic lenses for measuring model size. Source: `[2:49]` to `[3:28]`.
- **Operational emergence.** A capability is emergent when it is absent at small scale and present at large scale. Source: `[4:38]` to `[5:05]`.
- **MMLU.** The lecture uses it as a broad benchmark where larger models show clear threshold effects. Source: `[6:34]` to `[7:50]`.
- **BIG-bench.** The benchmark supplies the stranger cases, including partial-credit traps and tasks that only open up at larger scale. Source: `[8:05]` to `[10:25]`, `[13:24]` to `[14:00]`.
- **Metric dependence.** Sparse scoring and partial-credit rules can make a capability look emergent even when the underlying curve is smoother. Source: `[12:00]` to `[13:15]`.
- **Data quality.** In-domain, higher-quality data can shift emergence to smaller models. Source: `[20:11]` to `[21:13]`.
- **Teacher-to-student transfer.** Large models can generate data that teaches smaller models the same behavior. Source: `[21:17]` to `[22:43]`.
- **Perplexity as proxy.** The lecture treats dev-set perplexity as another way to track downstream emergence. Source: `[23:03]` to `[24:04]`.
- **Prompting.** Few-shot prompting is not separate from emergence; it is part of the same story. Source: `[25:15]` to `[26:58]`.
- **Chain-of-thought.** The lecture frames it as a test-time-compute trick that can unlock abilities in smaller models. Source: `[38:00]` to `[54:22]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- A task can look emergent because the metric is brittle. Change the scoring rule and the threshold moves. Source: `[12:00]` to `[13:15]`.
- A medium model can get worse before a large model recovers the behavior. The curve is not monotonic by default. Source: `[9:45]` to `[10:25]`, `[13:24]` to `[14:00]`.
- Emergence is not locked to one model size. Better data and architecture can shift the threshold left. Source: `[18:25]` to `[19:12]`, `[20:11]` to `[21:13]`.
- Bigger models can be teachers. Once a behavior is found, it can often be distilled back into smaller systems. Source: `[21:17]` to `[22:43]`.
- Predicting future emergent abilities is a real open problem. The lecture calls it promising and underexplored. Source: `[27:57]` to `[29:55]`.
- Chain-of-thought behaves like search over latent reasoning paths, which makes test-time compute part of the scaling story. Source: `[39:56]` to `[42:28]`, `[53:23]` to `[54:22]`.
