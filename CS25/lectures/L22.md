# L22 Recipe for Training Helpful Chatbots

This lecture is the alignment pipeline in miniature: data curation, preference modeling, and evaluation all matter more than the headline architecture.

## Summary
- H4 tries to recreate the InstructGPT recipe on open models: supervised fine-tuning, RLHF, then distillation and evaluation. Source: `[0:50]` to `[3:23]`.
- The SFT data landscape splits into human-written sets, synthetic bootstraps like Self-Instruct, and hybrid pipelines such as UltraChat and CAMEL. Source: `[3:41]` to `[8:53]`.
- RLHF data design forces hard choices: single-turn versus multi-turn, helpfulness versus honesty versus harmlessness, and ranking versus scoring. Source: `[15:56]` to `[21:24]`.
- Zephyr shows the synthetic path is viable: UltraChat plus UltraFeedback plus DPO on Mistral beats ChatGPT on AlpacaEval. Source: `[26:28]` to `[28:42]`, `[43:03]` to `[43:43]`.
- GPT-4 is not a neutral judge. It has positional bias, prefers GPT-4-trained outputs, and correlates better with humans on creative tasks than on math or coding. Source: `[45:49]` to `[49:07]`.

## Key Concepts
- **InstructGPT recipe.** The talk keeps using the three-step alignment stack: supervised fine-tuning, reward modeling, and PPO-style RL. Source: `[2:29]` to `[3:10]`.
- **Instruction-demonstration data.** Helpful chatbots start from prompt/response pairs, not just raw text. The label is the whole example, not a single token. Source: `[3:41]` to `[4:34]`.
- **Synthetic data bootstrapping.** Self-Instruct starts from a small seed set and expands it with a model; UltraChat adds a human-in-the-loop loop; CAMEL pushes farther into role-played conversations. Source: `[5:50]` to `[8:53]`.
- **Human preference data.** RLHF flips the setup: humans write the prompt, models write candidate answers, and annotators rank the outputs. Source: `[15:56]` to `[18:20]`.
- **Task distribution.** The team moved toward factual prompts because ranking is easier when there is a clearer notion of correctness. Source: `[20:35]` to `[21:24]`.
- **Evaluation stack.** The talk separates instruction-following eval, reward-model eval, and red-teaming instead of pretending one benchmark covers all three. Source: `[31:21]` to `[37:25]`.
- **DPO.** Direct Preference Optimization removes the iterative RL loop and trains directly on chosen responses. Source: `[28:18]` to `[28:42]`.
- **GPT-4-as-judge.** GPT-4 is used as a proxy evaluator in several leaderboards, but its own biases distort the picture. Source: `[32:45]` to `[35:10]`, `[45:49]` to `[49:07]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The bottleneck is not "more data." The talk keeps showing that a few thousand high-quality examples can saturate faster than expected. Source: `[41:13]` to `[42:25]`.
- When the model is weak, annotation quality collapses too. The team found that internal raters disagreed heavily because the outputs were all bad. Source: `[18:44]` to `[19:48]`.
- SFT alone gets most of the gain. DPO without SFT fails; SFT on synthetic data already carries most of the weight. Source: `[44:01]` to `[45:12]`.
- Benchmark choice changes the story. Open LLM leaderboard metrics, MT Bench, and TruthfulQA did not all rank the same models the same way. Source: `[38:00]` to `[40:47]`.
- GPT-4 looks better on high-entropy creative tasks than on low-entropy reasoning tasks, which is the opposite of what people often assume. Source: `[48:17]` to `[49:07]`.
- A reward model has no good open-source leaderboard yet. That gap is part of the lecture, not a side note. Source: `[35:38]` to `[37:04]`.

## Referenced Papers
- **InstructGPT.** The three-step alignment recipe the lecture rebuilds. Source: `[1:43]` to `[3:10]`.
- **Self-Instruct.** The seed-to-synthetic bootstrapping pattern for instruction data. Source: `[5:50]` to `[6:40]`.
- **UltraChat.** Human-in-the-loop synthetic instruction generation. Source: `[6:50]` to `[7:50]`.
- **CAMEL.** Role-played synthetic conversations. Source: `[7:52]` to `[8:53]`.
- **UltraFeedback.** Multi-model response ranking used for preference training. Source: `[27:26]` to `[28:18]`.
- **DPO.** Direct Preference Optimization from Stanford. Source: `[28:18]` to `[28:42]`.
- **AlpacaEval.** Pairwise evaluation leaderboard used to compare chatbots. Source: `[33:12]` to `[33:36]`, `[43:03]` to `[43:43]`.
- **MT Bench.** Multi-turn chatbot benchmark from LMSYS. Source: `[34:00]` to `[35:10]`.
- **Chatbot Arena.** Crowdsourced Elo-style leaderboard from LMSYS. Source: `[33:36]` to `[34:00]`.
- **TruthfulQA.** The benchmark that separated the SFT variants most clearly. Source: `[38:00]` to `[40:47]`.
- **Open Assistant.** One of the human-preference and instruction-following baselines. Source: `[35:57]` to `[36:10]`, `[38:24]` to `[39:27]`.
- **Learning to Summarize.** The OpenAI reward-model paper used as a preference-data baseline. Source: `[36:01]` to `[36:13]`.
- **Anthropic HH.** The helpfulness/harmlessness dataset used for reward-model eval. Source: `[35:57]` to `[37:04]`.
