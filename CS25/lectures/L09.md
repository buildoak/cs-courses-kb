# L09 Transformers for Audio: Discrete Codes, WaveNet, and Wavelets

The lecture moves through three audio routes: raw-sample generation, discrete-code language modeling, and spectrogram-patch understanding. Source: `[12:51]` to `[43:35]`.

## Summary
- Audio is treated as a transformer problem by moving between waveform samples, spectrograms, and discrete codes. Source: `[6:17]` to `[12:51]` and `[24:42]` to `[30:27]`.
- Raw audio generation can be modeled as next-sample prediction, but the sequence lengths make it expensive and finicky. Source: `[12:51]` to `[21:03]`.
- VQ-VAE, wav2vec-style discretization, and Jukebox-style hierarchies make continuous audio look like language. Source: `[24:42]` to `[30:27]`.
- The representation-learning track shows that transformers can learn useful audio features even when no labels are available. Source: `[31:12]` to `[37:05]`.
- Spectrogram-patch transformers, plus a wavelet-style hierarchy inside embeddings, beat CNN baselines on audio understanding. Source: `[37:05]` to `[43:35]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Waveform.** The raw signal is just amplitude over time, sampled at 8k-16k Hz depending on the setting. Source: `[6:17]` to `[8:28]` and `[12:03]` to `[12:08]`.
- **Spectrogram.** Window the waveform, take Fourier transforms, and stack the slices to get a 2D time-frequency view. Source: `[8:28]` to `[10:51]`.
- **Sample-level language modeling.** Raw audio can be treated as a next-sample prediction problem, but the context length explodes. Source: `[12:51]` to `[15:08]`.
- **WaveNet.** Causal autoregressive audio synthesis is the baseline the talk keeps returning to. Source: `[14:54]` to `[21:03]`.
- **WaveRNN and SampleRNN.** The lecture uses these as the older state of the art for raw waveform generation. Source: `[15:08]` to `[18:20]`.
- **Discrete codes.** VQ-VAE, wav2vec, and related encoders turn continuous audio into tokens the transformer can model directly. Source: `[24:42]` to `[30:27]`.
- **Jukebox-style hierarchy.** Music generation gets easier when the code stream is hierarchical and low-rate enough to model autoregressively. Source: `[28:27]` to `[30:27]`.
- **Acoustic scene understanding.** The speech/audio language-modeling track is about recognizing what is in a chunk of sound, not just generating it. Source: `[22:45]` to `[24:31]`.
- **Spectrogram patches.** The vision-transformer analogy is literal here: chop the spectrogram into patches and feed them to a transformer. Source: `[37:28]` to `[38:27]`.
- **Wavelet decomposition.** The speaker adds a hierarchical decomposition inside transformer embeddings to recover structure without CNNs. Source: `[38:43]` to `[41:12]`.
- **Representation learning.** The model can be used to learn features, then a small head can classify the audio content. Source: `[35:14]` to `[37:05]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Audio only becomes transformer-friendly after you choose the right representation. The same signal is hard as raw samples and much easier as codes or patches. Source: `[12:51]` to `[30:27]`.
- The bottleneck in raw audio is not just quality. It is sequence length and the ear's sensitivity to tiny errors. Source: `[14:30]` to `[15:08]`.
- Discretization is the real bridge to language modeling. Once audio is tokenized, next-token prediction becomes next-code prediction. Source: `[24:42]` to `[25:55]`.
- The understanding track is the surprising one. The speaker gets strong results with unlabeled audio and a simple linear head. Source: `[35:14]` to `[37:05]`.
- Wavelet-style hierarchy inside embeddings is a way to smuggle signal-processing priors back into a transformer stack. Source: `[38:43]` to `[41:12]`.
- The lecture keeps pointing at the same meta-lesson: transformers are not just for text, but text is the easiest place to notice what they want to do. Source: `[47:23]` to `[47:47]`.
