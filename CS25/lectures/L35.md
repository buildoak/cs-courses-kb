# L35 Transformers United: RL as Product Design

The lecture argues that product behavior is trained, not added. Canvas, refusal tuning, and reward design are all part of the model.

## Summary
- Chat-based interfaces are too thin for the use cases the speaker cares about, so Canvas and Tasks are used to show model collaboration, code rendering, and schedulable work. Source: `[16:38]` to `[20:21]`.
- The core thesis is that RL is product plus research co-design: to change behavior you need to change data, reward, and evals together. Source: `[20:32]` to `[34:48]`.
- Refusal behavior is a taxonomy problem: benign overrefusal, creative-writing refusals, tool-call refusals, long-document refusals, and misdirected refusals each need different treatment. Source: `[23:30]` to `[31:42]`.
- Real product use cases require search, code tools, computer-use tools, long context, and multi-player or multi-agent interactions. Source: `[35:04]` to `[41:28]`.
- Reward hacks and model deception are the dark side; trustworthy verification and better affordances matter as much as the policy model itself. Source: `[43:28]` to `[45:32]`.
- Smaller distilled models trained on reasoning traces are increasingly the right fit for many agent tasks. Source: `[58:26]` to `[59:47]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Canvas.** The model is not just chatting; it is editing, writing code, and collaborating through a richer interface. Source: `[17:08]` to `[19:24]`.
- **Tasks.** The model can schedule work and continue narratives, which makes tool compositionality the product primitive. Source: `[19:47]` to `[20:21]`.
- **RLHF, DPO, RLAIF, and GRPO.** The lecture treats these as ways to steer behavior with preference data, sometimes synthetic. Source: `[21:46]` to `[31:46]`.
- **Refusal taxonomy.** The speaker breaks refusals into benign overrefusals, creative writing refusals, tool-call refusals, long-doc refusals, and misdirected refusals. Source: `[23:30]` to `[31:42]`.
- **Reward modeling.** The practical question is which data to collect, how to shape pairwise comparisons, and how to avoid spurious correlations. Source: `[27:27]` to `[30:20]`.
- **Reward hacking.** A policy model can optimize the evaluator instead of the task, especially when LLMs grade LLMs. Source: `[43:28]` to `[45:32]`.
- **Subjective evals.** Emotional intelligence, social intelligence, writing, and visual design are treated as the next benchmark frontier. Source: `[39:07]` to `[41:28]`.
- **Distillation from stronger models.** The talk repeatedly points to synthetic data and reasoning traces as the route to smaller, sharper models. Source: `[41:12]` to `[41:28]` and `[58:26]` to `[59:47]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The interface is part of training. Canvas is not a UI wrapper around the model; it is a behavioral constraint and a data source. Source: `[17:22]` to `[19:24]`.
- "Helpful" and "harmless" pull against each other. If you optimize one too hard, the model becomes either unsafe or unusable. Source: `[31:21]` to `[31:42]`.
- The best RL environment looks like the product itself. The lecture keeps returning to the idea that product design is reward design. Source: `[34:53]` to `[35:36]` and `[41:33]` to `[42:14]`.
- Subjective tasks are not a side quest. They become central once models are socially integrated in daily life. Source: `[39:07]` to `[40:52]`.
- Smaller models are not a downgrade if they carry distilled reasoning and the right task shaping. Source: `[58:26]` to `[59:47]`.
