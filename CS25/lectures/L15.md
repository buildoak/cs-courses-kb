# L15 Embodied AI: Offline Data, Language, and Control

This lecture is the robotics version of the scaling story: offline data, high-capacity models, and language as the API, all under a hard latency budget.

## Summary
- The lecture opens by arguing that robotics needs a foundation-model recipe: large offline data sets, high-capacity architectures, and language as the universal glue. Source: `[20:47]` to `[21:08]`.
- RT-1 turns that recipe into a concrete policy: RGB input, language instructions, tokenized patches, discretized actions, and a 3 Hz closed loop. Source: `[25:58]` to `[29:05]`.
- Generalization in robotics depends more on task diversity than on repeating the same task forever. More task types matter more than more episodes per task. Source: `[38:51]` to `[40:06]`.
- SayCan, Inner Monologue, and DIAL expand the same recipe upward: planning, closed-loop feedback, and vision-language relabeling. Source: `[44:25]` to `[64:04]`.
- The open bottlenecks are still brutal: low-level skill coverage, context length, and whether RL can augment imitation learning. Source: `[1:11:30]` to `[1:15:56]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Offline robot learning.** The lecture wants data generation and data consumption decoupled, not tied to online rollouts only. Source: `[10:04]` to `[10:15]`, `[20:47]` to `[21:08]`.
- **Teleoperation data.** RT-1 starts from 100,000 human demonstrations collected over a year and a half. Source: `[23:56]` to `[24:38]`, `[33:23]` to `[33:52]`.
- **BC-Z.** The baseline that made the case for multi-task imitation learning and exposed sensitivity to data distribution. Source: `[24:29]` to `[25:02]`, `[31:28]` to `[32:08]`.
- **RT-1.** Robotics Transformer 1: a decoder-only transformer with EfficientNet, TokenLearner, and discretized action tokens. Source: `[25:58]` to `[29:05]`.
- **TokenLearner.** A patch selector that reduces 81 image patches to 8 per timestep so inference stays tractable. Source: `[27:14]` to `[27:52]`.
- **Action democratization.** Continuous control is binned into 256 actions so the policy can operate as a token model. Source: `[28:22]` to `[29:05]`.
- **Gato comparison.** RT-1 is re-evaluated against a re-trained Gato baseline and comes out more robust. Source: `[31:28]` to `[32:49]`.
- **SayCan.** The planner combines language-model scores with robot affordance scores to choose actions the robot can actually execute. Source: `[44:25]` to `[49:20]`.
- **Inner Monologue.** Closed-loop feedback adds passive scene description, active questioning, and success detection so the robot can recover. Source: `[49:42]` to `[52:57]`.
- **DIAL.** Vision-language relabeling expands narrow template instructions into richer natural-language supervision. Source: `[54:17]` to `[64:04]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- In this setting, more tasks beat more demos per task. Diversity is the scaling axis that matters most. Source: `[38:51]` to `[40:06]`.
- Discretizing actions is not just compression. It is what makes the policy fast enough and interoperable enough to run on real robots. Source: `[28:22]` to `[29:05]`.
- Autoregressive action history can hurt, because the policy may overfit to the teleoperator identity instead of the task. Source: `[43:24]` to `[43:52]`.
- VLM-generated labels do not need to be perfectly clean to help. Some label noise is acceptable if the signal is directionally correct. Source: `[59:10]` to `[60:04]`, `[1:05:03]` to `[1:05:15]`.
- The bottleneck in SayCan is not just planning quality. It is the coverage of the low-level skill buffet the planner can choose from. Source: `[1:11:30]` to `[1:12:19]`.
- Six images of history is already close to the context limit. Long-horizon robotics still needs better memory or retrieval. Source: `[1:15:00]` to `[1:15:56]`.
