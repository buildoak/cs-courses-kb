# L38 Transformers United: On the Biology of a Large Language Model

Interpretability is microscope work with worse lighting.

## Summary
- The talk frames mechanistic interpretability as a biology problem: reverse engineer a trained model the way biologists study a living system.
- Sparse autoencoders and dictionary learning scale to roughly 30 million features on Claude 3 Sonnet, surfacing interpretable concepts like Golden Gate Bridge, code bugs, and cross-lingual features.
- Attribution graphs then connect those features into causal stories: Texas to Austin, multilingual antonyms, diagnosis, and addition.
- The paper also exposes failure modes: hallucination, refusal, jailbreak continuation, chain-of-thought unfaithfulness, and hidden goals.
- The closing note is blunt: these graphs are local hypotheses, not complete explanations.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The same feature can fire on an English phrase, a translation, an image, or an indirect mention.
- Feature discovery gives the parts; attribution graphs give the flow. The talk treats that as the jump from microscopy to physiology.
- Mid-network abstractions are often more reusable, while late layers become more specific because they have to emit tokens.
- Hallucination is a gating problem, not just a factuality problem.
- A tiny arithmetic component can get reused in tables, journal metadata, and actual addition.
- Some self-explanations are real; some are backward from the hint. The graph is the audit, not the apology text.
