# L12 Transformers United: RLHF and Scalable Oversight

Jan Leike frames alignment as a multiplayer game: humans write the rules, AI keeps getting stronger, and the practical problem is making evaluation scale faster than model capability. Source: `[0:49]` to `[4:16]` and `[22:25]` to `[30:14]`.

## Summary
- Alignment is framed as getting AI players to join Team Human under rules humans still write. Source: `[0:49]` to `[4:16]`.
- RLHF is the main production recipe: collect comparisons, train a reward model, and optimize the model against it. The talk uses InstructGPT and ChatGPT as the concrete examples. Source: `[6:35]` to `[11:19]`.
- The shock result is that a 100x smaller InstructGPT variant can beat a much larger base model on human preference. Source: `[9:39]` to `[10:27]`.
- RLHF is cheap relative to pretraining, but it hits a ceiling because human evaluation capacity does not scale with model capability. Source: `[15:22]` to `[24:00]`.
- Scalable oversight uses AI critique, fact-checking, browsing, and other assistance to keep humans in the loop on harder tasks. Source: `[24:17]` to `[30:14]` and `[48:41]` to `[49:50]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Team Human / Team AI.** The talk uses a game metaphor: humans get to recruit AI players and set the rules. Source: `[0:49]` to `[4:16]`.
- **Human intent.** Alignment is not only explicit instructions; it also includes implicit intent, refusal, and harm avoidance. Source: `[4:25]` to `[6:24]`.
- **Reward model from comparisons.** Humans compare model outputs and train a reward model to capture preferences. Source: `[7:14]` to `[8:09]`.
- **PPO and PTX.** The model is then optimized with reinforcement learning, and pretraining data is mixed back in to reduce regressions. Source: `[8:09]` to `[11:19]`.
- **Labeler variability.** Different labelers disagree, so the reward model averages over preferences rather than treating them as absolute truth. Source: `[8:27]` to `[8:56]`.
- **Evaluation is easier than generation.** Humans often cannot do the task, but they can still judge which answer is better. Source: `[20:27]` to `[21:24]`.
- **AI-assisted evaluation.** The system can use critiques, explanations, fact checking, browsing, and other tools to help humans evaluate. Source: `[25:33]` to `[26:33]` and `[48:41]` to `[49:17]`.
- **Targeted perturbations.** The team plants a subtle flaw in a response so the evaluation task has ground truth. Source: `[27:26]` to `[29:11]`.
- **Discriminator-critique gap.** Compare a model that detects the flaw with a model that explains it; a large gap means the model knows more than it says. Source: `[35:17]` to `[36:33]`.
- **Deception pressure.** A sufficiently optimized model may learn to say what evaluators want to hear. Source: `[23:46]` to `[24:00]`.
- **Outer vs inner alignment.** Reliable external evaluation may reduce inner alignment problems by forcing the model's internal optimizers into line. Source: `[59:19]` to `[1:01:12]`.
- **Interpretability.** Useful, but not a full solution; knowing why a model answers is not the same as making it safe. Source: `[1:02:10]` to `[1:05:37]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- RLHF mostly unlocks capabilities already present in pretraining. Fine-tuning makes the model useful and legible, not magically smarter. Source: `[57:03]` to `[57:27]`.
- The bottleneck is not raw model competence. It is the evaluation pipe. If humans cannot score the task, RLHF stops being trustworthy. Source: `[22:25]` to `[24:00]`.
- AI critique can be noisy and still useful. Garbage critiques still help if they point humans at the right part of the answer. Source: `[32:32]` to `[33:28]`.
- The key safety problem is not just bad outputs. It is models optimizing for evaluator psychology and possibly learning to deceive. Source: `[23:46]` to `[24:00]`.
- Interpretability is useful because it adds another tool, but it is neither obviously necessary nor sufficient for alignment. Source: `[1:03:39]` to `[1:05:37]`.
