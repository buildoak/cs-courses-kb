# L10 Transformers United: GLOM and Islands of Agreement

Hinton starts from the old capsules problem: how do you represent part-whole structure when neurons cannot be allocated on demand? GLOM's answer is pre-allocated columns, dynamic embeddings, and agreement-driven hierarchy. Source: `[0:28]` to `[5:28]` and `[50:13]` to `[52:32]`.

## Summary
- Hinton frames GLOM as a way to represent part-whole hierarchies without violating how neurons work, after capsules showed the idea was real but awkward. Source: `[0:28]` to `[5:28]`.
- SimCLR-style contrastive learning gives a workable unsupervised backbone, but naive contrastive loss mostly learns scene-level agreement unless hierarchy and attention separate object-level structure. Source: `[20:46]` to `[26:19]`.
- GLOM turns each patch into a column with multiple levels; bottom-up and top-down transforms plus neighbor attention are used to form islands of agreement. Source: `[29:00]` to `[40:28]`.
- Ambiguity is handled by carrying a multimodal distribution inside the embedding itself, then sharpening it through consensus and agreement across nearby columns. Source: `[41:23]` to `[49:46]`.
- The closing neural-fields example makes the same logic spatial: one face vector predicts different lower-level patches once location is fed in. Source: `[50:13]` to `[52:32]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Part-whole hierarchy.** The target is not just object recognition; it is representing nested wholes inside parts. Source: `[0:28]` to `[0:39]`.
- **Capsules.** Hinton revisits the idea of pre-allocating neural groups and routing them into a parse tree. Source: `[2:54]` to `[3:26]`.
- **Embeddings as pointers.** In GLOM, the embedding vector is the dynamic thing that acts like a pointer, not the weights. Source: `[5:22]` to `[5:31]`.
- **Columns and levels.** Each image patch gets a column, and each column carries multiple levels of representation. Source: `[4:16]` to `[4:47]` and `[31:18]` to `[31:45]`.
- **Islands of agreement.** The hierarchy is built by making nearby embeddings converge on the same interpretation. Source: `[4:50]` to `[5:18]` and `[36:25]` to `[36:41]`.
- **Contrastive learning.** Two crops from the same image should match; crops from different images should not. Source: `[21:02]` to `[24:16]`.
- **Collapse and negative examples.** Without negatives, the representation collapses to the same vector for everything. Source: `[23:03]` to `[24:14]`.
- **BERT analogy.** The talk maps the GLOM column to BERT-style stacked representations, but with growing islands of agreement instead of just token features. Source: `[26:31]` to `[28:12]`.
- **Transformational random field.** Ambiguity is handled by messages that are gated by coordinate transforms, not just raw pairwise similarity. Source: `[37:18]` to `[38:54]`.
- **Hough-transform style support.** Parts predict wholes, and nearby columns agree on the same whole from different evidence. Source: `[39:06]` to `[40:28]`.
- **Consensus embedding.** The next state is a weighted consensus of current, top-down, bottom-up, and neighbor inputs. Source: `[45:45]` to `[47:10]`.
- **Neural fields.** The same top-down network can output different lower-level vectors if it is also given the location it is predicting for. Source: `[50:13]` to `[52:12]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The move from capsules to GLOM is not about adding more memory. It is about moving the dynamic part into activations and letting agreement do the routing. Source: `[2:54]` to `[5:31]`.
- Plain contrastive learning is too coarse. It can line up crops from the same scene while still erasing object-level distinctions. Source: `[25:15]` to `[26:19]`.
- The talk treats attention as a support mechanism, not just a similarity operator. Each message asks whether nearby structure supports the hypothesis you are already holding. Source: `[38:01]` to `[40:20]`.
- Replicating embeddings at the object level is useful, not wasteful, because it lets the model hedge before settling on one parse. Source: `[47:27]` to `[48:19]`.
- Sparsity gets easier at higher levels because big islands of agreement reduce how much of the system needs to be active. Source: `[49:00]` to `[49:46]`.

## Connections
- To capsules: GLOM keeps the dynamic routing intuition but removes the need for per-object neuron allocation. Source: `[2:54]` to `[3:26]`.
- To SimCLR: the unsupervised backbone is the same-crop / different-crop agreement trick. Source: `[21:54]` to `[24:16]`.
- To BERT: the column structure is close, but the target is larger and more spatially coherent representations. Source: `[26:31]` to `[28:12]`.
- To transformers: the inter-column interaction is just attention with coordinate transforms layered on top. Source: `[34:26]` to `[35:40]`.
- To active vision and neural fields: the talk assumes a real system looks where it wants to look, then reuses the same hardware at new fixations. Source: `[29:00]` to `[30:20]` and `[50:13]` to `[52:32]`.
