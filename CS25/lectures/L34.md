# L34 Transformers United: Prompting, Memory, and State Space Models

The lecture treats pretraining as the foundation and then spends most of its time on the harder problem: how to shape behavior after the model is already trained.

## Summary
- The talk frames pretraining as the base layer, then moves to prompting and post-training as the practical tools for behavior shaping. Source: `[25:01]` to `[32:24]`.
- Chain-of-thought, tree-of-thought, program-of-thought, Socratic questioning, and computational graphs are all presented as ways to decompose hard tasks at inference time. Source: `[25:38]` to `[29:12]`.
- RLHF, DPO, and RLAIF turn human or model preferences into reward data, but the speaker keeps warning that the data distribution matters more than the algorithm label. Source: `[29:23]` to `[31:42]`.
- The lecture says scaling is hitting diminishing returns, so Mamba, state space machines, and broader architectural changes deserve serious attention. Source: `[52:18]` to `[54:07]`.
- Continual learning remains the hard boundary: ROME, MEMIT, lifelong mixture-of-experts, CLOB, and progressive prompts are useful, but only some of them really update the model rather than wrap it. Source: `[56:57]` to `[1:00:09]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Pretraining vs post-training.** Pretraining gives the model its base capability; post-training steers its behavior toward useful responses and safer refusal patterns. Source: `[25:03]` to `[32:24]`.
- **Chain-of-thought.** Step-by-step prompting exposes intermediate reasoning and often converts wrong one-shot answers into correct ones. Source: `[25:38]` to `[26:47]`.
- **Tree/program/Socratic decomposition.** The lecture treats these as related ways to break a task into multiple reasoning branches or explicit code steps. Source: `[26:51]` to `[29:12]`.
- **RLHF and DPO.** Human preference pairs train reward models; DPO is presented as a cleaner alternative to PPO-based RLHF. Source: `[29:23]` to `[30:16]`.
- **RLAIF.** Model-generated preference data can reduce cost and still shape refusals and helpfulness. Source: `[30:22]` to `[31:04]`.
- **Catastrophic forgetting.** Overdoing post-training can erase earlier capabilities. Source: `[52:50]` to `[53:19]`.
- **State space models.** Mamba and related state-space architectures are named as possible non-transformer routes past current scaling limits. Source: `[53:35]` to `[54:07]`.
- **Model editing.** ROME and MEMIT are presented as ways to target specific factual updates without full retraining. Source: `[56:57]` to `[59:08]`.
- **Prompt-memory methods.** CLOB and progressive prompts keep knowledge in compressed prompt form instead of changing weights. Source: `[59:30]` to `[1:00:09]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture is not saying prompting is enough. It is saying prompting is an inference-time workaround, and the ceiling is still set by the base model. Source: `[25:38]` to `[29:12]` and `[52:50]` to `[53:05]`.
- The paper label matters less than the data distribution. The speaker keeps returning to how carefully the preference pairs are constructed. Source: `[29:31]` to `[30:20]`.
- ROME-style editing is useful for facts, but not for skill transfer. The lecture draws a hard line between factual updates and capability updates. Source: `[57:11]` to `[58:20]`.
- Prompt-only memory hacks are not the same thing as learning. They help with persistence, but they do not update the brain. Source: `[59:30]` to `[1:00:09]`.
- The lecture keeps looking for simpler post-training controls and eventually new architectures because the current stack saturates. Source: `[52:18]` to `[54:07]`.
