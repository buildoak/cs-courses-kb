# L05 Switch Transformer: Sparse Experts as a Scaling Axis

This lecture argues that conditional sparsity is a better scaling axis than just making dense transformers wider.

## Summary
- The talk starts from scaling laws: bigger models are more sample-efficient, and sparsity adds another way to scale beyond dense width alone. Source: `[0:24]` to `[3:05]`.
- Mixture-of-experts routing sends each token to a subset of experts; Switch Transformer simplifies that to top-1 routing and adds training fixes for stability. Source: `[3:13]` to `[10:21]`, `[25:33]` to `[28:41]`.
- Lower capacity factors can beat higher-capacity routing on wall-clock quality because they reduce communication, compute, and memory overhead. Source: `[26:21]` to `[28:41]`.
- More experts produce consistent gains at fixed FLOPs, with roughly 7x training-speed improvements over dense T5 baselines in the reported setup. Source: `[29:48]` to `[30:36]`, `[32:01]` to `[32:46]`.
- Sparse models behave differently by task: they tend to underperform dense models on reasoning-heavy SuperGLUE but do very well on knowledge-heavy TriviaQA and multilingual settings. Source: `[43:23]` to `[47:36]`.
- Distillation can recover part of the sparse-model gain in a dense model, but the key advantage comes from structural sparsity, not plain weight pruning. Source: `[47:47]` to `[54:23]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Scaling laws.** Larger models usually improve predictably with size and compute. Source: `[0:53]` to `[2:06]`.
- **Sparse experts.** Different inputs use different parameter sets, even when the compute budget per token stays similar. Source: `[2:45]` to `[4:18]`.
- **Router.** A gating network chooses which expert processes each token. Source: `[3:45]` to `[4:18]`.
- **Switch layer.** Top-1 routing to a single expert, simpler than top-2 mixture-of-experts routing. Source: `[5:03]` to `[7:18]`.
- **Selective precision.** Cast the router path carefully and use lower precision elsewhere to keep sparse models stable. Source: `[9:00]` to `[10:21]`.
- **Load balancing.** Auxiliary loss prevents all tokens collapsing onto a few experts. Source: `[8:25]` to `[8:52]`, `[19:36]` to `[20:17]`.
- **Capacity factor.** Limits how many tokens each expert can process; lower values reduce overhead. Source: `[26:21]` to `[28:41]`.
- **Expert parallelism.** Treat sparsity as a hardware-friendly partitioning scheme, distinct from model parallelism and data parallelism. Source: `[31:31]` to `[41:16]`.
- **Distillation.** Train a dense student to match sparse teacher outputs. Source: `[47:43]` to `[50:55]`.
- **Multilingual routing.** Experts can specialize across languages and speed up training. Source: `[47:07]` to `[47:36]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Sparsity here is not "make weights disappear." It is conditional block structure that gives the model more parameters without paying full FLOPs for all of them. Source: `[52:02]` to `[53:36]`.
- More parameters do not help equally across tasks. The sparse models gain more on knowledge-heavy tasks than on reasoning-heavy ones. Source: `[44:18]` to `[46:02]`.
- Top-1 routing is not just a simplification. At low capacity factors, it is the Pareto better choice because it cuts communication overhead hard enough to win on wall-clock time. Source: `[27:09]` to `[28:41]`.
- Distillation preserves only part of the gain because the sparse teacher learned routing structure, not just a weight pattern. Source: `[48:02]` to `[50:55]`.
- The research question is really adaptive computation. Sparsity is the first practical move toward variable computation on hardware that likes SPMD batching. Source: `[21:44]` to `[25:17]`.

## Connections
- To L01 and L02, the same transformer backbone is being scaled, but now the axis is conditional computation instead of sequence length or prompt size.
- To distributed training, the talk leans on mesh-style SPMD partitioning and explicit core-aware tensor layouts. Source: `[40:09]` to `[41:16]`.
- To later sparse work in vision, the same idea is reused for image classification with very low capacity factors. Source: `[1:00:35]` to `[1:01:49]`.
- To inference systems, sparse models make sense when throughput matters more than per-device compactness. Source: `[1:04:41]` to `[1:05:17]`.
