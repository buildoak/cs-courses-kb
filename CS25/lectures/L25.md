# L25 Retrieval Augmented Language Models

RAG is treated here as a memory and systems problem: keep truth in an index, then make the generator learn how to query it.

## Summary
- The lecture opens by framing hallucination, stale knowledge, and attribution as the reasons to move facts out of parameters and into external memory. Source: `[1:26]` to `[8:50]`.
- It starts with classic sparse retrieval, then moves through TF-IDF, BM25, dense retrieval, BERT-style embeddings, and FAISS-backed vector search. Source: `[12:14]` to `[21:41]`.
- Frozen RAG is the baseline, but the lecture keeps pushing toward better coupling between retriever and generator through DPR, RETRO, REPLUG, chunk cross-attention, and in-context retrieval. Source: `[35:17]` to `[50:17]`.
- The lecture argues that long context alone is not the answer; long-context systems often drift toward sparse retrieval under the hood anyway. Source: `[54:09]` to `[55:32]`.
- The ending is blunt: RAG 2.0 should be a systems stack, not a loose pairing of a model and a retriever, and the right tradeoff is cost, quality, and legal safety. Source: `[1:08:48]` to `[1:10:29]`, `[1:12:15]` to `[1:18:21]`.

## Key Concepts
- **External memory.** RAG is presented as a way to keep knowledge outside the weights and feed it into the model at inference time. Source: `[6:12]` to `[8:50]`.
- **Frozen RAG.** The default baseline is a retriever plus a generator, with the generator left mostly unchanged. Source: `[1:03:08]` to `[1:03:28]`.
- **TF-IDF and BM25.** Sparse retrieval is still the starting point because it is simple, cheap, and effective. Source: `[12:14]` to `[14:39]`.
- **Dense retrieval.** BERT-style sentence embeddings let the retriever find semantically related passages instead of just lexical matches. Source: `[15:37]` to `[16:57]`.
- **DPR.** Dense Passage Retriever shows that retrieval can be trained as a latent variable in the system. Source: `[15:59]` to `[16:40]`.
- **FAISS and vector databases.** Dense retrieval is operationalized with approximate nearest neighbor search and vector storage. Source: `[17:20]` to `[19:56]`.
- **Hybrid search.** Reciprocal rank fusion and reranking combine sparse and dense signals instead of pretending one retrieval method is enough. Source: `[1:03:34]` to `[1:03:59]`.
- **RETRO.** Retrieval can be built into the generator itself with chunk cross-attention instead of treating retrieval as a pre-step. Source: `[35:17]` to `[37:18]`, `[46:56]` to `[50:17]`.
- **REPLUG.** Retrieval quality can be trained with a language-model signal rather than only retriever-side losses. Source: `[41:24]` to `[42:13]`, `[1:00:21]` to `[1:00:40]`.
- **FLARE.** Active retrieval means the model decides when to spend compute on search. Source: `[56:14]` to `[56:31]`.
- **SILO.** Safe train-time / broad test-time retrieval is a way to separate model training data from the larger index. Source: `[57:43]` to `[58:45]`.
- **Tool augmentation.** Retrieval is only one tool; the lecture explicitly broadens the frame to tool-using language models. Source: `[1:01:27]` to `[1:01:40]`.
- **Hallucination definition.** Hallucination is not just a mistake; it is a mismatch against a ground truth source. Source: `[1:15:48]` to `[1:18:21]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture treats hallucination as a retrieval design failure first and a model failure second. Source: `[7:38]` to `[8:50]`, `[1:15:48]` to `[1:18:21]`.
- BM25 is not a dead baseline. It stays relevant because it is cheap, broad, and often the right first-stage net. Source: `[1:06:06]` to `[1:06:10]`, `[1:12:18]` to `[1:12:31]`.
- Long-context systems and RAG converge. When long-context attention gets sparse enough, it starts behaving like retrieval anyway. Source: `[54:09]` to `[55:32]`.
- Frozen RAG is brittle because ordering matters more than you want and the model overweights the first and last retrieved chunks. Source: `[58:49]` to `[59:44]`.
- The real unit is not a model but a system: retriever, reranker, generator, index, and data governance all move together. Source: `[1:08:48]` to `[1:10:29]`, `[1:12:15]` to `[1:13:49]`.
- End-to-end training is the long-term target, but the lecture spends most of its time on useful intermediate hybrids. Source: `[1:00:05]` to `[1:03:03]`, `[1:13:22]` to `[1:13:40]`.
- The strongest legal-risk argument in the lecture is that a safe training corpus plus a broader test-time index can be a practical compliance strategy. Source: `[57:52]` to `[58:45]`.

## Referenced Papers
- **Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks (RAG).** The baseline frozen retrieval-augmented setup. Source: `[7:38]` to `[8:50]`, `[30:14]` to `[30:54]`.
- **Dense Passage Retrieval (DPR).** Dense retrieval trained on supervised relevance data. Source: `[15:59]` to `[16:40]`, `[51:52]` to `[52:06]`.
- **Retrieval-Augmented Language Model Pre-Training (RETRO).** Retrieval built into the generator with chunk cross-attention. Source: `[35:17]` to `[37:18]`, `[46:56]` to `[50:17]`.
- **REPLUG.** The retrieval-loss variant that uses model signal to train the retriever. Source: `[41:24]` to `[42:13]`, `[1:00:21]` to `[1:00:40]`.
- **FLARE.** Active retrieval augmentation where the model decides when to search. Source: `[56:22]` to `[56:31]`.
- **SILO.** Safe training data plus broader test-time retrieval. Source: `[57:43]` to `[58:45]`.
- **InstructRetro.** Instruction-tuning the full retrieval-augmented stack. Source: `[1:02:29]` to `[1:03:03]`.
- **Hypothetical Document Embeddings (HyDE).** Hallucinate answers, then retrieve against the hallucinated text. Source: `[1:03:59]` to `[1:04:24]`.
