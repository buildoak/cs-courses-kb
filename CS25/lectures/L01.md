# L01 Transformers United: Attention Beats Memory

This lecture starts with a simple complaint about older sequence models: they keep context too slowly, and they miss the relevant token when the answer sits far away.

## Summary
- CS25 opens as a cross-domain transformer seminar, not a pure NLP course. The framing is NLP, computer vision, reinforcement learning, and generation. Source: `[0:05]` to `[2:52]`.
- The talk traces the path from RNNs and LSTMs to attention, then to self-attention as the transformer core. Source: `[3:02]` to `[8:05]`.
- A working transformer needs more than self-attention: positional embeddings, nonlinear feed-forward layers, masking, layer norm, and residual connections. Source: `[12:10]` to `[16:09]`.
- The main wins are short path length and GPU-friendly parallelism. The main tax is quadratic attention cost. Source: `[16:14]` to `[17:47]`.
- GPT and BERT show the two dominant branches of the architecture: decoder-only autoregressive generation and encoder-only masked language modeling. Source: `[18:10]` to `[22:09]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Attention as importance weighting.** The lecture starts with soft and hard attention over image regions, then extends that intuition to sequence attention. Source: `[6:18]` to `[8:05]`.
- **Self-attention as retrieval.** Query, key, and value form a search problem: find the keys most similar to the query, then return their values. Source: `[8:25]` to `[9:35]`.
- **Multi-head attention.** Multiple heads let the model learn different relations in parallel, such as syntax or part-of-speech structure. Source: `[9:40]` to `[10:21]`.
- **Positional embeddings.** Self-attention loses order, so the model adds position information back in. Source: `[12:10]` to `[12:30]`.
- **Nonlinearity.** Attention alone is linear; the feed-forward block gives the transformer a richer mapping. Source: `[12:30]` to `[12:54]`.
- **Masking.** Decoder masking prevents future-token leakage during autoregressive generation. Source: `[12:56]` to `[13:26]`.
- **Encoder-decoder architecture.** The encoder reads the full input, the decoder emits tokens one by one, and the decoder also attends to the encoder output. Source: `[13:29]` to `[16:09]`.
- **Parallelization.** Transformers train faster than same-size LSTMs because sequence computation parallelizes cleanly on GPUs. Source: `[16:14]` to `[17:22]`.
- **Quadratic attention.** Full self-attention scales as n^2, which is why long sequences are still expensive. Source: `[17:26]` to `[17:47]`.
- **GPT vs BERT.** GPT is decoder-only and next-token driven; BERT is encoder-only and needs masked language modeling plus next sentence prediction. Source: `[18:18]` to `[22:01]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Self-attention is easiest to understand as retrieval, not as a vague weighted average. That framing makes q/k/v feel inevitable. Source: `[8:40]` to `[9:35]`.
- The transformer win is not self-attention alone. It is attention plus positions plus MLP plus masking plus residual plumbing. Source: `[12:10]` to `[16:09]`.
- Masking is a causal training rule, not a cosmetic detail. It is what makes the decoder legal. Source: `[12:56]` to `[13:26]`.
- The real breakthrough is path length. Any token can reach any other token in one hop, which changes long-range dependency handling. Source: `[16:29]` to `[16:55]`.
- BERT and GPT are opposite compromises on the same backbone. One optimizes full-context representation, the other optimizes causal generation. Source: `[18:25]` to `[22:01]`.
- Quadratic attention is the hidden bill. The rest of the efficiency literature is mostly trying to pay it down. Source: `[17:26]` to `[17:47]`.
