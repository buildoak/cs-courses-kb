# L07 Transformers United: Self-Attention Between Datapoints

This lecture starts as a transformer recap, then turns into a dataset-as-context argument for non-parametric transformers. Source: `[0:47]` to `[19:10]`.

## Summary
- The first half replays the core transformer ingredients: self-attention, multi-head attention, and fast autoregressive decoding with causal masking. Source: `[0:47]` to `[7:12]`.
- The NPT pitch is blunt: supervised learning usually predicts per example, but non-parametric transformers take the whole dataset as input and learn from interactions between datapoints. Source: `[13:28]` to `[19:10]`.
- The architecture adds a masking matrix, dataset-wide attention, and attention over attributes so the model can route information at both the row and feature level. Source: `[24:35]` to `[33:32]`.
- On tabular and image data, NPTs compete with strong baselines; on the protein dataset, removing attention between datapoints drops performance hard. Source: `[36:22]` to `[38:35]` and `[50:40]` to `[53:04]`.
- The semi-synthetic lookup experiments show that the model can learn an explicit relational mechanism, not just interpolate feature vectors. Source: `[54:16]` to `[58:12]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Self-attention as intra-sequence relation learning.** The opening transformer recap frames self-attention as relating tokens inside one sequence rather than across source and target sequences. Source: `[1:15]` to `[2:19]`.
- **Multi-head attention as pattern parallelism.** Different heads can specialize in different relationships, such as adjective-to-noun structure. Source: `[2:25]` to `[3:20]`.
- **Fast autoregressive decoding.** Feeding the gold target sequence during training makes the next-token objective parallelizable. Source: `[3:24]` to `[5:15]`.
- **Causal masking.** The model must be prevented from looking ahead, or it just cheats by reading the answer token. Source: `[5:22]` to `[6:55]`.
- **NPT as non-parametric prediction.** The second half defines non-parametric models as prediction in explicit dependence on the training data. Source: `[17:18]` to `[19:10]`.
- **Dataset-as-input representation.** NPTs take a full data matrix `X` plus a masking matrix `M`, not a single example at a time. Source: `[24:35]` to `[25:48]`.
- **Attention between datapoints.** The model uses self-attention across rows so training and test points can influence each other. Source: `[27:26]` to `[28:02]`.
- **Attention between attributes.** The architecture also runs self-attention within each row to build better per-example representations. Source: `[29:49]` to `[30:25]`.
- **Permutation equivariance.** Row order should not matter, because the dataset is a set, not a sequence. Source: `[30:43]` to `[31:01]`.
- **Masking as supervision and regularization.** The masking objective tells NPTs what to predict and makes the task harder in a useful way. Source: `[31:01]` to `[33:32]`.
- **Relational lookup.** The semi-synthetic protein setup forces the model to recover values from paired rows, which exposes the non-parametric mechanism directly. Source: `[54:16]` to `[58:12]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- NPTs are not just parametric models with a fancier head. They move the training set into the forward pass, which turns prediction into a relational computation. Source: `[16:12]` to `[19:10]`.
- The masking objective is doing two jobs. It hides targets and also gives the model extra supervised signal from other visible targets. Source: `[31:01]` to `[33:32]` and `[45:52]` to `[48:27]`.
- The architecture behaves like k-NN 2.0, but the distance function is learned instead of hand-coded. Source: `[18:04]` to `[19:10]`.
- The sharpest evidence comes from ablation: if you remove attention between datapoints, protein performance collapses from best to worst. Source: `[50:40]` to `[53:04]`.
- The semi-synthetic intervention test is stronger than a leaderboard result. The model has to follow the relation when the target values are swapped, and it mostly does. Source: `[57:54]` to `[58:40]`.
- The scale question is not whether the idea works at all. The scale question is how to batch and sparsify it without losing the dataset-level interaction. Source: `[34:48]` to `[35:22]` and `[61:11]` to `[61:12]`.

## Connections
- To L01: the masking trick is transformer DNA, but now the mask selects hidden targets in a dataset rather than future tokens. Source: `[31:01]` to `[33:32]`.
- To classical non-parametrics: Gaussian processes, k-NN, and kernel methods are the conceptual ancestors the talk keeps circling. Source: `[18:15]` to `[18:54]`.
- To graph learning: the speaker explicitly compares NPT-style interactions with neural relational inference and message passing over fully connected graphs. Source: `[1:03:04]` to `[1:03:32]`.
- To meta-learning: the model is trained on a whole dataset and then predicts within that dataset, so the boundary between training and inference gets blurry. Source: `[21:43]` to `[22:24]` and `[59:24]` to `[61:12]`.
- To tabular ML: the baselines are exactly the usual suspects, because tabular data is where deep learning usually needs the most help. Source: `[36:22]` to `[38:35]`.
