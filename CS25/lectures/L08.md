# L08 Transformer Circuits: Induction Heads Explain In-Context Learning

This lecture starts with mechanistic interpretability, then narrows to one question: why does GPT-3 suddenly behave like a few-shot learner? Source: `[0:45]` to `[9:10]`.

## Summary
- Mechanistic interpretability is framed as reverse-engineering the algorithms hidden inside weights, with safety as the practical reason to care. Source: `[0:45]` to `[3:27]`.
- The mystery is in-context learning: GPT-3 looks like a model that can adapt from the prompt without gradient updates. Source: `[5:00]` to `[9:10]`.
- A one-layer attention-only transformer can be rewritten in a way that exposes attention heads as information movers, and most heads end up doing copying. Source: `[15:17]` to `[36:30]`.
- Two-layer attention-only models add a more interesting compositional story, where virtual heads and layered attention make induction possible. Source: `[37:55]` to `[42:59]`.
- Induction heads line up with the in-context-learning phase change and can even implement soft translation-style lookup. Source: `[43:45]` to `[55:44]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Mechanistic interpretability.** The talk defines the field as turning neural weights back into human-readable algorithms. Source: `[0:45]` to `[3:27]`.
- **In-context learning as the mystery.** GPT-3 is remarkable not just because it scales, but because it changes behavior from context alone. Source: `[5:00]` to `[9:10]`.
- **Phase change.** The talk treats the abrupt jump in few-shot behavior as a structural event inside the model, not just a smooth metric improvement. Source: `[14:27]` to `[15:17]` and `[48:33]` to `[50:16]`.
- **Attention-only toy models.** The speaker strips away MLPs, LayerNorm, and biases to understand the smallest useful attention stack. Source: `[15:17]` to `[15:39]`.
- **OV circuit.** The value and output matrices describe what information a head reads and writes. Source: `[17:45]` to `[18:17]`.
- **QK circuit.** The query-key path decides which tokens attend to which other tokens. Source: `[21:28]` to `[22:41]`.
- **Residual stream.** The model can be written as paths through the residual stream plus attention-head terms, which makes the algebra legible. Source: `[18:17]` to `[19:42]`.
- **Copying heads.** In the one-layer model, most heads behave like skip-trigram copy machines. Source: `[24:36]` to `[36:30]`.
- **Induction heads.** These heads look for a previous copy, shift forward one token, and predict the continuation. Source: `[45:39]` to `[47:27]`.
- **Soft induction.** The same pattern can extend to translation and arbitrary function lookup, not just literal token copying. Source: `[50:34]` to `[55:44]`.
- **Ablation as evidence.** The phase change lines up with induction heads appearing and with ablations hurting in-context scores. Source: `[48:33]` to `[50:16]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The useful split is not "attention versus no attention." It is "what moves information" versus "which positions get connected." OV and QK cleanly separate those roles. Source: `[17:45]` to `[18:17]` and `[21:28]` to `[22:41]`.
- A fixed attention pattern makes the model linear. That is the part that makes the circuit analysis work at all. Source: `[19:34]` to `[20:02]`.
- One-layer models mostly copy. That looks trivial until you realize copying is already a strong language prior and a useful compression strategy. Source: `[24:36]` to `[36:30]`.
- Induction heads are a better explanation of in-context learning than vague "meta-learning" language. They make the prompt feel like a lookup table. Source: `[45:51]` to `[50:16]`.
- The model's strange ability to translate and apply arbitrary functions inside context makes the copy mechanism feel less like a toy and more like a real substrate. Source: `[50:56]` to `[55:44]`.
- The talk keeps implying a harsher truth: transformers want to do in-context learning because it is an efficient way to reduce entropy in the text stream. Source: `[33:05]` to `[33:34]`.
