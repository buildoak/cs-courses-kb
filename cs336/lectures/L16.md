Lecture 16 finishes RLHF, then pivots into verifiable rewards and the reasoning-model recipes that made GRPO look practical.

## Summary
- The lecture starts by finishing RLHF and DPO, but its real target is the new wave of verifiable-reward training that powers reasoning models. Source: `[0:04]` to `[5:12]`.
- The warning from the RLHF side is still overoptimization: better proxy reward does not guarantee better human judgment, and calibration can get worse. Source: `[6:23]` to `[9:04]`.

- R1, Kimi K1.5, and Qwen 3 show the same high-level recipe in different forms: long-CoT SFT, reasoning RL, then a normal post-training pass, plus tricks like format rewards, length control, and thinking-mode fusion. Source: `[42:49]` to `[1:19:23]`.

## Key Concepts
- **DPO / SimPO / length-normalized DPO:** the post-training family that still dominates the RLHF side of the field. Source: `[2:03]` to `[4:45]`.
- **Overoptimization:** proxy reward rises first, then starts diverging from real human preference. Source: `[6:23]` to `[7:39]`.
- **Calibration:** RLHF models tend to become more overconfident and less probabilistic. Source: `[8:01]` to `[8:58]`.
- **Verifiable rewards:** deterministic outcome rewards, usually correct / incorrect rather than human-rated. Source: `[13:03]` to `[13:44]`.
- **Policy gradient theorem:** the basic `reward * grad log p` update. Source: `[1:08:15]` to `[1:09:09]`.
- **Baseline / advantage:** subtract a state-dependent expectation to reduce variance. Source: `[1:09:20]` to `[1:09:53]`.
- **GRPO:** group-relative policy optimization, where the other rollouts for the same question form the baseline. Source: `[31:27]` to `[33:09]`.
- **Outcome reward vs process reward:** outcome rewards are easy to verify; process rewards are richer but much harder to build. Source: `[14:06]` to `[14:40]`, `[56:11]` to `[56:34]`.
- **R1 zero:** the controlled DeepSeek experiment that runs math RL on a base model with no prior instruction tuning. Source: `[45:49]` to `[49:07]`.
- **PRM:** process-reward models, which can help but are hard to source reliably. Source: `[56:11]` to `[56:47]`.
- **Thinking mode fusion:** Qwen 3's way of keeping both a thinking and a no-think mode in one parameter set. Source: `[1:16:31]` to `[1:18:17]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The interesting RL distinction is not robotics vs language, but reachability vs correctness. In language models, you can reach any token state; the hard part is making it truthy. Source: `[3:23]` to `[4:23]`.
- Longer chains of thought are not automatically proof of better reasoning. The lecture treats some of the R1 "aha" story as an objective artifact, not magic. Source: `[47:30]` to `[48:54]`.
- Outcome rewards can beat PRMs in practice because PRMs are harder to get right in the first place. More signal is not always more usable signal. Source: `[56:11]` to `[57:07]`.
- Small long-CoT SFT sets can produce surprisingly large gains because the base model already has latent thinking capability. Source: `[50:24]` to `[52:00]`.
- Thinking-mode fusion is really inference control. It gives one model a budget knob instead of forcing separate short- and long-answer models. Source: `[1:17:27]` to `[1:18:17]`.
- The infrastructure matters. RL training needs rollout workers, inference workers, and weight sync; the lecture treats that as part of the algorithm, not a side note. Source: `[1:09:06]` to `[1:10:48]`.
