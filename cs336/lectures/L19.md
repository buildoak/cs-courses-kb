# L19 Science and Scaling

Mike Lewis treats frontier pretraining as an experimental science. The point is not just "scale the model," but figure out which small experiments can actually predict what survives at frontier scale.

## Summary
- The talk starts with a blunt premise: pretraining is simple in definition and hard in practice because the real problem is extrapolation from small runs to main training runs. Source: `[00:02:44]` to `[00:04:57]`, CLSP abstract.
- He uses compute budget as the main axis of comparison and treats scaling laws as a way to predict model behavior before spending the full cluster budget. Source: `[00:18:38]` to `[00:23:34]`, `[00:31:02]` to `[00:34:53]`.
- Raw benchmark accuracy is too noisy for prediction, so the team leans on smoother soft metrics like likelihood, then maps those back to accuracy with calibration curves. Source: `[00:31:02]` to `[00:34:53]`.
- Llama 3 stayed close to the old Transformer recipe and won by better infrastructure, more compute, and better data, not by being creatively different for its own sake. Source: `[00:35:23]` to `[00:39:10]`, `[00:44:27]` to `[00:50:13]`.
- Data quality and evaluation coverage are the real control knobs. The lecture keeps returning to filtering, domain tradeoffs, and the gaps between training data and the tasks people later care about. Source: `[00:44:27]` to `[00:50:13]`, `[00:53:00]` to `[00:55:09]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Extrapolation from small scale:** the scientific question is whether a tiny experiment says anything useful about a giant run. Source: `[00:03:05]` to `[00:04:57]`.
- **Compute budget:** use FLOPs as the summary variable for model size and compare models at equal compute. Source: `[00:18:38]` to `[00:20:26]`.
- **Scaling laws:** fit trend lines on small runs, then extrapolate where the frontier model will land. Source: `[00:21:43]` to `[00:23:34]`.
- **Compute factor:** compare architectures by how much compute they save at equal performance. Source: `[00:36:11]` to `[00:36:50]`.
- **Likelihood vs accuracy:** likelihood is smooth enough to predict; accuracy is the target but not the prediction signal. Source: `[00:32:05]` to `[00:34:53]`.
- **Architecture discipline:** the lecture favors the simplest thing that works, including MoE only when the compute factor is worth the extra complexity. Source: `[00:35:23]` to `[00:38:39]`.
- **Data quality:** most of the bad web data is removed with heuristics, then higher-level definitions of "good data" are used to score the remaining corpus. Source: `[00:45:31]` to `[00:47:28]`.
- **Evaluation coverage:** the team tracks where benchmarks are missing coverage and tries not to overfit data selection to only one domain. Source: `[00:48:21]` to `[00:50:13]`.
- **Test-time scaling:** pretraining metrics do not fully capture reasoning-style test-time behavior, so the talk calls out pass@k-style proxies in the Q&A. Source: `[00:59:32]` to `[01:00:47]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The best predictive signal is often not the metric people care about. Accuracy is the product metric; likelihood is the engineering signal. Source: `[00:32:05]` to `[00:34:53]`.
- Bigger models are not always a new shape of progress. Often the win is just better compute efficiency plus better data. Source: `[00:39:05]` to `[00:39:14]`, `[00:50:18]` to `[00:52:27]`.
- Scaling laws are conditional. Change the data, architecture, or task mix, and the fit can stop meaning what you think it means. Source: `[00:57:13]` to `[00:58:26]`.
- Data tradeoffs are zero-sum. More code improves coding benchmarks, but it can pull performance away from knowledge-heavy tasks. Source: `[00:49:21]` to `[00:50:13]`.
- The lecture is not selling creativity. It is selling ruthless reduction of uncertainty before the expensive run. Source: `[00:53:20]` to `[00:54:54]`.
- Long-context complaints are partly an evaluation problem. The Q&A explicitly says better benchmarking is needed to know what a useful context model really is. Source: `[01:01:50]` to `[01:02:38]`.

## Connections
- To L18: both lectures treat evaluation as the hard part. L18 uses verifiers and agent scaffolds; L19 uses calibration, benchmark coverage, and data selection.
- To L09/L11: this is the industrial version of scaling-law thinking, but applied under real data, real compute, and real release pressure.
