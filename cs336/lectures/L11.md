# L11 Scaling Laws 2

Lecture 11 is the practical scaling-law lecture: what to do when you are actually scaling a model, where the textbook Chinchilla ratio starts to wobble and mu P is the escape hatch.

## Summary
- The lecture is a case-study tour: Cerebras-GPT, DeepSeek, MiniCPM, Llama 3, Hunyuan-Large, and MiniMax-01. Source: `[1:29]` to `[3:21]`, `[40:37]` to `[48:23]`.
- Chinchilla-style iso-FLOP analysis is still the reference method, but the optimal token-to-parameter ratio keeps drifting upward. Source: `[14:54]` to `[17:13]`, `[40:37]` to `[44:22]`.
- MiniCPM's WSD schedule makes Chinchilla-style sweeps practical by letting you reuse the stable middle of the run. Source: `[18:57]` to `[25:29]`.
- mu P makes learning-rate transfer across widths mostly stable, which is the whole point of the lecture's second half. Source: `[38:38]` to `[41:39]`, `[49:58]` to `[1:12:40]`.
- The end result is blunt: scaling in the wild is mostly batch size, learning rate, schedule design, and parameterization discipline. Source: `[47:46]` to `[1:18:05]`.

## Key Concepts
- **IsoFLOP analysis:** sweep model sizes at fixed FLOPs and read off the minimum. Source: `[54:41]` to `[58:12]`.
- **Chinchilla ratio:** the classic 20 tokens per parameter rule of thumb. Source: `[51:48]` to `[52:43]`.
- **Warmup-stable-decay (WSD):** a trapezoid LR schedule that makes data scaling reusable. Source: `[20:31]` to `[25:29]`.
- **Critical batch size:** the batch-size analog of the scaling-law fit. Source: `[14:54]` to `[17:13]`.
- **mu P / width mu P:** reparameterize initialization and learning rates so the optimal LR transfers across width. Source: `[40:05]` to `[41:39]`.
- **Hyperparameter transfer:** the actual win from mu P is that the best LR stops moving around. Source: `[1:11:47]` to `[1:12:40]`.
- **Ablation discipline:** the lecture spends a lot of time on where mu P survives and where it fails. Source: `[1:12:57]` to `[1:16:21]`.
- **Tokens-per-parameter drift:** modern runs tend to sit above the original Chinchilla ratio. Source: `[1:01:29]` to `[1:02:41]`, `[27:41]` to `[28:22]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The ratio itself is less stable than the method used to estimate it. The iso-FLOP machinery is the durable part. Source: `[27:29]` to `[28:30]`.
- WSD is basically a schedule hack that turns many expensive runs into something much closer to one reusable run. Source: `[20:10]` to `[22:25]`.
- The lecture is not saying Chinchilla was wrong. It is saying the 20:1 number is a starting point, not a law of nature. Source: `[27:29]` to `[28:22]`.
- mu P is not magic. It is a way to stop one of the nastiest tuning variables from moving when width changes. Source: `[40:05]` to `[41:39]`.
- Stronger weight decay and exotic optimizers are where the neat transfer story starts to break. Source: `[1:15:01]` to `[1:16:21]`.
