# L17 Alignment - RL 2

Lecture 17 is the mechanics lecture: state, action, reward, baseline, advantage, clipping, KL, and the ugly little systems details that make GRPO actually work.

## Summary
- The lecture starts from first principles: in language-model RL, the state is the prompt plus partial response, the action is the next token, and the reward is the quality of the whole response. Source: `[0:57]` to `[5:08]`.
- Sparse binary rewards make naive policy gradient stall, so the class leans hard on baselines and advantages to get variance under control. Source: `[9:00]` to `[28:19]`.
- GRPO's trick is to exploit the natural group structure of multiple rollouts for the same question, using the group as the baseline instead of a separate critic. Source: `[31:27]` to `[39:44]`.
- Partial credit and reward shaping can help, but they also create odd incentives, no-op batches, and misleading loss curves if you are not careful. Source: `[34:08]` to `[1:12:40]`.
- The final lesson is operational: RL systems are harder to scale than pre-training because you need rollout generation, reference models, current models, old models, and inference workers all at once. Source: `[55:02]` to `[1:15:47]`.

## Key Concepts
- **State / action / reward:** prompt-plus-response state, next-token action, whole-response reward. Source: `[1:13]` to `[1:28]`.
- **Outcome reward:** the reward arrives only at the end of the response. Source: `[1:31]` to `[1:45]`.
- **Sparse reward:** most responses get zero, so the gradient can vanish. Source: `[9:36]` to `[10:20]`.
- **Policy gradient theorem:** the `reward * grad log p` trick that turns RL into gradient ascent. Source: `[6:26]` to `[7:16]`.
- **Baseline:** any state-dependent subtraction that leaves the gradient valid. Source: `[19:16]` to `[20:08]`, `[25:33]` to `[26:10]`.
- **Advantage:** reward minus expected reward given the state. Source: `[26:30]` to `[28:19]`.
- **GRPO:** group-relative policy optimization, built around leave-one-out or group-average baselines. Source: `[31:27]` to `[39:44]`.
- **Partial credit reward:** reward shaping that gives intermediate signal instead of all-or-nothing correctness. Source: `[34:08]` to `[37:23]`.
- **Centered / normalized rewards:** GRPO variants that reduce the variance of the update. Source: `[44:09]` to `[47:17]`.
- **KL penalty:** keep the current policy close to a reference model. Source: `[56:17]` to `[58:09]`.
- **Clipping:** bound the update ratio relative to the old policy. Source: `[55:02]` to `[55:28]`.
- **Reference model vs old model:** the reference is the stable anchor; the old model is the cheap stored baseline for inner-loop updates. Source: `[1:03:05]` to `[1:04:52]`.
- **Loss vs reward:** the loss is not a stable monitoring signal because the response distribution changes every rollout. Source: `[1:12:11]` to `[1:13:20]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- In language-model RL, the hard part is not state reachability. You can generate any token sequence you want; the hard part is making the sequence correct and non-hackable. Source: `[3:23]` to `[4:23]`.
- Any state-dependent baseline is legal. GRPO just uses the most natural one in this setting: the other samples from the same question. Source: `[25:33]` to `[28:19]`, `[31:35]` to `[33:09]`.
- Loss curves can lie. As the policy changes, the sampled data changes, so reward is a better signal than a static loss number. Source: `[1:12:11]` to `[1:13:20]`.
- More reward shaping is not automatically better. If every sample gets the same shaped reward, the update can become a no-op. Source: `[1:11:39]` to `[1:12:06]`.
- The reference-model copy is the memory tax. The cheaper implementation trick is to cache log-probs instead of keeping a full old model around. Source: `[1:04:10]` to `[1:04:52]`.
- RL systems are multi-model systems. Once you have policy, reference, old policy, inference worker, and environment, training stops looking like plain pre-training. Source: `[1:15:04]` to `[1:15:47]`.
