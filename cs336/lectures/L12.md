# L12 Evaluation

Lecture 12 is the evaluation lecture: the score only matters if you know what question it answers, and most benchmarks are messier than they look.

## Summary
- Evaluation is not one thing. It changes with the question: purchase decision, capability probe, policy check, model debugging, or deployment gate. Source: `[0:07]` to `[8:03]`, `[7:23]` to `[8:03]`.
- The basic framework is inputs, prompting, outputs, and interpretation. Prompt choice, few-shot order, and the exact format can move the score. Source: `[8:03]` to `[15:29]`.
- Perplexity still matters because it is smooth and sensitive to distribution shift, but train-test contamination makes it easy to fool yourself. Source: `[17:18]` to `[24:24]`.
- MMLU is useful as a base-model probe, but MMLU-Pro and GPQA exist because the older benchmark saturates and is too easy to game. Source: `[32:36]` to `[44:16]`.
- Open-ended evals move to Chatbot Arena, IFEval, AlpacaEval, and WildBench, then spill into agents and safety where the target is no longer just answer quality. Source: `[52:24]` to `[1:17:14]`.

## Key Concepts
- **No one true eval:** the right benchmark depends on what you are trying to learn from the model. Source: `[6:01]` to `[8:03]`.
- **Input design:** prompts need to cover the real use case, including hard tails and multi-turn behavior. Source: `[8:11]` to `[9:11]`.
- **Prompt sensitivity:** few-shot, zero-shot, and chain-of-thought are not cosmetic choices; they change the measured result. Source: `[9:15]` to `[9:43]`, `[36:07]` to `[38:23]`.
- **Red teaming:** if you adapt the evaluation to the model, you gain realism but lose generality. Source: `[13:51]` to `[14:24]`.
- **Perplexity:** still useful for scaling and distributional smoothness, even if downstream task accuracy gets more attention. Source: `[23:39]` to `[24:24]`.
- **Train-test contamination:** overlap is real, and decontamination by n-gram matching is only a partial fix. Source: `[20:58]` to `[21:29]`.
- **HELM:** a framework for collating benchmarks and drilling down into individual predictions. Source: `[34:58]` to `[36:30]`.
- **MMLU / MMLU-Pro:** MMLU started as a base-model benchmark; MMLU-Pro raises difficulty and reduces saturation. Source: `[39:02]` to `[44:16]`.
- **GPQA:** PhD-level, Google-proof questions with expert validation. Source: `[42:18]` to `[44:16]`.
- **Instruction-following evals:** Chatbot Arena, IFEval, AlpacaEval, and WildBench all test different slices of open-ended behavior. Source: `[53:17]` to `[1:00:07]`.
- **Agent evals:** SWE-bench, CyBench, and MLEBench test tool use, code changes, and multi-step task completion. Source: `[1:00:17]` to `[1:03:50]`.
- **Safety evals:** HarmBench, AIR-Bench, and MedHELM turn vague safety claims into concrete test suites. Source: `[1:06:11]` to `[1:17:14]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The metric is often a proxy for the benchmark design. If the prompt is bad, the score is mostly measuring the prompt. Source: `[8:03]` to `[9:43]`.
- MMLU is closer to a base-model exam than an instruction-tuned product benchmark, which is why it still says something useful. Source: `[39:02]` to `[40:31]`.
- Chatbot Arena is dynamic and popular, which also makes it a target for gaming and PR. Goodhart shows up quickly. Source: `[54:29]` to `[55:31]`.
- Open-ended benchmarks tend to converge on the same judge models, so evaluation of evaluation can become circular. Source: `[58:42]` to `[1:00:07]`.
- Safety is not just refusal. Capability, propensity, hallucination, and dual-use all interact. Source: `[1:11:22]` to `[1:13:47]`.
- Real-world evaluation splits into asking and quizzing. Standardized exams mostly test quizzing, not usefulness. Source: `[1:14:05]` to `[1:15:55]`.
- Benchmark error matters. Some scores are inflated by label noise or bad splits, not model progress. Source: `[1:17:22]` to `[1:19:26]`.

## Connections
- To L09 and L11: the same instinct that makes scaling curves useful also makes benchmark design matter. The number only works if the setup is honest.
- To L13 and L14: train-test overlap, contamination, and data quality are downstream of how corpora are built.
- To the inference lecture: deployment cost and real usage shape what "good" means, especially for open-ended and agentic systems.
- To alignment: safety benchmarks already separate capability from propensity, which is the bridge into post-training.

## Referenced Papers
- **MMLU** - the canonical multiple-choice benchmark for broad knowledge. Source: `[32:36]` to `[40:31]`.
- **HELM** - benchmark aggregation and drill-down framework. Source: `[34:58]` to `[35:23]`.
- **MMLU-Pro** - harder successor with more choices and lower saturation. Source: `[40:46]` to `[42:15]`.
- **GPQA** - expert-written, Google-proof questions. Source: `[42:18]` to `[44:16]`.
- **The Leaderboard Illusion** - the paper the lecture cites for Chatbot Arena protocol problems. Source: `[55:12]` to `[55:31]`.
- **IFEval** - instruction-following benchmark with mechanically checkable constraints. Source: `[56:01]` to `[57:17]`.
- **AlpacaEval** - LLM-as-judge win-rate benchmark for open-ended responses. Source: `[57:59]` to `[59:28]`.
- **WildBench** - human-bot conversation benchmark with checklist judging. Source: `[59:32]` to `[1:00:07]`.
- **SWE-bench** - codebase + issue + PR benchmark for agents. Source: `[1:01:00]` to `[1:01:31]`.
- **CyBench** - cybersecurity benchmark built around command execution and server access. Source: `[1:01:31]` to `[1:03:00]`.
- **MLEBench** - Kaggle-style multi-step model-building benchmark. Source: `[1:03:04]` to `[1:03:50]`.
- **ARC AGI / AGI-2** - reasoning benchmark lineage focused on pattern completion. Source: `[1:04:37]` to `[1:05:50]`.
- **HarmBench** - harmful-behavior benchmark for safety refusal and compliance. Source: `[1:06:41]` to `[1:07:41]`.
- **AIR-Bench** - policy-anchored safety benchmark. Source: `[1:07:41]` to `[1:08:34]`.
- **MedHELM** - clinical-use benchmark suite built from clinician use cases. Source: `[1:16:02]` to `[1:17:14]`.
