# L15 Alignment - SFT/RLHF

Lecture 15 is the bridge from pre-training to post-training: first supervised fine-tuning, then preference data, then the DPO escape hatch.

## Summary
- The lecture opens with the field shift from GPT-3 style pre-training to ChatGPT-style assistants: the model has capabilities, but post-training is what makes it useful and safer. Source: `[0:05]` to `[5:54]`.
- The core RLHF loop is model outputs, pairwise comparisons, a reward model, and a policy update with a KL anchor to the SFT model. Source: `[46:41]` to `[1:07:53]`.
- Annotation quality is the bottleneck. The lecture uses InstructGPT guidelines, the Google Bard leak, and crowdworker studies to show how noisy preference data really is. Source: `[48:17]` to `[57:34]`.
- RLHF is not just a different loss. It creates generator-validator gaps and overoptimization curves, and it also pushes models away from calibration. Source: `[45:52]` to `[46:03]`, `[6:23]` to `[9:04]`.
- DPO is the cleanup move: remove the explicit reward model, turn preference optimization into a supervised objective, and keep the policy close to the reference model. Source: `[1:11:15]` to `[1:14:32]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Supervised fine-tuning (SFT):** the first post-training stage, usually on expert demonstrations. Source: `[4:45]` to `[5:02]`.
- **Pairwise preference data:** annotators compare two model outputs instead of writing the target output themselves. Source: `[46:41]` to `[47:28]`.
- **Reward model:** a scalar scorer trained from pairwise feedback. Source: `[47:12]` to `[47:28]`.
- **Helpful / truthful / harmless:** the InstructGPT annotation rubric. Source: `[48:17]` to `[49:04]`.
- **Generator-validator gap:** judging is often cheaper than generating, and sometimes higher quality than writing from scratch. Source: `[45:52]` to `[46:03]`.
- **Overoptimization:** optimizing the reward proxy too hard eventually diverges from real human preference. Source: `[6:23]` to `[7:39]`.
- **Calibration drift:** RLHF models often become more overconfident and less probabilistically calibrated. Source: `[8:01]` to `[8:58]`.
- **Bradley-Terry model:** the preference model used to turn pairwise comparisons into implied rewards. Source: `[1:06:47]` to `[1:07:31]`.
- **KL regularization:** RLHF keeps the updated policy close to the SFT policy. Source: `[1:06:02]` to `[1:06:35]`.
- **PPO:** the older RLHF workhorse, with reward model, value function, and clipping. Source: `[1:07:53]` to `[1:10:40]`.
- **DPO:** the simplified preference-optimization route that avoids the explicit PPO stack. Source: `[1:11:15]` to `[1:14:32]`.
- **SimPO / length-normalized DPO:** recent variants that normalize by response length and remove the reference-policy machinery. Source: `[3:21]` to `[4:45]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- The lecture's real warning is data quality, not algorithm choice. If annotation quality is weak, the whole preference-learning stack is noisy from the start. Source: `[50:36]` to `[54:58]`.
- Different annotators optimize different things. The lecture shows factuality getting less attention than formatting in crowdworker settings. Source: `[56:23]` to `[56:49]`.
- AI feedback is not just a cheaper substitute for humans; it changes the supervision distribution and can introduce self-preference effects. Source: `[56:55]` to `[1:03:38]`.
- DPO is attractive because it removes system complexity, not because it magically solves alignment. The simplification is the win. Source: `[1:11:15]` to `[1:14:32]`.
- Setting matters. The lecture explicitly shows that better SFT can erase the apparent PPO-vs-DPO gap in later experiments. Source: `[5:12]` to `[6:12]`.
