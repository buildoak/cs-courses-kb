# L14 Data 2

Lecture 14 turns data curation into algorithms: language ID, quality filtering, toxicity filtering, and deduplication are all the same problem wearing different clothes.

## Summary
- The lecture breaks data processing into reusable primitives: language identification, quality filtering, toxicity filtering, and deduplication. Source: `[23:48]` to `[24:25]`, `[30:20]` to `[37:01]`, `[37:01]` to `[1:13:47]`.
- fastText is the cheap workhorse for language ID and simple classifiers, and open web math shows how the same machinery can target a domain rather than a language. Source: `[25:40]` to `[30:14]`.
- Quality filtering increasingly comes from model-defined positives: GPT-3-style high-quality sources, Llama-style references, phi-1 educational value, and instruction-like data. Source: `[30:20]` to `[34:18]`.
- Deduplication splits into exact dedup with Bloom filters and near dedup with MinHash + LSH. Source: `[37:01]` to `[46:08]`, `[46:42]` to `[1:13:15]`.
- The lecture's practical conclusion is blunt: some repetition is useful, synthetic data complicates similarity, and the right dedup setting depends on whether you are cleaning pre-training or mid-training data. Source: `[1:14:03]` to `[1:16:43]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Language identification:** isolate text in the target language so compute goes where you want it. Source: `[23:53]` to `[25:28]`.
- **fastText:** a simple classifier toolkit with an off-the-shelf language ID model. Source: `[25:40]` to `[26:24]`.
- **Open web math:** treat math as a target distribution and filter for it explicitly. Source: `[28:57]` to `[30:14]`.
- **Quality filtering:** train a classifier on positive examples that represent the target style. Source: `[30:20]` to `[31:35]`.
- **GPT-3 / Llama style positives:** use high-quality sources or pages linked from Wikipedia as the positive class. Source: `[30:52]` to `[31:35]`.
- **phi-1:** use GPT-4 to define educational value, then distill a cheaper classifier. Source: `[31:39]` to `[34:18]`.
- **Toxicity filtering:** use labeled toxic-comment data to build fast classifiers for hate and NSFW filtering. Source: `[35:14]` to `[36:51]`.
- **Exact duplicates:** mirrored pages and copied license text create large exact-repeat regions on the web. Source: `[37:01]` to `[40:33]`.
- **Hash functions / MurmurHash:** the basic primitive that makes exact dedup scalable. Source: `[42:17]` to `[45:24]`.
- **Bloom filters:** exact dedup with very low false-positive rate and no item storage. Source: `[46:42]` to `[58:29]`.
- **Jaccard similarity:** the similarity measure used for near-duplicate detection. Source: `[58:53]` to `[1:00:31]`.
- **MinHash:** a hash construction whose collision probability equals Jaccard similarity. Source: `[1:00:31]` to `[1:04:37]`.
- **LSH:** banding and bucket tricks sharpen the MinHash collision curve around a threshold. Source: `[1:05:07]` to `[1:13:15]`.
- **Synthetic data:** paraphrase-sensitive and often worthy of repeated exposure, which changes the dedup problem. Source: `[1:13:56]` to `[1:16:43]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Language ID is not just tagging. It is compute allocation and coverage control in multilingual corpora. Source: `[24:01]` to `[25:28]`.
- Quality filters often invent the target distribution first, then look for more of it at scale. Source: `[30:52]` to `[34:18]`.
- Dedup is pairwise, so the algorithmic trick is always to compress pairwise comparison into hashable structure. Source: `[42:20]` to `[45:24]`.
- Bloom filters work for exact dedup because false positives can be made tiny without storing the items themselves. Source: `[46:42]` to `[58:29]`.
- MinHash is useful precisely because hash collisions become a feature, not a bug, when they track similarity. Source: `[1:00:31]` to `[1:04:37]`.
- LSH is the sharpening step. It turns a soft similarity signal into a hard threshold with controllable precision and recall. Source: `[1:05:07]` to `[1:13:15]`.
- High-quality data is not always a dedup candidate. In mid-training, repeated exposure to a good corpus can be the right move. Source: `[1:15:33]` to `[1:16:43]`.
