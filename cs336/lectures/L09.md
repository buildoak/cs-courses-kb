# L09 Scaling Laws 1

Lecture 9 starts from the old theory question and lands on the practical one: how to use small runs to choose big-model settings without burning the cluster.

## Summary
- Scaling laws are the empirical version of sample-complexity theory: the lecture starts with classical generalization bounds and then moves to log-log fits that predict model behavior from small runs. Source: `[3:38]` to `[5:18]`, `[12:00]` to `[15:29]`.
- Hestness-style plots give the now-familiar three regions: random guessing, a power-law region, and the irreducible-error floor. Source: `[8:44]` to `[9:17]`.
- The same scaling logic applies to architecture choices, not just data size. Transformers beat LSTMs by a constant factor, and GLU / MoE style designs keep showing up as the better bet. Source: `[27:26]` to `[30:18]`.
- Parameter counting is not neutral. Embeddings behave differently, so the cleanest fits often exclude them. Source: `[31:05]` to `[31:42]`.
- The lecture ends by connecting batch size, learning rate, and mu P to Chinchilla-style compute-optimal training. Source: `[38:38]` to `[41:39]`, `[50:19]` to `[59:30]`.

## Key Concepts
- **Data scaling:** test loss falls roughly linearly in log-log space as dataset size grows. Source: `[12:08]` to `[15:29]`.
- **Irreducible error:** the asymptote matters because the power-law region ends somewhere. Source: `[14:01]` to `[14:30]`.
- **Architecture scaling:** compare models across compute levels instead of training one huge model and hoping. Source: `[27:30]` to `[29:33]`.
- **Optimizer scaling:** SGD vs Adam also shows up as a constant-factor gap in the scaling plots. Source: `[29:34]` to `[29:53]`.
- **Aspect ratio:** width-to-depth has a basin of near-optimal values, not a single magical point. Source: `[30:09]` to `[30:58]`.
- **Parameter accounting:** embeddings are not the same as the rest of the network. Source: `[31:05]` to `[31:42]`.
- **Learning-rate transfer:** the lecture sets up mu P as a way to make optimal learning rates transfer across widths. Source: `[38:57]` to `[41:39]`.
- **Chinchilla tradeoff:** data and model size can be optimized jointly for a fixed FLOP budget. Source: `[50:28]` to `[59:30]`.

## Non-Obvious Insights
- Scaling laws are not AGI mysticism. They are a way to buy down search cost before the expensive run. Source: `[1:39]` to `[2:38]`.
- The useful part is often the constant factor, not the slope. A consistent gap between two model families matters just as much as a new curve fit. Source: `[27:44]` to `[29:18]`.
- You cannot treat every checkpoint as a valid substitute for a separate run when the learning-rate schedule changes with target data size. Source: `[18:57]` to `[20:28]`.
- The lecture treats parameterization as a design variable. That is the real bridge to mu P. Source: `[38:57]` to `[41:39]`.
- The product-era shift matters: once deployment cost becomes real, the optimal token-to-parameter ratio stops being a pure training question. Source: `[1:01:29]` to `[1:02:41]`.

## Referenced Papers
- **Vapnik / Cortes et al. (Bell Labs, 1993).** Early predictive modeling work that the lecture treats as a precursor to modern scaling laws. Source: `[5:25]` to `[6:19]`.
- **Hestness et al., 2017.** The early large-scale neural scaling-law paper the lecture points to as the cleanest empirical baseline. Source: `[8:23]` to `[8:49]`.
- **Kaplan et al.** The main reference for data, compute, and parameter scaling curves. Source: `[12:36]` to `[13:25]`, `[27:06]` to `[33:33]`.
- **Rosenfeld et al.** Joint scaling forms used for compute/data tradeoff discussions. Source: `[50:19]` to `[51:24]`.
- **Chinchilla / Hoffmann et al., 2022.** The compute-optimal training reference that shifted the token-to-parameter rule of thumb. Source: `[51:04]` to `[59:30]`.
